The Algebraic Degree of Network Games via Tropical Geometry: A Geometric Perspective on Datta’s Formula ∗

Hangkun Hu † , Jingyi Wang ‡ , and Minggang Wang §

Abstract. The algebraic degree of a network game measures the complexity of its totally mixed Nash equilib-ria. For sparse multilinear network games, Datta’s formula expresses this degree combinatorially in terms of a permanent, but the geometric origin of this formula has remained unclear. In this paper, we provide a tropical-geometric derivation of Datta’s formula by identifying totally mixed equilibria with stable intersection points of tropical hypersurfaces associated with the indifference equations. We show that the mixed cells arising from the multilinear Newton polytope structure induce the cycle-cover combinatorics of the polynomial graph, so that the permanent appears as a tropical intersection count. This interpretation yields several structural consequences. We prove that the algebraic degree is multiplicative over strongly connected components, and we establish a sharp con-trast between two basic multilayer coupling mechanisms: Cartesian-type couplings remain bounded through a transfer-matrix trace formula, whereas tensor-type couplings exhibit exponential growth governed by the permanent of a local gadget. These results show that algebraic degree provides a structural complexity invariant for network architecture. We further illustrate the theory on coupled cyclic games and networked energy-market models, and we support the theoretical predictions with numerical experiments based on homotopy continuation.

In this perspective, the number of isolated solutions in the complex algebraic torus—the algebraic degree of the game—provides a rigorous upper bound on the number of totally mixed Nash equilibria and serves as a structural measure of equilibrium complexity. More broadly, the computational difficulty of Nash equilibrium is well known [17, 8], which makes structural results for special classes of games particularly valuable. For network games, however, the equilibrium equations are far from arbitrary. They are sparse and multilinear, and their monomial structure reflects the underlying dependency graph. This suggests that the algebraic degree should admit a more refined geometric in-terpretation than one obtains from general-purpose equilibrium computation alone. On the algebraic side, Bernstein’s theorem and its polyhedral developments show that the number of isolated torus solutions of a sparse polynomial system is governed by Newton polytopes and mixed volumes [5, 11, 19, 7]. On the tropical side, stable intersections of tropical hypersurfaces provide a piecewise-linear shadow of this root-counting theory [15, 14]. These perspectives strongly suggest that the combinatorics of network dependence should have a geometric origin. The starting point of the present paper is that, despite this suggestion, an important con-ceptual gap remains. Datta’s degree formula for network-type game systems is fundamentally combinatorial: it is expressed through a permanent and admits a cycle-cover interpretation [9, 10]. By contrast, BKK theory and tropical intersection theory are geometric. What is missing is a precise mechanism explaining why the cycle-cover combinatorics of the polyno-mial graph should arise from the geometry of the equilibrium equations. The purpose of this paper is to provide that missing mechanism. Our main contribution is a tropical-geometric reinterpretation of the algebraic degree of network games. We show that Datta’s permanent formula is not merely a combinatorial counting device, but the shadow of a tropical stable-intersection count. More precisely, we analyze totally mixed equilibria through stable intersection points of tropical hypersurfaces associated with the indifference equations, and we show that the mixed cells arising from the multilinear Newton polytope structure give rise to the cycle-cover combinatorics of the polynomial graph. In this way, the permanent records the tropical intersection multiplicities forced by the geometry of the system. The geometric rigidity behind this correspondence comes from the special multilinear structure of network games. The Newton polytope of each indifference equation decomposes as a Minkowski sum of simplices supported on disjoint coordinate blocks, one for each neigh-boring player. This imposes strong constraints on full-dimensional mixed cells: generically, each equation contributes exactly one one-dimensional choice, and collectively these choices assemble into a permutation-type structure. The resulting combinatorics is reflected in disjoint cycle covers, thereby recovering Datta’s degree formula from tropical intersection theory. Beyond this conceptual reinterpretation, we derive several structural consequences for equilibrium complexity. First, we show that the algebraic degree is multiplicative over strongly connected components, reflecting the block structure of the interaction graph. Second, we an-alyze two basic coupling mechanisms in multilayer systems. These results show that algebraic degree behaves as a genuine complexity invariant of graph compo-sition, sharply distinguishing topologies that preserve equilibrium tractability from those that generate combinatorial explosion. This distinction also has an applied interpretation. In coupled cyclic architectures, stan-dard local couplings preserve low algebraic complexity, whereas cross-layer tensor couplings induce a rapid proliferation of equilibria. The present paper lies at the intersection of algebraic game theory, tropical geometry, and sparse polynomial systems. On the game-theoretic side, Datta’s work established the algebraic-combinatorial framework for generic Nash equilibrium computation [9, 10], and more recent work has extended algebraic-degree questions to generalized Nash equilibrium problems [18]. On the tropical side, recent years have seen increasing interaction between tropical methods and equilibrium-related problems, including product-mix auctions, tropi-cal complementarity problems associated with Nash equilibria, and tropical approaches to mechanism design [20, 2, 12]. Recent work on sparse tropical polynomial systems further illustrates the continued development of tropical algebra in directions adjacent to the present one [1]. Nevertheless, these developments do not address the specific question studied here: the tropical-geometric origin of Datta’s permanent formula for multilinear network games.

7. Illustrative Game Instances. To ground the algebraic results in game-theoretic con-texts, we introduce two specific instances of network games. These examples illustrate how topological changes directly impact the number of totally mixed equilibria. 

7.1. The Cyclic Matching Pennies Game. Consider a network of N players arranged in a directed cycle 1 → 2 → · · · → N → 1. Each player i has two strategies Si = {H, T } (Heads, Tails). The payoff structure is a generalized “Matching Pennies” game: 

• Player i wins if their strategy matches Player i − 1 (Payoff 1), and loses otherwise (Payoff −1). 

• Or, alternatively, Player i wins if they mismatch Player i − 1. Algebraically, let xi be the probability that Player i plays H. The indifference equation for Player i (assuming they want to match i − 1) takes the form: 

ui(H, x i−1) − ui(T, x i−1) = ( xi−1 − (1 − xi−1)) − ((1 − xi−1) − xi−1) = 4 xi−1 − 2 = 0 This implies xi−1 = 0 .5. The dependency propagates uniquely around the cycle: xi−1 deter-mines xi. Solving this system yields exactly one solution ( xi = 0 .5 for all i). This illustrates Theorem 5.2, where the algebraic degree of a simple cycle is D = 1. The system is rigid; information propagates linearly without branching. 

7.2. The Cross-Fire Inspection Game (Tensor Coupling). To visualize the complexity explosion in Theorem 6.2, consider a dual-layer “Attacker-Defender” game on a ring of N

sites. 

• Layer 1 (Attackers): Player iA chooses between {Attack, Wait }.

• Layer 2 (Defenders): Player iD chooses between {Defend, Sleep }.In a Standard Prism configuration, Attacker iA only cares about the Defender at the same site 

iD. The system decouples into local problems or simple waves. However, in a Cross-Coupled configuration (Theorem 6.2), we introduce an “interlocking fields of fire” mechanism: 

• Attacker iA’s payoff depends on the action of the next Defender ( i + 1) D.

• Defender iD’s payoff depends on the action of the next Attacker ( i + 1) A.This creates a topology equivalent to the tensor product CN ⊗ K2,2 (or similar crossover gadget). Unlike the cyclic matching pennies where xi−1 fixes xi, here the condition at site i

7.3. Case Study: Instability in Coupled Smart Microgrids. To illustrate the practical implications of Theorem 6.2 in energy systems, we consider a model of N interconnected smart microgrids arranged in a distribution ring. This system operates as a Cyber-Physical System (CPS) [16] with two distinct layers: 

• Layer 1 (Physical Layer - P): Represents the physical flow of electricity and battery storage status. Strategies: SP = {Charge , Discharge }.

• Layer 2 (Market Layer - M): Represents the algorithmic trading agents bidding in the local energy market. Strategies: SM = {High Bid , Low Bid }.

Scenario A: Decoupled Operation (Standard Prism). In a traditional setup, the market agent at node i sets prices primarily based on the physical state of its own node i. The coupling is vertical ( iP ↔ iM) and horizontal flow is minimal. According to Corollary 6.5, the algebraic degree is bounded ( D ≈ 4). This suggests a predictable system with a small number of stable operating points, making grid control straightforward.

The Physical Controller at node i decides to Charge/Discharge based on the price signal from the neighbor i + 1. 2. 9. Conclusion. In this paper, we have established a rigorous tropical geometric framework for analyzing the algebraic degree of network games. By reinterpreting Datta’s combinatorial theorem through the lens of stable intersections and mixed cells, we provided a geometric justification for the factorial normalization factors inherent in the structure matrix. Our analysis demonstrates that the algebraic degree is not merely a combinatorial invariant but a reflection of the underlying tropical geometry of the indifference equations. Beyond the theoretical foundation, we explored the impact of network topology on com-plexity. We showed that the algebraic degree behaves multiplicatively over strongly connected components, allowing for the decomposition of complex networks. Furthermore, our investi-gation into multilayer systems reveals a sharp contrast in complexity: while standard prism graphs maintain bounded algebraic degrees, cross-coupling topologies induce an exponential explosion in the number of isolated complex solutions. Taken together, (4.1), (5.1), and (6.2) show that algebraic degree is not merely a counting invariant, but a structured complexity quantity compatible with graph composition and transfer mechanisms. This framework thus offers a powerful tool for characterizing the intrinsic complexity of strategic interactions on networks.
