# Distribution-Free Equilibrium in Search Contests 

# Murat Erkurt ∗ Emre Ozdenoren †

# March 24, 2026 

Abstract 

We study contests in which players sequentially search for a high score at a cost per draw, with unlimited opportunities, no recall, and the best score wins a prize. In the unique symmetric equilibrium, the acceptance probability depends only on the number of players, the cost, and the prize, not on the distribution, and total expenditure equals the prize. These properties extend to multiple prizes and hierarchical team competition. Efficiency relative to a planner is determined by the hazard rate of the distribution. With a finite horizon, a selectivity effect can dominate the discouragement effect when search costs are low. 

Keywords: Contests, search, McCall search, rent dissipation, optimal stopping, tourna-ments. 

JEL Codes: C72, D82, D83. 

# 1 Introduction 

When AI laboratories search over model architectures, pharmaceutical firms screen drug candidates, or teams compete in innovation challenges, competitive effort takes the form of sequential search. Each attempt is costly and produces an outcome of uncertain quality, but the reward depends only on relative performance: a fixed prize goes to whoever produces the best result, regardless of its absolute level. This paper studies such environments by combining two classical frameworks. In the theory of optimal search [McCall, 1970, Stigler, 1961], a single agent draws offers from a distribution and must decide when to stop. Our model is simple. N risk-neutral players independently draw values from a continuous distribution F at a cost c > 0 per draw. There is no recall: a player must accept or reject each draw immediately. The player with the highest accepted value wins a fixed prize W > 0; all others receive nothing. Each player’s problem resembles McCall’s job search model, except that the value of stopping depends on the behavior of competitors. Despite its simplicity, the model delivers a rich set of results. In the unique symmetric equilibrium, each player uses a threshold strategy, accepting a draw if and only if it exceeds a cutoff λ determined by 1 − F (λ) = N c/W . The threshold 

λ = F −1(1 − N c/W ) depends on F , but the acceptance probability 1 − F (λ) = N c/W 

does not: it is pinned down by the number of players, the search cost, and the prize alone. The expected number of draws, the expected search cost, and all payoff-relevant quantities are therefore distribution-free. The mechanism behind this result is the separation between 

cardinal scores (drawn from F ) and ordinal rankings (which determine the winner): because the prize depends only on who has the highest value, not on what that value is, the probability integral transform maps any continuous F into the same ordinal competition. This separation holds in any finite- or infinite-horizon version of the model. In a standard McCall problem, a searcher keeps the cardinal value of her accepted draw, so the distribution shapes the option value and determines the reservation wage. In our contest, the reward is a fixed prize for winning a rank-order competition, so the distribution washes out. With an infinite horizon, a further consequence is that competition drives the continuation value to zero, yielding full rent dissipation. An immediate consequence of the zero-profit condition is full rent dissipation: each player’s expected payoff is exactly zero, and total search expenditure across all players equals the prize W . This holds for any F , any N ≥ 2, and any cost-prize ratio satisfying N c < W .The result parallels the classic rent dissipation in Tullock contests and all-pay auctions, but arises through an entirely different mechanism: sequential search rather than simultaneous effort. The distribution-free property extends to multiple prizes. With rank-order prizes W1 ≥· · · ≥ WN , the equilibrium acceptance probability becomes c/ ( ¯W − WN ), where ¯W is the average prize: search incentives are driven by the gap between the average prize and the consolation prize. It also extends to hierarchical competition. In many applications, search competition has a team structure: pharmaceutical firms employ research teams that screen compounds, 2AI labs employ engineers who try architectures, and universities field departments whose members compete for grants. We embed the individual contest within such a hierarchy: M

designers each employ N workers, funding an internal prize to incentivize search within their team. The designer whose team produces the highest value wins a meta-prize Ω. The designer competition is also distribution-free, with partial rent dissipation at rate N (M −1) /(N M −1). To study efficiency, we compare the competitive equilibrium with a planner who maxi-mizes expected output quality minus search costs. The planner’s optimum is distribution-dependent, so the comparison turns on the shape of F . We characterize the efficient prize 

W ∗ = N c/ (1 − F (b∗)): the unique prize level that makes competition exactly efficient. A prize above W ∗ produces oversearch; a prize below W ∗ produces undersearch. We show that 

W ∗ is a weighted average of N/h over the planner’s search region, where h is the hazard rate of F . If one distribution has a pointwise higher hazard rate than another, its efficient prize is lower. Finally, we examine how restricting the number of available draws affects the equilibrium. With an infinite horizon, adding competitors reduces individual search: the acceptance prob-ability N c/W increases with N , so each player accepts sooner and draws fewer times, even though total expenditure remains at W . With a finite number of draws, however, this dis-couragement effect can reverse. When search costs are low, more competitors makes players 

more selective in early rounds, because only high draws can win and the fallback option of drawing again is cheap. We prove this selectivity effect analytically for the two-draw contest with free search, and show numerically that for positive costs the threshold is single-peaked in N , with the peak shifting toward smaller N as either the cost or the number of avail-able draws increases, converging to the infinite-horizon result where discouragement always dominates. The paper is organized as follows. Section 2 presents the model. Section 3 derives the equilibrium and its properties. Section 4 analyzes the finite-horizon case. Section 5 introduces hierarchical competition among team designers. Section 6 studies welfare and the optimal prize. Section 7 discusses related literature. Section 8 concludes. 

# 2 Model 

There are N ≥ 2 risk-neutral players. Time is discrete, t = 1 , 2, 3, . . . Each period, a player who has not yet stopped may pay cost c > 0 to draw a value x independently from a distribution F with continuous, strictly positive density f on the support [0 , K ], where 

K ≤ ∞ .1

3After observing x, the player either accepts (stops permanently with value x) or rejects 

(the draw is discarded, and the player proceeds to the next period). There is no recall: rejected values cannot be recovered. Players do not observe each other’s draws or stopping decisions. After all players have stopped, the player with the highest accepted value wins the prize W > 0. All others receive 0. Ties, which occur with probability zero, are broken uniformly at random. A (stationary) strategy for a player is a threshold λ ∈ [0 , K ]: accept a draw if and only if x ≥ λ. We look for symmetric Nash equilibria in threshold strategies. We assume N c < W . The boundary cases N c = W and N c > W are discussed in Remark 2 below.

Remark 1 (Asymmetric equilibria for N ≥ 3) . For N ≥ 3, asymmetric equilibria exist in which one player uses a high threshold λH and earns positive rents, while the remaining

A low-threshold player who draws a value in [λL, λ H ) accepts it even though she cannot win: since her continuation value is zero, stopping with a losing value (payoff zero) is no worse than continuing to search. She cannot improve by raising her threshold, because her expected payoff is zero at every threshold in [λL, λ H ]. Two conditions pin down the thresholds: the high-threshold player’s optimality determines λH as a best response to N − 1 low-threshold opponents, and the low-threshold player’s zero-profit condition determines λL such that her expected prize from draws above λH

exactly covers her search costs given the high-threshold player’s strategy. The reason such equilibria require N ≥ 3 is that the high-threshold and low-threshold players face different opponent mixes. The high-threshold player faces N − 1 low-threshold opponents, all of whom sometimes hold values below λH ; she therefore has a positive winning probability at λH , which sustains her rents. Each low-threshold player, by contrast, faces the high-threshold player—whose value always exceeds λH —plus N − 2 other low-threshold players, making her winning probability lower. With N = 2 , each player faces exactly one opponent, so there is no scope for different opponent mixes, and Proposition 1 shows the unique equilibrium is symmetric. # 3.3 Distribution-Free Property 

Proposition 2. In the unique equilibrium, the following quantities depend only on N , c,and W , and are independent of the distribution F :1. The acceptance probability per draw: 1 − F (λ) = N c/W .

62. The expected number of draws per player: E[draws ] = W/ (N c ).3. The expected search cost per player: E[cost ] = W/N .4. The dissipation ratio (expected total search cost over prize): N · E[cost ]/W = 1 .5. Each player’s expected payoff: V = 0 .

The distribution-free property arises because the prize depends on ordinal rank, not on the cardinal value of the accepted draw. The probability integral transform converts any continuous F into the same uniform competition over ranks, so all payoff-relevant quantities depend on F only through the acceptance probability 1 −F (λ). In a non-competitive McCall search problem, by contrast, the searcher keeps the cardinal value of her draw, so the reser-vation value equates the cost c to the option value R Kλ (x − λ) dF (x), which depends on the shape of F . In the infinite-horizon contest, competition additionally drives V to zero, which pins down the acceptance probability through the zero-profit condition (3) and delivers full rent dissipation. Part (iv) states that the entire prize is dissipated through search costs. This outcome is reminiscent of the classic Tullock contest [Tullock, 1980], where identical risk-neutral players dissipate the entire rent through effort. However, the mechanism is different. In a Tullock contest, each player chooses a single effort level and the probability of winning is a deterministic function of efforts. Here, no player chooses a total expenditure; each chooses a threshold, and the total cost W/N per player is a consequence of the equilibrium threshold, not a direct choice. The distribution-free property is what makes dissipation exact: because 1 − F (λ) = N c/W regardless of F , the expected cost per player c/ (1 − F (λ)) = W/N is pinned down by the zero-profit condition, not by the shape of the distribution.

# 4 Finite Horizon 

In the infinite-horizon model, the equilibrium threshold is decreasing in N for all c > 0: more competitors means less search. This discouragement effect is driven by full rent dissi-pation, which pins the continuation value to zero. With a finite number of draws, players retain a positive continuation value—they still have valuable remaining attempts—and a countervailing force emerges. More competitors means only high draws can win, so a player who can cheaply try again should be pickier about what she accepts early on. We call this the selectivity effect . # 7 Related Literature 

Our paper connects two large literatures. On the search side, McCall [1970] and Mortensen [1986] study sequential search by a single agent. On the contest side, Lazear and Rosen [1981], Tullock [1980], and Moldovanu and Sela [2001] study one-shot effort provision in competitions. Moldovanu and Sela [2001] show that a winner-take-all prize structure max-imizes total effort when costs are linear or concave; multiple prizes become optimal only with convex costs. In our model, players face a constant per-draw cost, and the multiple-prizes extension (Proposition 3) shows that rank-order rewards preserve the distribution-free property regardless of prize structure. The closest theoretical paper is Taylor [1995], who studies a research tournament in which firms sequentially draw innovations from a general distribution F at a fixed cost per 16 draw and the best innovation wins a prize. He establishes a unique symmetric equilibrium in threshold strategies, shows that more contestants reduce per-firm research effort (the discouragement effect), and analyzes optimal tournament design. His model features recall (firms keep their best draw), a finite horizon of T periods, and free entry with an entry fee. With free entry, N F E = P/C firms enter and each researches exactly once, yielding rent dissipation through entry rather than through search intensity. Our model differs in three respects: no recall, an infinite horizon, and a fixed number of contestants. These differences produce the distribution-free equilibrium and full rent dissipation with fixed N , neither of which appear in Taylor’s analysis, where the equilibrium threshold depends on both F and the time horizon. The distribution-free structure also enables extensions that would be intractable in Taylor’s framework: multiple prizes (Section 3.5), hierarchical competition (Section 5), and a sharp efficiency characterization through the hazard rate (Section 6). Seel and Strack [2013] analyze a contest in which players privately observe Brownian motions and choose when to stop; the player with the highest stopped value wins. Their equilibrium involves mixed stopping strategies, and in the martingale case the equilibrium distribution is independent of volatility. Our model differs in that draws are i.i.d. (not path-dependent), costs are explicit, and the equilibrium is in pure threshold strategies. Our distribution-free property is also stronger: the equilibrium acceptance probability is inde-pendent of the entire draw distribution F , not just of a single parameter. Whitmeyer [2017] studies a discrete-time model with finitely many rounds, i.i.d. uniform draws, no search cost, and no recall. His equilibrium depends on the number of rounds and the uniform distribution assumption. Our model introduces explicit search costs, allows a general distribution F , and has an infinite horizon, which together produce the distribution-free result and full rent dissipation. Our full rent dissipation result connects to the all-pay auction literature and the patent race literature. Baye, Kovenock, and de Vries [1996] show that in all-pay auctions with complete information, total expenditure dissipates the entire rent in the symmetric case. Loury [1979] shows that in patent races with free entry, expected R&D expenditure equals the patent value. Our model provides a sequential-search microfoundation for rent dissipation that requires neither a specific auction format nor free entry: with any fixed N ≥ 2 and any distribution F , total search cost equals W .A related literature studies strategic experimentation, where agents face multi-armed bandits and learn from each other’s outcomes [Bolton and Harris, 1999, Keller, Rady, and Cripps, 2005]. The key difference is that in those models the interaction is informational (ex-perimentation is a public good, leading to free-riding and under-experimentation), whereas in ours the interaction is purely competitive (search is private, leading to rent dissipation). 17 Akcigit and Liu [2016] study a research competition in which firms privately discover dead ends; the key friction is information, not search costs, and efficiency requires disclosure mech-anisms. Our agents draw from a known distribution, so there is no learning and no state variable, which is what generates the distribution-free result. A literature on optimal contest design studies whether limiting entry is beneficial. Fuller-ton and McAfee [1999] show that with heterogeneous contestants, the optimal number of competitors is often two, because additional entrants dilute incentives. Che and Gale [2003] reach a similar conclusion in a procurement setting: restricting entry to two firms is optimal when contestants are symmetric. In our model the number of contestants is fixed, but the welfare analysis raises a natural design question: a principal choosing both W and N faces a trade-off between order-statistic gains and search costs that depends on the distribution F .Terwiesch and Xu [2008] decompose the effect of adding contestants into a negative in-centive effect (discouragement) and a positive parallel-path effect (more diverse solutions). Boudreau, Lacetera, and Lakhani [2011] provide empirical evidence for this tradeoff using data from TopCoder programming contests: more competitors reduce individual effort, but for problems with high uncertainty the parallel-path effect dominates and the best solution improves. Our finite-horizon analysis (Section 4) provides a theoretical counterpart: selectiv-ity dominates discouragement when search costs are low and the number of available draws is limited. 

# 8 Conclusion 

We have studied a simple and natural model at the intersection of search theory and contest theory. Players sequentially search for a high score at a per-draw cost, and the highest score wins a prize. Despite the model’s simplicity, it yields sharp and surprising results: a distribution-free equilibrium, full rent dissipation, and a nuanced efficiency characterization driven by the hazard rate of the draw distribution. The distribution-free property arises because competition drives continuation values to zero, replacing the distribution-dependent option value of McCall search with a distribution-free zero-profit condition. This mechanism operates at every level of a competitive hierarchy: the meta-contest between team managers is also distribution-free, with partial rent dissipa-tion reflecting team diversification. Only the planner, facing no competitive pressure, retains the full distribution-dependent structure of the underlying search problem. The efficient prize W ∗ = N c/ (1 − F (b∗)) provides a concrete tool for contest design: it is the unique prize level that aligns competitive incentives with the social optimum. Since 

W ∗ is a weighted average of N/h over the search region, distributions with pointwise higher 18 hazard rates have lower efficient prizes. IFR distributions have small W ∗ and competition tends to oversearch; DFR distributions have large W ∗ and competition tends to undersearch; the exponential is the knife-edge. This finding has implications for the design of innovation contests: in domains where breakthrough innovations are rare but transformative (fat tails), prizes must be substantially inflated to induce efficient search. Several directions for further research suggest themselves. As shown in Section 4, the discouragement effect reverses with a finite horizon and low search costs; extending this anal-ysis to general distributions and characterizing the critical cost c∗(k, F ) is an open problem. If players differ in search costs, the symmetric equilibrium breaks down and low-cost players earn positive rents, connecting to the literature on asymmetric contests. Third, if players observe each other’s draws or stopping decisions, strategic information revelation interacts with search incentives in a way that connects to the feedback-in-contests literature [Ederer, 2010, Aoyagi, 2010]. Finally, a principal choosing both the prize level W and the number of contestants N faces the trade-off studied by Fullerton and McAfee [1999] and Che and Gale [2003]: more contestants improve the best outcome through order statistics but increase total search costs. The optimal N depends on F and remains an open question.
