Abstract 

Scalable Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (MARL) is fundamentally challenged by the curse of dimensionality. A common solution is to exploit locality, which hinges on an Exponential Decay Property (EDP) of the value function. However, existing conditions that guarantee the EDP are often conservative, as they are based on worst-case, environment-only bounds (e.g., supremums over actions) and fail to capture the regularizing effect of the policy itself. In this work, we establish that locality can also be a policy-dependent phenomenon. Our central contribution is a novel decomposition of the policy-induced interdependence matrix, Hπ , which decouples the environment’s sensitivity to state ( Es) and action ( Ea) from the policy’s sensitivity to state ( Π( π)). This decomposition reveals that locality can be induced by a smooth policy (small Π( π)) even when the environment is strongly action-coupled, exposing a fundamental locality-optimality tradeoff. We use this framework to derive a general spectral condition ρ(Es +EaΠ( π)) < 1 for exponential decay, which is strictly tighter than prior norm-based conditions. Finally, we leverage this theory to analyze a provably-sound localized block-coordinate policy improvement framework with guarantees tied directly to this spectral radius. 

Keywords: Multi-agent Reinforcement Learning 

1. Introduction 

Multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) is a powerful paradigm for controlling complex, decen-tralized systems. However, its application is fundamentally challenged by the curse of dimensionality: the joint state and action spaces ( S = Q

This problem places MARL in the category of “succinctly described” but intractable MDPs (Blondel and Tsitsiklis, 2000; Papadimitriou and Tsitsiklis, 1999), rendering standard, centralized learning algorithms computationally infeasible. A highly effective approach to overcome this barrier is to exploit the local interaction structure inherent in many networked systems. This line of work, often termed Networked MARL (Qu et al., 2020a, 2019; Lin et al., 2020), assumes agents are situated on a graph and that their local transitions and rewards depend only on a local neighborhood. These foundational papers show that such systems often exhibit an Exponential Decay Property (EDP), where the influence of distant agents on an agent’s value function decays exponentially with graph distance. This property is the theoretical bedrock that justifies truncating computations to a small κ-hop neighborhood, enabling scalable algorithms whose complexity depends on the neighborhood size, not the full network.

A critical question, however, is when this EDP holds. In the discounted setting, one gets EDP for “free” due to a γ < 1 multiplicative factor. In the more challenging average-reward ( γ = 1 ) setting, prior work has established the EDP by proving a Dobrushin-style weak coupling condition (Qu et al., 2020a). This condition, however, is policy-independent - it relies on a worst-case (action-supremum) bound on the environment’s influence. This bound is inherently conservative. It can fail to certify locality in a system where the environment is strongly action-coupled, even if the agents’ policies are “smooth” (e.g., highly exploratory) and effectively break this strong coupling. This paper’s central thesis is that locality is best viewed depending on both the environment and the policy. Our main contribution is a new theoretical framework that formally decouples the sources of influence. We introduce a novel decomposition of the one-step interdependence matrix, Cπ, that separates the environment’s “physics” from the policy’s “logic”: 

Cπ ⪯ Es + EaΠ( π)

Here, Es and Ea measure the environment’s sensitivity to changes in state and action, respectively, while Π( π) measures the policy’s sensitivity to changes in state (Proposition 1). This decomposition reveals that a smooth policy (a small Π( π)) can induce locality even in an environment with strong action-based coupling (a large Ea). This framework allows us to derive a new, more general condition for locality: the system exhibits exponential decay if the spectral radius ρ(Hπ) < 1 (Theorem 3). This is strictly tighter than prior norm-based, policy-independent tests. We connect this theory to practice by showing that for standard entropy-regularized policies (Geist et al., 2019), the temperature τ serves as a direct, practical knob to control Π( π) and thus manage the system’s locality (Lemma 10). This exposes a fundamental locality-optimality tradeoff. Finally, we leverage this theory to analyze a deterministic, oracle-based policy improvement framework (Section 5). By decoupling the analysis of spatial approximation from the complex analysis of stochastic sampling, we provide a clean, provably-sound justification for localized algorithms. We prove that the error of our localized framework is bounded deterministically by the tail of a Neumann series governed by ρ(Hπ), completing the link from our fundamental theory to a provable algorithmic guarantee. We decsribe additional related work in Appendix A.

The matrix Cπ = [ Cπj←i] is the policy–induced interdependence matrix. It governs how one step of the kernel maps oscillations of functions. This policy-dependent definition is a critical departure from prior approaches. A common alternative for measuring influence is to define a single, policy-independent matrix that captures the worst-case coupling of the environment. This is typically done by taking a supremum over all joint actions, rather than an expectation (or “mixture”) over the policy π(· | s). While this action-supremum approach has the advantage of being a fixed property of the environment, it is fundamentally conservative. It conflates the “physics” of the environment with the “logic” of the policy. Consider an environment where the next state Pj is extremely sensitive to a neighbor’s action ak.An action-supremum bound would conclude the system is tightly coupled (i.e., not local), even if the agent’s policy πk is “dumb” and never chooses that action, or is completely insensitive to the state

4. The Algorithmic Link: Controlling Interdependence

5. A Provable Framework for Localized Policy Improvement 

We now integrate the results developed so far into a complete framework for localized policy improvement . The starting point is conceptual rather than algorithmic. Most analyses of multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL)—especially stochastic ones such as actor–critic—intertwine spatial truncation error (from limiting attention to local neighborhoods) with stochastic approximation error (from finite samples and noisy updates). This coupling makes it difficult to tell whether a poor bound arises from the algorithm or from the structure of the environment. We therefore take a step back and study an oracle model in which all expectations under π (i.e., rπ, Hπ) are known exactly. This idealization isolates the purely structural aspect of locality. Within this setting, we can quantify precisely how the truncation radius κ, the decay rate λ, and the policy smoothness (via τ ) interact. For each policy π, we associate a directed support graph Gπ on vertices [n] by drawing an edge i → j whenever Hπj←i > 0. This graph captures which coordinates can directly influence each other in one step of the policy–environment dynamics. When Hπ is sparse—e.g., due to limited observation scopes or weak couplings—this graph has small degree, and a κ-hop neighborhood covers only a small subset of agents. All our algorithms will exploit this structure to compute local approximations by message passing on Gπ.

Appendix A. Related work 

Our work addresses the curse of dimensionality in multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL). The challenge is that the global state and action spaces ( S = Q 

> i

Si, A = Q 

> i

Ai) grow exponentially with the number of agents, n. This problem falls into the category of “succinctly described” MDPs (Blondel and Tsitsiklis, 2000), which are known to be computationally intractable in the general case, even with structural assumptions (Papadimitriou and Tsitsiklis, 1999). To achieve scalability, researchers have explored various structural assumptions. One common approach in general MARL is to use function approximation (Zhang et al., 2018; Lowe et al., 2017) or assume independent learners (Tan, 1993), though the latter can suffer from non-stationarity (Matignon et al., 2012). Other related areas include Factored MDPs (Kearns and Koller, 1999; Guestrin et al., 2003), which assume local states but typically a global action, and Weakly Coupled MDPs (Meuleau et al., 1998), which assume agents’ transitions are independent and coupling only occurs through the reward. Our work differs from these by focusing on systems with local, coupled transitions, which is common in networked systems. This paper is most related to the line of work on scalable Networked MARL (Qu et al., 2020a, 2019; Lin et al., 2021a). These foundational papers were the first to show that if the system’s local transitions and rewards depend only on a local graph neighborhood, the system exhibits an Exponential Decay Property (EDP). This property, where influence decays exponentially with graph distance, justifies κ-hop truncation and enables scalable algorithms. A critical distinction lies in how this EDP is proven. In the challenging average-reward ( γ = 1 )setting, these works establish the EDP by proving a Dobrushin-style weak coupling condition*(Qu et al., 2020a). This condition is policy-independent, it relies on a worst-case (action-supremum) bound on the environment’s influence, requiring ∥C∥∞ < 1.This paper builds directly on that foundation, but identifies that a policy-independent bound is inherently conservative. It can fail to certify locality in systems where the environment’s action-based coupling ( Ea) is strong, even if the policy itself is smooth and breaks this coupling. Our central contribution is a more general and fundamental characterization of locality.
