Abstract 

A celebrated consequence of the minimax theorem is that two-player zero-sum games ad-mit a tractable equilibrium characterization. In many central applications, however, each side comprises multiple independent agents who share a common objective but cannot perfectly coordinate their actions. Such settings can be modeled as team zero-sum games , a natural gen-eralization of both two-player zero-sum games and potential gamesвҖ”the two most well-studied classes of games in algorithmic game theory. In this paper, we settle the complexity of team zero-sum games by establishing that com-puting Nash equilibria is PPAD -complete. As a result, despite the global adversarial structure, team zero-sum games are as hard as general-sum games. Our hardness result holds even when i) the precision is inverse polynomial, thereby ruling out a fully polynomial-time approximation scheme (unless P = PPAD ); ii) each team consists of only two players; and iii) the underlying class of games is polymatrix. As a byproduct, we resolve the complexity of group-wise zero-sum polymatrix games, a class introduced and examined in the seminal work of Cai and Daskalakis (SODA вҖҷ11), and more recently highlighted by Hollender, Maystre, and Nagarajan (ICLR вҖҷ25). Moreover, we show that computing a first-order stationary point in min-max optimization is 

PPAD -complete even for quadratic (multilinear) objectives. From a technical standpoint, we develop a series of team zero-sum game gadgets that allow us to simulate the breakthrough reduction of Bernasconi and Castiglioni (STOC вҖҷ26). Moreover, to obtain hardness results for quadratic objectives, we make use of a general technique based on linear local approximation, which is of independent interest.

# 1 Introduction 

Two-player zero-sum games constitute one of the central models of strategic interaction, going back to the foundational work of von Neumann [1928] and the inception of game theory. The minimax theorem endows two-player zero-sum games with a well-defined value and an unambiguous prescrip-tion of optimal play. Moreover, a minimax equilibriumвҖ”equivalently, a Nash equilibrium [Nash, 1951]вҖ”can be computed in polynomial time via linear programming. This tractability has placed two-player zero-sum games at the forefront of operations research, optimization, and machine learn-ing for many decades. However, many strategic interactions involve multiple agents who share the same objective and compete against an opposing team of agents. Canonical examples include competition between firms [Coase, 1937, Marschak, 1955], the weak selection model in evolutionary biology [Chastain, Livnat, Papadimitriou, and Vazirani, 2014, Nowak, Sasaki, Taylor, and Fudenberg, 2004], adver-sarial machine learning with multiple classifiers or attackers [Durugkar, Gemp, and Mahadevan, 2017], and multi-agent reinforcement learning [Tampuu, Matiisen, Kodelja, Kuzovkin, Korjus, Aru, Aru, and Vicente, 2015, Guan, Afshari, and Tsiotras, 2023, Kalogiannis, Anagnostides, Panageas, Vlatakis-Gkaragkounis, Chatziafratis, and Stavroulakis, 2023a, Hu, Zhu, Li, and Zhao, 2024, Zeng, Zhang, Yang, Zhang, and Zhang, 2024, Kalogiannis, Yan, and Panageas, 2024]. These settings can be naturally modeled as team zero-sum games [Schulman and Vazirani, 2019]. The key distinction from two-player zero-sum games is that each team comprises several independent decision makers who may be unable to perfectly coordinate their actions. This reflects a practical constraint at the heart of group decision making: communication between the members of the team can be expen-sive or even infeasible [Coase, 1937, Marschak, 1955]; the popular card game bridge furnishes one such illustrative example. In fact, team zero-sum games generalize both two-player zero-sum games and potential games [Monderer and Shapley, 1996], the two most well-studied classes of games in algorithmic game theory. In this paper, we examine the complexity of computing a Nash equilibriumвҖ”the standard solution concept in noncooperative gamesвҖ”in team zero-sum games. A celebrated series of works established that computing a Nash equilibrium even in two-player general-sum games is PPAD -complete [Daskalakis, Goldberg, and Papadimitriou, 2009, Chen, Deng, and Teng, 2009]. However, those results do not apply to team zero-sum games since they are significantly more structured: there are only two diametrically opposing objectives, one for each team. That is, unlike general-sum games, team zero-sum games maintain a global adversarial structure. From a technical standpoint, this fundamentally precludes the use of existing techniques and reductions developed for general-sum games, which explains why the complexity of computing Nash equilibria in team zero-sum games has eluded prior research. A central question is to understand whether the tractability of two-player zero-sum games extends in the multiagent setting, or whether it is subject to the complexity barriers circumscribing general equilibrium computation. A team zero-sum game can be viewed as a min-max optimization problem over a product of probability simplices. The key challenge is that once a team contains multiple players, the objective of the team becomes nonconvex/nonconcave in terms of their joint strategy. In this context, a recent breakthrough result by Bernasconi and Castiglioni [2026] established that computing a 

first-order stationary point вҖ”the counterpart of Nash equilibrium beyond finite gamesвҖ”in min-max optimization is PPAD -hard even when the precision is inverse polynomial in the dimension. However, team zero-sum games are significantly more structured than general min-max optimization problems in terms of both the underlying objective and the constraint sets. This leaves open the tantalizing question of characterizing the complexity of team zero-sum games. The complexity landscape of computing Nash equilibria is by now well-understood in the special case where one of the two teams contains a single player. Anagnostides, Kalogiannis, Panageas, Vlatakis-Gkaragkounis, and McAleer [2023] established membership in CLS , thereby implying CLS -completeness by virtue of the hardness result of Babichenko and Rubinstein [2021] concerning multi-player identical-interest games. More recently, Anagnostides, Panageas, Sandholm, and Yan [2025] established CLS -completeness even when a team of two players is competing against a single adversarial player. However, the more general setting beyond a single adversarial player remained open. 

1.1 Our results 

Our main contribution is to close this gap, establishing that team zero-sum games are as hard as general-sum games [Daskalakis, Goldberg, and Papadimitriou, 2009, Chen, Deng, and Teng, 2009]. 

Theorem 1.1. Computing an Пө-approximate Nash equilibrium in 2 vs. 2 team zero-sum games is 

PPAD -complete even when Пө is inversely polynomial in the size of the input. 

As a result, we show that the complexity of team zero-sum games collapses to that of 2 vs. 2 team zero-sum games. Together with prior results, we thus paint a complete picture of the complexity landscape in team zero-sum games. Theorem 1.1 also rules out the existence of a fully polynomial-time approximation scheme (unless P = PPAD ). Taking a step further, we establish PPAD -completeness in polymatrix (e.g. , Cai and Daskalakis, 2011, Cai et al., 2016) team zero-sum games. 

Theorem 1.2. Computing an Пө-approximate Nash equilibrium in polymatrix team zero-sum games is PPAD -complete even when Пө is inversely polynomial in the size of the input. 

As a byproduct, our results resolve the complexity of group-wise zero-sum polymatrix games, a class introduced and examined in the seminal work of Cai and Daskalakis [2011]. In their PPAD -hardness result, the players can be partitioned into three groups such that the games played inside each group are coordination ( i.e. , identical-interest) whereas the games played across groups are zero-sum. We show that PPAD -hardness persists even when there are two groups of players. Reducing to a quadratic polynomial The objective we have constructed so far contains degree-3 monomials involving three independent maximizers. This is an obstacle in establish-ing the desired hardness for 2 vs. 2 team zero-sum games, since one team would need to contain at least 3 players. To address this challenge, our next step is to eliminate the degree-3 terms. To do so, we use an algebraic identity to write every cubic monomial Лң xЛңyЛңz as a linear combination of univariate cubic functions of averages of Лң x, Лңy, Лңz. We then approximate each univariate cubic through a discretized family of tangent functions (Figure 2). In particular, we introduce auxiliary variables whose role is to select the correct tangent. It can be shown that, with sufficiently fine discretization and sufficiently small precision, any approximate stationary point of the quadratic objective yields an approximate stationary point of the original degree-3 objective. 1

1.3 Further related work 

The study of adversarial team games goes back to the seminal work of von Stengel and Koller [1997], who introduced the notion of a team maxmin equilibrium (TME) , which can be viewed as the best Nash equilibrium of the team. However, computing a TME is NP -hard [Borgs, Chayes, Immorlica, Kalai, Mirrokni, and Papadimitriou, 2010]. This has shifted the research focus to relaxations of TME that make allowance for ex ante correlation between the members of the team, a direction that has proven particularly fruitful in the context of extensive-form games [Basilico, Celli, Nittis, and Gatti, 2017, Farina, Celli, Gatti, and Sandholm, 2018a,b, 2021, Zhang and An, 2020]. More recently, Carminati, Zhang, Farina, Gatti, and Sandholm [2023] considered hidden role games , which is a considerably more challenging class of problems. Our focus in this paper is on the standard Nash equilibrium. Beyond settings with a single adversary, Schulman and Vazirani [2019] analyzed the duality gap in team zero-sum games as a function of the number of players. Kalogiannis, Panageas, and Vlatakis-Gkaragkounis [2023b] established that certain natural learning algorithms fail to converge even in 2 vs. 2 team zero-sum games; our main hardness result provides an explanation for this phenomenon. Hollender, Maystre, and Nagarajan [2025] established CLS -hardness for computing Nash equilibria in team zero-sum games with multiple independent adversaries. CLS , which stands for continuous local search, is a complexity class introduced by Daskalakis and Papadimitriou [2011]. Fearnley, Goldberg, Hollender, and Savani [2023] showed that, surprisingly, CLS = PPAD вҲ©

PLS . PPAD was introduced by Papadimitriou [1994], and was famously shown to characterize the complexity of computing Nash equilibria in general-sum games [Daskalakis, Goldberg, and Papadimitriou, 2009, Chen, Deng, and Teng, 2009]. One question that remains open in team zero-sum games concerns the regime where the precision Пө is an absolute constant. Rubinstein [2016] established a quasipolynomial lower bound in general-sum games under a natural complexity assumption; extending that result in team zero-sum games with a constant number of players is an 6important open question. Turning to the more general setting of min-max optimization, Daskalakis, Skoulakis, and Zam-petakis [2021] established PPAD -hardness for computing first-order stationary points under cou-pled constraints . Subsequent work simplified their reduction [Anagnostides, Panageas, Sandholm, and Yan, 2025, Bernasconi, Castiglioni, Celli, and Farina, 2026a]. A recent breakthrough result by Bernasconi and Castiglioni [2026] showed PPAD -hardness even with uncoupled constraints, which is more in line with the applications of min-max optimization. Even more recently, Bernasconi, Castiglioni, Celli, and Hollender [2026c] established exponential query lower bounds in the black box model for computing first-order stationary points in min-max optimization. Motivated by a host of applications in machine learning and reinforcement learning, there has been a tremendous amount of interest in solving min-max optimization problems; e.g. , we refer to Ostrovskii, Lowy, and Razaviyayn [2021], Jordan, Lin, and Zampetakis [2023], Nouiehed, Sanjabi, Huang, Lee, and Razaviyayn [2019], Wibisono, Tao, and Piliouras [2022], Mokhtari, Ozdaglar, and Pattathil [2020], Choudhury, Gorbunov, and Loizou [2023], Luo, Ye, Huang, and Zhang [2020], Xu, Zhang, Xu, and Lan [2023], Gorbunov, Taylor, and Gidel [2022], Cai, Oikonomou, and Zheng [2022], Daskalakis, Foster, and Golowich [2020], and references therein. 

Concurrent and independent work An independent and concurrent work by Bernasconi, Castiglioni, Celli, and Hollender [2026b] also establishes PPAD -hardness for min-max optimization with quadratic objectives. We will elaborate on the technical differences between the two approaches in a future version of this paper.

# 6 Conclusions and future research 

We established that computing approximate Nash equilibria in team zero-sum games is PPAD -complete even in the polymatrix setting with a 2 vs. 2 structure. As a result, team zero-sum games are computationally as hard as general-sum games, despite maintaining a global adversar-ial structure. As a byproduct, we also showed that computing first-order stationary points in min-max optimization is PPAD -complete even for quadratic objectives, strengthening the recent breakthrough result of Bernasconi and Castiglioni [2026]. Our results leave open an important question. Specifically, the hardness established here applies under an inverse polynomial precision, ruling out a fully polynomial-time approximation scheme (FPTAS )вҖ”unless P = PPAD . It remains unclear whether a PTAS exists. Can one adapt the hardness result of Rubinstein [2016] (or even Babichenko, Papadimitriou, and Rubinstein, 2016) in team zero-sum games?
