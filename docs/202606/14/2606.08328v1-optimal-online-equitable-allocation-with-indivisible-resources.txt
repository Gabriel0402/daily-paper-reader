# Optimal Online Equitable Allocation with Indivisible Resources 

Ramiro N. Deo-Campo Vuong ∗

Abstract 

Equitable allocation of indivisible goods to agents in online settings is an algorithmic primi-tive with applications for load balancing, network routing, online marketplaces, and multi-agent systems. We consider a general setting in which allocations are constrained to be bases of discrete polymatroids that arrive online. Our work demonstrates that a simple, myopic algorithm called Brick-Laying , which greed-ily minimizes the sum of squared loads on agents, achieves a universal and objective-free notion of optimality called majorization minimax-optimality [BDK26] for this setting. As a consequence, 

Brick-Laying simultaneously guarantees minimax optimal competitive ratios and regret for 

all Schur-concave and Schur-convex objectives, and for any number of agents and resources (despite being agnostic to problem scale). Departing from popular primal-dual analysis, we employ majorization to compare alloca-tions. We leverage the conjugates of integer partitions – which act as a discrete dual to ma-jorization – to characterize worst-case instances for the Brick-Laying algorithm. Our approach reveals a novel structural connection between the geometry of partitions and online equitable allocation.

# 1 Introduction 

Equitable allocation of indivisible resources (goods/chores) to agents in online settings is a funda-mental algorithmic primitive. In such problems, resources arrive over time, and must be irrevocably assigned according to some specified combinatorial constraints. Equity is measured in terms of some function of the allocations, and optimizing it is challenging because assignments are made without knowledge of future constraints. In this work, we consider the following general variant of this problem: feasible allocations are described by a sequence of discrete polymatroid bases that arrive online. We model worst-case settings by assuming these allocation constraints are designed by an adaptive adversary who has access to past resource assignments. Below, we provide some special cases of our model: 

Online b-Matching [KP00]: There are n offline nodes. When online node t of m total online nodes arrives, it reveals a neighborhood Nt ⊆ [n] and must be irrevocably assigned to a neighboring offline node. The goal is to maximize P

Online Batched b-Matching [FN25]: This problem is the same as the above, except online nodes arrive together in batches instead of one at a time. All online nodes in a batch are irrevocably matched before the arrival of the next batch. 

Online Network Load Balancing: There are n servers. In an online fashion for h rounds, an integral capacity flow network is revealed with servers as sink nodes. An integral max flow from sources, representing requests, to servers is selected. The goal is to route requests to minimize the sum of server latencies, where the latency of a server routed l total requests is 12 l(l + 1). Despite the generality of polymatroid constraints and numerous different equity objectives one could consider, these settings admit a simple solution: brick-laying . This algorithm, which greedily allocates resources to minimize the sum of squared agent loads, satisfies a universal notion of equitable allocation optimality that we call majorization minimax-optimality (MMO ). As a consequence of MMO , brick-laying guarantees minimax optimal regret and competitive ratios for all reasonable equity-promoting objectives and any number of agents and resources. The performance of online allocation algorithms is often measured via competitive ratios, defined on one or many objectives. Despite the numerous celebrated results derived from competitive ratio analysis and objective design, these approaches have several shortcomings. First, objective design risks over-stylization, which may lead to unintuitive and sensitive algorithms. Second, natural objectives are neglected if they do not admit a satisfactory bound for the artificial measure of competitive ratios. For instance, the Nash Social Welfare 1 objective f (ℓ) = ( Q

We use the objective-free MMO condition to analyze allocation algorithms. This notion, developed by [BDK26] for online allocation with divisible resources, uses the well-studied majorization preorder to compare the equity of various allocations. Our elegant MMO analysis implies sharp competitive ratio optimality guarantees for brick-laying under many equity-promoting objectives, suggesting that 

brick-laying is the most robust algorithm for worst-case online equitable allocation.

1.2 Majorization Minimax-Optimality 

In this section, we introduce majorization minimax-optimality ( MMO ) as a natural objective-free notion of optimality and illuminate how it implies optimal α-regret for all comparison factors 

α. To show that deterministic allocation strategy A minimizes regret under equity measure f

and comparison factor α, it suffices to show Reg n,m f,α (A) ≤ Reg n,m f,α (A′) for all alternate policies 

A′. Since regret considers worst-case adversarial play, this is equivalent to the following: for all deviation policies A′ and adversarial strategies P ∈ P n,m , there exists an adversarial strategy 

P′ ∈ P n,m such that A′ is no better against P′ than A against P:

∀A ′ ∀P ∃P ′ cf,α (A, P) ≤ cf,α (A′, P′).

A sufficient condition for the inequality above is that (A, P) simultaneously achieves weakly higher allocation equity and weakly lower hindsight equity than ( A′, P′), i.e., f (ℓ(A, P)) ≥ Ef (ℓ(A′, P′)) and f (OPT f (A, P)) ≤ Ef (OPT f (A′, P′)). This reduces optimality to pairwise comparisons of load vector equity, as measured by f . To remove dependence on f , we can ask: is there an objective-free notion of vector equity that recovers the above as a consequence for all reasonable equity-promoting objectives? Majorization theory answers this question in the positive. Majorization, a preorder relation on vectors, is founded on the Dalton-Pigou transfer or Robin principle that specifies that transferring resources from the wealthy to the poor yields more equitable outcomes [Arn87, MOA11].

Equipped with majorization, we now present majorization minimax-optimality. We alter the previous sufficient condition for optimal regret in two ways. First, we apply Fact 1 below, which establishes the existence of a majorization-minimal hindsight load vector. This implies that there is a randomized load vector ℓ∗, depending only on the realization of the set H(A, P), such that

Definition 3 (Majorization Minimax-Optimality) . For n agents and m resources, a deter-ministic allocation strategy A is majorization minimax-optimal ( MMO ) if for all alterna-tive strategies A′ and adversarial strategies P ∈ P n,m , there exists an adversarial strategy

ℓ(A, P) ⪯ ℓ(A′, P′) and OPT (A, P) ⪰ OPT (A′, P′)Like an equilibrium strategy in the online allocation game, an MMO strategy implies that deviation from the strategy leaves the algorithm designer susceptible to worse game outcomes. The notable difference is that MMO uses majorization to compare outcomes, rather than regret (or competitive ratios). Thus, an MMO strategy is more robust than a regular equilibrium strategy because it implies minimax optimal regret under any reasonable equity-promoting objectives.

1.4 Main Result 

We can now formally state our main results, establishing that brick-laying is MMO .

Theorem 1 (brick-laying Optimality) . The brick-laying algorithm is majorization minimax-optimal under any number of agents n and resources m.

As an immediate consequence of the MMO property, we get that brick-laying simultaneously achieves optimal regret minimization under any Schur-monotone function, and any comparison factor α (and hence minimax optimal competitive ratios).

Discussion of Results: Before proceeding, we highlight three aspects of this result. 

• By establishing brick-laying is MMO compared to any algorithm facing an adaptive adver-sary, Theorem 1 also implies that brick-laying is the optimal deterministic policy against an oblivious adversary – one who designs the entire polymatroid sequence with knowledge of the algorithm. It does not, however, apply when comparing to randomized strategies for oblivious adversaries, such as the Ranking algorithm for online matching [KVV90]. 

• While allowing complex polymatroid constraints allows us to capture a wide variety of set-tings, interestingly, it does not give the adversary additional power: in Lemma 1, we show that the best response to brick-laying is an online semi-matching instance. In this sense, there is no loss in considering polymatroids over more commonly studied semi-matching constraints. 

• Since polymatroids are closed under Minkowski sums, and brick-laying yields the hindsight optimal solution if given the full instance (Fact 1), one can view Theorem 1 as an online 

local-to-global property: it shows that myopically using brick-laying on local views of the polymatroid is optimal in a minimax sense. 

1.5 Related Work 

Majorization is a cornerstone for analyzing equity in many domains [Arn87, HLP88, MOA11], in-cluding semi-matchings [FM22, HLLT06], network flows [Meg74], load balancing [BGM01, KRT99], and persuasion [BMSW24]. It is linked to integer partition conjugates, which characterize feasible degree distributions in bipartite graphs and directed graphs via the Gale-Ryser and Fulkerson The-orems [Ful60, Gal57, Rys57]. Further, several equivalent characterizations exist for majorization-minimal polymatroid bases [FM18, FM22], which can be computed greedily [FG86]. [KVV90] pioneered online matching, with subsequent work simplifying its analysis [BM08, DJK13, EFFS21] and extending matching to broader allocation problems [KP00, MSVV07, Meh10]. Recent research has explored fractional variants [BN09, DJ12, HJP +24, Pat26] and the use of pre-dictions to optimize objectives, which otherwise admit poor competitive ratios [BGGJ22, CS19, KMS23]. Most existing online allocation analyses rely on primal-dual techniques and differential equations. However, as noted by [DJ12], these methods often insinuate a mysterious “kind of duality between the algorithm and the counterexample”. We suggest that in integral equitability settings, this duality is captured by the conjugate of integral partitions. The applications of majorization to online equitable allocation remain sparse. [GMP05] proves that a special case of brick-laying yields allocations that approximately minorize the optimal hindsight solution. Most closely related to our work is [BDK26], which proves the Water-Filling 

algorithm is MMO for online fractional semi-matching. The techniques in [BDK26] critically depend on the uniqueness and functional form of the Water-Filling allocation, and it is unclear if the insights carry over to integer allocations. To circumvent this, we establish novel structural connections between online allocation and integer partition conjugates, which then allow us to extend MMO to more complex combinatorial constraints. 

# 2 Proof Overview and an Illustrative Example 

Since MMO resembles an equilibrium condition, our analysis parallels equilibrium analysis in two-player zero-sum games. In particular, the crux of our proof is showing that the adversary’s best response to the algorithm designer using brick-laying is to employ a nested strategy .5Definition 5 (Nested Strategy) . An adversarial strategy P ∈ P n,m is nested if, for any strategy A,the profile (A, P) induces a polymatroid sequence P1, . . . , P m whose rank functions r1, . . . , r m take the form rt(A) = 1 {A ∩ Nt̸ = ∅} for N1 ⊇ · · · ⊇ Nm with probability 1.

Nested strategies pose a challenge because the algorithm designer would prefer to assign re-sources to agents who are absent from future neighborhoods, but she cannot identify such agents from past information. Notably, nested strategies are semi-matching instances in which online nodes arrive one at a time: online node t has neighborhood Nt.In Lemma 1, we demonstrate that nested strategies are a best response to brick-laying . We do so by treating the load vector produced by brick-laying as an integer partition of m resources into n parts (agents) and leveraging its conjugate. 

Definition 6 (Conjugate Load Vector) . The conjugate load vector of ℓ ∈ Zn 

> ≥0

with ℓ([ n]) = m,denoted as ℓ◦ ∈ Zm

> ≥0

, has ℓ◦(j) = |{ i ∈ [n] | x(i) ≥ j}| for all j ∈ [m].

Informally, the conjugate of a load vector – that behaves like a discrete dual to majorization – encodes the number of agents receiving at least j resources for each j. In Section 2.1, we illustrate via a simple example how we use the conjugate to establish that nested instances are a best response to brick-laying . The formal result for general polymatroids is presented in Section 3. The second part of our proof, Lemma 3, compares an alternative algorithm A′ to brick-laying .Using Lemma 1, we can focus only on nested strategies P. Our goal now is to construct P′ on which ( BL , P) provides weakly better outcomes than ( A′, P′). To construct such a P′, we relabel offline nodes so that agents with low load under strategy A′ never have another opportunity to be allocated a resource. We note that a similar proof is performed in [BDK26] for fractional allocation problems. Composing Lemma 1 and Lemma 3 shows that brick-laying is MMO .

B.1 Polymatroids 

Polymatroids generalize matroids to multisets [HH02, Fuj05]. Like matroids, polymatroids possess several axiomatic properties that make them conducive to greedy optimization. Given vectors x, y,we use x ≤ y when x(i) ≤ y(i), ∀i.

Fact 3 (Polymatroid Axioms [HH02]) . A discrete polymatroid P satisfies each of the following: 

• Zero-vector membership: ⃗ 0 ∈ P

• Downward closure: if x ∈ P and y ≤ x for y ∈ Zn 

> ≥0

then y ∈ P

• Exchange property: if x, y ∈ P with x([ n]) < y([ n]) , then there is i ∈ [n] satisfying y(i) > x(i)

and x + χi ∈ P .

B.2 Convex Games 

A convex game is a special case of a cooperative game among n agents. These games admit a non-empty core – outcomes that cannot be improved upon by any coalition of players – that can be characterized by a simple greedy algorithm [Sha71]. Discrete convex games are characterized by a characteristic function v : 2 [n] → Z≥0 that maps a coalition of players to the value they can collectively generate. The function must be normalized v(∅) = 0 and supermodular v(A) + v(B) ≤

v(A ∪ B) + v(A ∩ B) for all A, B ⊆ [n]. The core of the game is the set of payoff vectors x ∈ Z≥0

that are efficient x([ n]) = v([ n]) and incentivize every coalition to participate in the grand coalition 

x(A) ≥ v(A) for all coalitions A ⊆ [n]. Our results extend to the problem of computing equitable cores in convex games, as they are intimately related to bases of polymatroids. Specifically, a vector is a basis of the polymatroid with rank function r([ n] \ A) = v([ n]) − v(A) if and only if the same vector is a core of the convex game with characteristic function v [Fuj05]. 

# C Proof of Brick-Laying Properties 

Proposition 1 (brick-laying Majorization Minimality) . Given Pt and ℓt−1 as input, Algorithm 1 computes xt ∈ Bases (Pt) yielding majorization minimal xt + ℓt−1 in polynomial time. Proof. This proof has three components. We first argue efficiency. We have value oracle access to rt

by assumption and can thus implement a value oracle for ert = rt + ℓt−1 in polynomial time. A value oracle of ert, defining polymatroid ePt, can be used to efficiently implement an independence oracle for ePt that decides x ∈ ePt.
