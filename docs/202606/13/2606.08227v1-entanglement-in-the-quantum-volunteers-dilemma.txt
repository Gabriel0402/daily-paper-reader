A well-known model in game theory, the Volunteer’s Dilemma describes a group of n players who decide whether to volunteer for a collective benefit at a personal cost, or to abstain and risk forfeiting the benefit altogether. A quantum version of this dilemma, developed within the Eis-ert–Wilkens–Lewenstein framework, allows each player to manipulate one qubit of a shared entan-gled state, leading to symmetric Nash equilibria with higher expected payoffs than in the classical game. Existing analyses, however, assume maximal entanglement. Within the same framework, we introduce a generalized Quantum Volunteer’s Dilemma with a tunable entanglement parameter 

γ and study the extent to which equilibrium behavior depends on the level of entanglement. We derive explicit conditions relating γ, the number of players, and the players’ strategies under which symmetric Nash equilibria exist, focusing on two canonical strategy profiles: one for 2 ≤ n ≤ 9, and one for even n. We find that maximal entanglement is not required to sustain symmetric equilibria. Instead, equilibrium behavior persists above a threshold value, which we compute analytically in both cases. We also demonstrate that the threshold value directly depends on system size. This characterization is directly relevant for implementations on resource-constrained quantum devices, where entanglement is inherently limited. 

I. INTRODUCTION 

Entanglement is a central resource that distinguishes quantum information processing from its classical coun-terpart [1]. It underlies many important applications in quantum information science, including quantum com-puting [2–8], quantum communication [9–13], quantum cryptography [14–16], and quantum metrology [17–19]. Entanglement has also proven to be a useful strategic as-set in quantum game theory, where classical games are generalized within a quantum framework [20]. In quantum games, players choose quantum operations to apply to their respective quantum systems, rather than being restricted to classical pure or mixed strategies [21– 23]. Such formulations can introduce non-classical cor-relations among players’ strategies, change payoff struc-tures and equilibrium conditions, and enable outcomes that are inaccessible in the corresponding classical game. Quantum game theory therefore provides a natural set-ting in which entanglement can be studied as a strategic resource. Early work by Meyer showed that a player with access to quantum operations can outperform a player restricted to classical strategies in a simple finite game setting [24]. Eisert, Wilkens, and Lewenstein then developed one of the central frameworks for quantizing nonzero-sum games, showing through the Prisoner’s Dilemma that quantum strategies can change the equilibrium structure

of a classical dilemma [20]. In this framework, players ap-ply local unitary operations to a shared quantum state, with payoffs assigned according to the outcome of a final measurement. Since these early developments, quantum game theory has been applied to a broad range of game-theoretic sce-narios, including multiplayer [25–27], continuous-variable [28], evolutionary [29], and other generalized settings [30], as well as to a variety of game-theoretic models [31–44], with computational tools developed to support their analysis [45–47]. Studies of noise have examined how noise affects quantum-game behavior [48–56], whilst experiments across platforms have connected quantum games to practical issues of noise and control [57–64]. In parallel, various works have investigated the role of quantum resources in quantum strategic advantage [65–75]. Among these resources, entanglement plays a central role. Du et al. showed that entanglement can in-duce threshold behavior in the equilibrium structure [65], while their later work showed that it can enhance equi-librium payoffs in multiplayer games [66]. Li and Yong showed that entanglement can guarantee the emergence of cooperation in evolutionary quantum games on net-works [70]. More recently, Bugu showed that multipar-tite entanglement can enhance coordination and strategic efficiency in adversarial quantum games [74]. Together, these results show that entanglement is not merely an auxiliary feature of quantum games, but a resource that can shape strategic behavior itself. This perspective motivates the study of entanglement in multiplayer collective-action games. The Volunteer’s Dilemma is a canonical example of such a problem. First 

> arXiv:2606.08227v1 [quant-ph] 6 Jun 2026

2introduced by Diekmann [76], it describes a situation in which individuals in a group decide whether to volun-teer, thereby securing a collective benefit at a personal cost, or to abstain, thereby avoiding the cost but risking the loss of the collective benefit altogether. The dilemma captures a broad class of social traps in which individ-uals prefer that a public benefit be provided, but also prefer that someone else bear the cost. It has therefore been used to study leadership emergence [77], burden-sharing [78], free-riding [79], the bystander effect [80, 81], and diffusion of responsibility [82, 83], with applications in economic modeling [84], climate policy [85], and mili-tary strategy [86]. Its scope also extends beyond human decision-making, with applications to collective behavior in animal groups [87–91], cancer cells [92–94], and mi-crobial populations [95, 96]. Several variants have also been introduced, including asymmetric [97–101], thresh-old [102, 103], timing [98, 104], and cost-sharing versions [78, 86, 105]. In particular, the cost-sharing version in-troduced by Weesie and Franzen allows the burden of volunteering to be shared equally among multiple volun-teers [78]. The Quantum Volunteer’s Dilemma of Koh, Kumar, and Goh [41] builds on the Weesie–Franzen cost-sharing formulation [78]. In the underlying classical game, the pure Nash equilibria are asymmetric: equilibrium occurs when exactly one player volunteers, while the remain-ing players receive the collective benefit without paying the cost. A symmetric mixed-strategy equilibrium also exists, but this restores symmetry only at the level of ex-pected payoffs. In any particular realization of the game, there remains a nonzero probability that no player volun-teers, which eliminates any collective benefit. Thus, the Classical Volunteer’s Dilemma contains both an asym-metry problem, because the burden falls on a single vol-unteer, and a coordination problem, because symmetric play can still lead to collective failure [98]. The Quantum Volunteer’s Dilemma revisits this coor-dination problem by allowing players to act on a shared entangled quantum state [41]. Koh, Kumar, and Goh showed that, under maximal entanglement, the quantum game admits symmetric Nash equilibria with higher ex-pected payoffs than the classical mixed equilibrium [41]. However, existing analyses assume that the shared state is maximally entangled. This leaves open an important question: are these symmetric equilibria a feature only of the maximally entangled case, or do they persist when the amount of entanglement is reduced? In this paper, we address this gap by introducing a variable entanglement parameter and studying how the equilibrium behavior changes as the amount of entangle-ment is varied. In doing so, we characterize the amount of entanglement required for the quantum game to retain the symmetric equilibrium behavior found in the maxi-mally entangled case. The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. In Sec. II, we introduce the mathematical formulations of the Classical Volunteer’s Dilemma and the Quantum Volunteer’s Dilemma. In Sec. III, we generalize the quan-tum game by introducing the entanglement parameter γ.In Secs. IV and V, we analyze two strategy sets of interest and derive lower bounds on γ in terms of the number of players n. Finally, in Sec. VI, we summarize our results.

> B. Nash Equilibria

To evaluate the quality of a strategy profile, we use the notion of a Nash equilibrium [106], which describes a scenario where no player has an incentive to deviate from their strategy. A profile x is “stable” in this sense if no player i can improve their payoff by changing their own strategy while all others are held fixed: 

$i(x) ≥ $i(x ⊕ ei),

where ei is the binary vector with a 1 in the i-th position and 0 elsewhere, and ⊕ denotes bitwise addition. More 3generally, a strategy profile s = ( s1, . . . , s n) is a Nash equilibrium of a game G if, for every player i ∈ [n] and every alternative strategy ti ∈ Ti \ { si},

$i(s1, . . . , s i, . . . , s n) ≥ $i(s1, . . . , t i, . . . , s n). (3) In the classical Volunteer’s Dilemma, the only Nash equilibria are those in which exactly one player volun-teers, i.e., wt( x) = 1 . These equilibria are inherently asymmetric: one player incurs the cost while the others free-ride, leading to unequal payoffs. They are also fragile in practice, since coordinating to produce a single volun-teer is difficult. Several variants attempt to address this. Mixed-strategy formulations, for example, assign a com-mon probability of volunteering across players [76, 78]. This restores symmetry at the level of expected payoffs, but it does not eliminate the possibility that no players volunteer. The quantum formulation of the game, how-ever, addresses the issue well.

> VI. CONCLUSION

In this study, we introduced a generalization of the Quantum Volunteer’s Dilemma by incorporating the en-tanglement parameter γ, thereby extending the EWL-based formulation of Koh, Kumar, and Goh [41]. This generalization allowed us to analyze the extent to which the previously identified symmetric Nash equilibria de-pend on maximal entanglement to exist. In particular, we studied two families of symmetric Nash equilibria. The first relates to the strategy profile Qn = (0 , πn )n

while the second relates to An = (0 , π 

> 2

)n. In both cases, we showed that the profile remains a Nash equilibrium for suitable non-maximal values of γ, provided that a specific inequality is satisfied. These conditions quantify how the entanglement threshold for each symmetric Nash equilibrium depends on the number of players n.In summary, our results show that the strategic advan-tages of the Quantum Volunteer’s Dilemma are not con-fined to the case of maximal entanglement. Symmetric Nash equilibria with quantum advantage can persist un-der partial entanglement, suggesting a degree of robust-ness that is relevant for implementations in near-term quantum devices [107].
