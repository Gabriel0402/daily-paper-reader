## Abstract 

Offline multi-agent reinforcement learning in general-sum settings is challenged by the distribution shift between logged datasets and target equilibrium policies. While standard methods rely on manual pessimistic penalties, we demonstrate that KL regularization suffices to stabilize learning and achieve equilibrium re-covery. We propose General-sum Anchored Nash Equilibrium (GANE), which recovers regularized Nash equilibria at an accelerated statistical rate of ЋЬO(1 /n ).For computational tractability, we develop General-sum Anchored Mirror Descent (GAMD), an iterative algorithm converging to a Coarse Correlated Equilibrium at the standard rate of ЋЬO(1 /вИЪn+1 /T ). These results establish KL regularization as a standalone mechanism for pessimism-free offline learning that achieves equivalent or accelerated rates in multi-player general-sum games. 

## 1 Introduction 

Offline reinforcement learning (RL) provides a framework for developing decision-making policies from pre-collected datasets, making it suitable for applications where active exploration is restricted by safety or financial costs (Levine et al., 2020). While much of the offline literature has historically addressed single-agent optimization, many strategic environments involve interactions among multiple agents with distinct objectives. General-sum games offer a flexible model for these scenarios, capturing strategic complexities in economic markets, multi-agent coordination, and the alignment of large language models through collective human preferences (Ouyang et al., 2022; Ye et al., 2024). In this offline multi-agent setting, the learning objective is to recover an equilibrium policy using only fixed historical data. A central difficulty in offline general-sum games is distribution shift, which occurs when the learned strategy explores regions of the state-action space that are poorly represented in the logged dataset (Fujimoto et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2023). Standard methodologies typically address this shift through the principle of explicit pessimism (Jin et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023). These approaches incorporate manually designed lower confidence bounds (LCB) or penalty terms to suppress the estimated values of actions that lack sufficient offline data support (Cui and Du, 2022b; Zhong et al., 2022). While theoretically sound, these pessimistic mechanisms often require the construction of complex uncertainty quantifiers over joint action spaces and intensive hyperparameter tuning to balance conservatism with performance (Cui and Du, 2022b; Zhang et al., 2023).

Recently, KL regularization with respect to a fixed reference policy has become a common objective for stabilizing multi-agent learning and enforcing behavioral constraints (Ouyang et al., 2022; Ye et al., 2024). While this framework has gained significant prominence in single-agent settingsвАФfor instance, in language model alignment through Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) (Ouyang et al., 2022)вАФits application to multi-player general-sum settings remains relatively scarce. In existing literature that utilizes KL regularization within game-theoretic contexts, the regularization is typically treated solely as an anchor to a reference distribution, while distribution shift is handled by another separate, explicit pessimism mechanism such as lower confidence bounds (Ye et al., 2024).However, the capacity of KL regularization to act as a standalone, implicit pessimism mechanism that independently stabilizes learning against distribution shift in multi-player general-sum games remains unestablished. In this work, we establish that KL regularization serves as a standalone, pessimism-free mechanism to stabilize offline learning in general-sum games. To achieve this, we leverage the reference-anchored data coverage framework (Anonymous, 2026), which bypasses the conceptual circularity inherent in prior works by anchoring coverage requirements to a known reference policy rather than an unknown equilibrium. We first introduce General-sum Anchored Nash Equilibrium (GANE), a theoretical framework that recovers regularized Nash equilibria at an accelerated statistical rate of 

ЋЬO(1 /n ). Our analysis reveals that the independent product structure of Nash policies enables the exact cancellation of first-order estimation errors, allowing GANE to bypass the standard ЋЬO(1 /вИЪn)

bottleneck encountered in unregularized offline games (Zhang et al., 2023; Cui and Du, 2022b). Recognizing that computing a Nash equilibrium is often computationally intractable, we further develop General-sum Anchored Mirror Descent (GAMD), a decentralized iterative algorithm. We prove that GAMD recovers a Coarse Correlated Equilibrium at the standard rate of ЋЬO(1 /вИЪn + 1 /T )

for n samples and T iterations. Our primary contributions are summarized as follows: 1. Pessimism-Free Equilibrium Recovery : We establish that KL regularization serves as an effective alternative to explicit pessimism, allowing for equilibrium recovery in general-sum settings without the overhead of tuning confidence-based bonuses. 2. Accelerated Rates for Nash Equilibria : We prove that GANE achieves an ЋЬO(1 /n ) sta-tistical rate. This result demonstrates that the product structure of Nash equilibria can be leveraged to achieve higher statistical efficiency than previously established for unregularized general-sum offline learning. 3. Tractable Iterative Learning : We show that GAMD provides a computationally efficient path to recovering Coarse Correlated Equilibria, achieving the standard ЋЬO(1 /вИЪn) statistical rate through simple, decentralized updates without relying on explicit pessimism.

## 4 Pessimism-Free Nash Equilibria 

Building on the regularized objective (1) , we investigate whether anchoring to a reference policy can independently mitigate the distribution shift inherent in offline multi-agent settings. We first introduce General-sum Anchored Nash Equilibrium (GANE), an algorithmic framework that leverages KL regularization to stabilize learning on the offline contextual bandit without the overhead of explicit pessimistic bonuses. GANE provides the foundational basis for our analysis, illustrating how the geometric properties of the regularizer interact with the structural properties of Nash equilibria. GANE operates via empirical risk minimization over the static offline dataset. For every player 

i вИИ [m], the algorithm first constructs an empirical estimate of the reward Q-function, ЋЖQi, by solving a regularized least-squares regression problem over the dataset D using the function class Qi. Given these empirical Q-functions, GANE assumes access to a stage-game equilibrium oracle that outputs a joint product policy ЋЖѕА(a | x) = Qmi=1 ЋЖѕАi(ai | x) forming an exact Regularized NE for the empirical game. 5.1 Theoretical Guarantees of GAMD 

To formalize the convergence of GAMD, we first bound the optimization error of the inner loop. Because the KL-divergence penalty transforms the update into a strongly concave objective, applying Mirror Descent yields an accelerated optimization rate. ## 6 Related Work 

Pessimism in Offline Reinforcement Learning. Offline reinforcement learning (RL) focuses on policy optimization from pre-collected datasets without the benefit of interactive exploration. The primary theoretical obstacle in this regime is distribution shift, wherein the target policyвАЩs visitation frequency deviates from the logged behavior, inducing severe overestimation and extrapolation error (Levine et al., 2020). To counteract this bias, researchers utilize the principle of pessimism , which provides a robust framework for establishing sample-efficient guarantees by penalizing values in poorly explored state-action regions (Liu et al., 2020; Rashidinejad et al., 2021; Jin et al., 2021; Xie et al., 2021; Uehara and Sun, 2021; Zhan et al., 2022). These conservative strategies, often implemented via lower confidence bounds or conservative value iterations, have been proven minimax optimal under various single-policy concentrability settings (Li et al., 2024). In game-theoretic environments, the complexity of this shift is intensified by strategic interactions, requiring sufficient data coverage of unilateral deviations for each agent to ensure successful equilib-rium recovery (Cui and Du, 2022b). Consequently, the current literature on offline Markov games relies heavily on these pessimistic foundations to mitigate information gaps within the static logs (Cui and Du, 2022b; Zhong et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023). In contrast, our approach establishes that KL regularization suffices to stabilize learning without relying on such explicit pessimistic mechanisms. 

Equilibrium Recovery in Multi-Agent Games. The transition from single-agent RL to multi-agent environments necessitates a shift from value optimality to equilibrium stability, targeting solution concepts such as Nash Equilibrium and Coarse Correlated Equilibrium (CCE) (Roughgarden, 2016; Cui and Du, 2022a,b). To recover these equilibria from fixed datasets, existing methodologies largely adapt the principle of pessimism to the game-theoretic setting, identifying minimax-optimal strategies through conservative value iteration and strategy-wise uncertainty bonuses (Zhong et al., 2022; Cui and Du, 2022a; Zhang et al., 2023; Yan et al., 2024). The theoretical validity of these pessimistic approaches is grounded in the notion of unilateral con-centrability вАФthe requirement that the offline logs sufficiently cover any individual agentвАЩs deviations 8from a target equilibrium (Cui and Du, 2022a). However, anchoring this coverage requirement to an unknown optimal equilibrium ѕАвИЧ introduces a fundamental conceptual circularity, as the very policy required to define data sufficiency is the target of the learning process itself. Our work addresses this by anchoring the learning objective to a fixed and known reference policy ѕАref (Anonymous, 2026), transforming the coverage requirement into a verifiable condition that remains grounded in the support of the offline data. 

KL Regularization and Behavioral Anchoring. KL regularization relative to a fixed reference policy has emerged as a fundamental tool for enforcing behavioral constraints and incorporating prior knowledge into reinforcement learning frameworks (Xiong et al., 2023; Munos et al., 2024). By pe-nalizing deviations from an anchor distribution, such as a pre-trained model or human demonstrations, this mechanism effectively mitigates policy drift and stabilizes the resulting learning dynamics (Ye et al., 2024; Nayak et al., 2025). The most prominent application of this principle is found in the alignment of large language models through Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF), where a KL divergence penalty ensures the optimized model remains within the trusted region of the initial distribution (Ouyang et al., 2022; Rafailov et al., 2023). While regularized objectives have been extensively analyzed across both single-agent and multi-agent regimes (Xie et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025a,b), current theoretical results typically suggest that regularization primarily serves as a numerical stabilizer, yielding sample complexity rates that mirror those of their unregularized counterparts (Ye et al., 2024). Consequently, the capacity of KL regularization to function as a standalone, implicit pessimism mechanism in offline general-sum games has remained unestablished. Our work bridges this gap by demonstrating that regularized KL anchoring suffices for stable equilibrium recovery without explicit bonuses. 

Statistical Efficiency and Fast Rates. Most offline learning guarantees characterize the standard 

eO(1 /вИЪn) statistical rate (Jin et al., 2021; Shi et al., 2022). While recent investigations by Zhang et al. (2026) demonstrated that an accelerated eO(1 /n ) rate is achievable in two-player zero-sum games, those results rely on the minimax structure and skew-symmetry inherent to strictly competitive dynam-ics. In contrast, general-sum games are fundamentally more complex; whereas finding an equilibrium is already PPAD-complete in the two-player general-sum case (Chen et al., 2009), the multi-player set-ting remains equally intractable while introducing more complex strategic interactions (Papadimitriou, 1994; Daskalakis et al., 2009). We broaden this theoretical landscape in two directions. First, we establish that the fast eO(1 /n ) rate persists for Nash equilibria in general-sum games, proving that independenceвАФrather than zero-sum symmetryвАФis the key driver of this acceleration. Second, for the computationally tractable CCE, we provide a pessimism-free framework that achieves the standard eO(1 /вИЪn) rate. Together, these findings establish a tractable foundation for pessimism-free learning in multi-player general-sum games. 

## 7 Discussion 

While our work establishes KL regularization as a standalone, pessimism-free mechanism for equilib-rium learning in general-sum games, several promising directions for future research remain. First, extending this framework to multi-step Markov games is essential to address the accumulation of estimation errors across temporal horizons. Second, adapting our regularized dynamics to partially observable environments or extensive-form games would broaden applicability to real-world sys-tems involving hidden states and asymmetric information. Finally, enhancing robustness against misspecified reference policiesвАФpotentially via adaptive regularization schedulesвАФis a key priority for maintaining reliability in highly non-stationary or adversarial strategic settings. 

## 8 Conclusion 

In this work, we have demonstrated that KL regularization serves as a robust and standalone mecha-nism for stabilizing offline multi-agent learning, effectively mitigating distribution shift without the need for explicit pessimistic penalties. By shifting the coverage requirement from unknown optimal policies to a fixed and known reference anchor, we established a verifiable theoretical framework 9that bypasses the conceptual circularity inherent in prior literature. We proposed GANE, which achieves an accelerated eO(1 /n ) statistical rate for Nash equilibria by leveraging the product structure of independent policies. Furthermore, we provided GAMD, a decentralized iterative framework that recovers Coarse Correlated Equilibria at the standard eO(1 /вИЪn) rate without explicit pessimistic bonuses. Together, these results establish KL regularization as a tractable, pessimism-free alternative for establishing optimal statistical rates in offline game-theoretic learning. 10 References
