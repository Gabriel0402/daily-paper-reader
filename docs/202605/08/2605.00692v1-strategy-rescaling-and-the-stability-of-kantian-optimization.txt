# Strategy Rescaling and the Stability of Kantian Optimization 

Igor Sloev 1 2, Gerasimos Lianos 3

This study investigates the properties and stability of the Multiplicative Kantian Equilibrium 

(MKE) in symmetric games. We first demonstrate that MKE lacks strategic equivalence: the 

Kantian best -response function is not invariant under monoton ic strategy rescaling. This 

strategic non -equivalence implies that the choice of measurement scale – a subjective 

interpretation of the game – materially impacts equilibrium outcomes. Exploiting this non -

equivalence, in a game where players may be Kantian or Nasher, we propose an efficient 

strategy rescaling that allows Kantian s to neutralize the free -rider advantage of Nasher s, while 

preserving Pareto -efficient ou tcomes among themselves. In a dynamic framework, we show 

that the subgame -perfect Nash equilibrium with endogenous choice of optimization type leads 

all players to prefer Kantian optimization over Nash optimization. In an evolutionary setup, we 

show that K antian optimization is an evolutionarily stable strategy (ESS). Our results suggest 

that the inherent strategic non -equivalence of Kantian optimization provides a robust pathway 

to stable cooperation.

rescaling ; Evolutionary stability ; Pareto efficiency

# 1. Introduction 

In a series of seminal publications, John Roemer (2010, 2015, 2019a ) proposed a formal the ory 

of "Kantian optimization" for analyzing cooperation situations and formalized the concept of the 

Multiplicative Kantian Equilibrium (MKE). In a game where players' strategies are represented by 

non -negative numbers, an MKE is a strategy profile such th at no player would prefer to rescale every 

player's strategy by some non -negative factor. Clearly, the MKE criterion differs sharply from the 

Nash criterion, under which players address the question: which action of mine will give me the 

greatest gain, giv en the actions of the other players .

As Roemer (2010) shows, in social dilemmas such as the tragedy of the commons, the prisoner's 

dilemma, Cournot oligopoly, and team production, MKEs are Pareto efficient , in contrast to Nash 

equilibria. Thus, when all players use Kantian optimization, each player ultimately obtains a benefit 

greater than that obtained if all players used Nash optimization. However, if one player begins to 

behave selfishly — maximi zing the ir benefit in the Nash sense — while the others remain Kantian, 

that player might obtain a higher payoff. This is the familiar free‑rider effect. In this regard, there has 

been a debate about whether Kantian behavior constitutes rational behavior or is dri ven by moral 

standards (Roemer 2020, Sher 2020 , Braham and Van Hees 2020 ). The question of the rationality 

and stability of Kantian optimization remains open, with significant consequences given the growing 

number of its applications in areas such as inter national trade (Eichner and Pethig 2020), 

environmental economics (Grafton et al. 2017), industrial organization (Donduran and Ünveren 

2021) , and efficient market systems (Roemer 2019b). 

Our objective in this paper is to contribute to the debate on the rat ionality and stability of the 

Kantian equilibrium by focusing on its strategic non -equivalence. This property has been noted (Sher 

2020) but remains largely unexplored in the literature. 

To gain an intuitive understanding of strategic non -equivalence, con sider the following simple 3

example. In a two -player game, the players’ strategies are 𝑥 𝑖 ≥ 1, and the unit of measurement is 

dollars. Suppose that, according to the solution concept used by Player 1, 𝑥 1 = 5 is Player 1's best 

response to Player 2 playing 𝑥 2 = 2. Now, suppose Player 1 wants to measure both players' strategies 

in logarithms of dollars. This means Player 1 applies the strategy rescaling: 𝑧 𝑖 = 𝑙𝑜𝑔 𝑥 𝑖 and 

optimizes with respect to these new variables . 4 It is then possible that 𝑧 1 = 𝑙𝑜𝑔 5 is no longer the 

best response to 𝑧 2 = 𝑙𝑜𝑔 2. Importantly, this transformation does not change the set of feasible 

outcomes or the underlying preferences over outcomes. It only changes the representation of 

strategies. Nevertheless, it may alter b est responses and, therefore, equilibrium outcomes. This 

observation illustrates the strategic non -equivalence of such transformations. 

We should emphasize that while the same question could be asked under the Nash equilibrium 

concept, the answer is trivia l: Nash equilibrium is invariant to monotonic strategy rescaling. Hence, 

a rescaling of strategies does not affect the set of Nash equilibria. However, as we demonstrate below, 

the best -response functions determined by MKE do depend on strategy rescaling; MKE is 

strategically non -equivalent. The identification and analysis of this property constitutes our first 

contribution. 

Sher (2020) noted this strategic non -equivalence as a critical shortcoming of MKE, requiring a 

normatively privileged measurement of strategies. Roemer (2020) defended it as inherent to 

cooperation, arguing that the right rescaling reveals the symmetry nec essary for collective action. Yet 

neither has investigated whether this non -equivalence could be turned to the advantage of Kantian in 

a game where Kantians and Nash ers interact. 

Exploiting this non -equivalence, however, requires a deliberate choice of the measurement 

scale — that is, the strategy space in which players optimize. As we show in Section 3, the rescaling

Buil ding on this extended definition, we propose an efficient strategy rescaling — measuring 

strategies as deviations from the Nash equilibrium — that neutralizes the free -rider advantage of 

Nashers while preserving Pareto efficiency among Kantians. We demonst rate its power in two 

settings. 

First, in a dynamic game, there are two types of players: Kantians, who apply Kantian 

optimization, and Nashers, who apply Nash optimization. At the first stage, all players choose their 

types. At the second stage, players are matched randomly and play a social dilemma game. We show 

that under the efficient rescaling, Kantians eliminate free -riding opportunities: when matched with a 

Nasher, both obtain the Nash payoff; when matched with another Kantian, they achieve the effi cient 

outcome. Consequently, at the first stage, all players prefer to become Kantians. 

Second, we analyze the stability of Kantian optimization in an evolutionary framework 

(Maynard Smith 1976, Weibull 1997, Hofbauer and Sigmund 1998). We show that adopti ng Kantian 

optimization is an evolutionarily stable strategy (ESS), whereas choosing Nash optimization is not. 

The construction of this rescaling and its applications constitute our third contribution. 

The intuition behind our results is as follows. The st rategy rescaling employed by Kantians is 

observed by Nashers and therefore has a strategic impact on their behavior. This mechanism 

resembles the indirect evolutionary approach (Güth and Yaari 1992; Avataneo et al. 2025; Dekel et 

al. 2007): a choice of pre ferences that differs from fitness -maximizing preferences changes 

opponents' behavior under perfect information (Heifetz et al. 2007; Avataneo et al. 2025). However, 

if preferences are not observed, the strategic effect disappears (Ely and Yilankaya 2001; Güth and 5

Peleg 2001; Ok and Vega -Redondo 2001). In our setting, the choice of strategy rescaling plays a role 

analogous to the choice of preferences, as it is observable and affects Nashers’ responses. Thus, we 

complement the existing results on the stabil ity of Kantian preferences with results on the stability of 

Kantian optimization. 

The stability of Kantian optimization has been shown previously by Curry and Roemer (2012) 

under the assumption that Kantians use Kantian optimization only when matched with another 

Kantian, while they use Nash optimization when matched with Nash er s. In contrast, in our model, 

Kantians use Kantian optimization irrespective of the matching partner. Kantian ethics do not stem 

from opportunism. The consistency of moral attitude s is a particularly interesting feature of our 

framework from a philosophical perspective. 

The rest of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 reviews the related literature. Section 3 

presents our technical results: strategic non -equivalence (3.1 – 3.2), the extended MKE definition 

(3.3), and the efficient rescaling (3.4). Section 4 applies these results to a dynamic game (4.1) and to 

evolutionary stability (4.2). Section 5 concludes. 

# 2. Literature on Kantian -Nasher interaction 

Roemer (2019a) consid ers the interaction between Kantians and Nashers in a prisoner's 

dilemma game. The author assumes that when Kantian meets Nasher, Kantian plays the strategy 

prescribed by the Kantian equilibrium, while Nasher plays the Nash equilibrium strategy. This setup 

implies that neither player is playing his best response to the opponent's strategy. Under this 

assumption and with perfect information about player types, Nashers obtain higher payoffs in the 

prisoner's dilemma. Consequently, within an evolutionary frame work, Nashers drive Kantians to 

extinction. 6

Curry and Roemer (2012) analyze the interaction between Kantians and Nashers under perfect 

information, assuming that when Kantian faces Nasher, Kantian optimizes according to the Nash 

criterion. Under this assum ption, the outcome of a Kantian -Nash meeting is the (inefficient) Nash 

equilibrium, while a meeting between two Kantians yields an efficient outcome. As a result, Kantians 

hold an advantage over Nashers. However, the assumption that Kantians switch their o ptimization 

protocol depending on the opponent's type appears rather strong. 

Grafton et al. (2017) examine interactions within a larger group of simultaneous players. In 

their model, Kantians do not apply the Kantian criterion when they interact with Nashe rs. Their results 

regarding the relative performance of Nashers and Kantians are similar to those of Curry and Roemer 

(2012). In contrast, in our approach, we assume that Kantians always play their best response to the 

opponent's strategy. 

Roemer (2015) br iefly presents an argument in support of Kantian behavior in a different 

context. Suppose there are many societies of fishers, each suffering from the tragedy of the commons. 

If some of these societies begin to optimize using the Kantian protocol (instead of the Nash protocol), 

they will achieve Pareto -efficient allocations, resulting in higher utility for their members than for 

members of societies using Nash optimization. This dynamic favors the spread of Kantian 

optimization. However, this model does not consider the direct interaction between Kantians and 

Nashers or the internal stability of different types of societies. Our model complements Roemer's 

analysis by showing that a Kantian society is evolutionarily stable. 

Alger and Weibull (2013, 2016, 2019) and Alger et al. (2020) employ a different approach. The 

authors show the advantage of players with (partially) Kantian preferences over egoistic players. They 

assume a non -uniform matching process: even if the share of Kantians in the population is arbitrarily 

small, the probability that Kantian meets another Kantian remains sufficiently high. As the authors 

show, under such assortative matching and unobservable preferences, having partially Kantian 7

preferences is more profitable than having egoistic preferences, proving the evolutionary stability of 

Kantian preferences. Our approach differs in that we consider choosing between Kantian and Nash 

optimization, rather than between Kantian and egoistic preferences.

# 3.3. Extension of the definition of MKE 

Roemer’s definition of MKE is undoubtedly applicable to strategic situations when both 

players’ strategies are of the same sign; however, it is less so in the case of strategies havi ng opposite 

signs. Consider, for instance, a game of contributions to a public good. Suppose for the moment that 

a player measures the strategy relative to a reference point x = 1 (i.e., applies the rescaling 𝑥 𝑖 = 1 +

𝑧 𝑖 , 𝑧 𝑖 ≥– 1) and wants to check wheth er the strategy profile (𝑧 1, 𝑧 2) = (1/2, −1/2) is a n MKE .

Then, according to the original definition, one would have to consider strategies (𝑎 𝑧 1, 𝑎 𝑧 2). The latter 

profile, however, would amount to players changing their contributions in different directio ns: for an 

increase in Player 1's contribution, Player 2's contribution would have to decrease, as when comparing 

the profiles (1/2, −1/2) and (1/4, −1/4) at a = 1/2 . This can be argued to contradict the idea of

# 5. Conclusion 

In this article , we investigated the rationality and stability of Kantian optimization as defined 

by John Roemer. We explored the technical properties of the MKE and demonstrated that it does not 

satisfy the property of strategic equivalence. We f ound that (a) a symmetric efficient MKE exists 

under any strategy rescaling; (b) whether the Kantian strategy is a best response to another player's 

strategy depends on the scale used by the play er to measure strategies. 

Metaphorically speaking, different strategy rescalings represent different subjective visions of 

reality, and what constitutes the right strategy – i.e., what satisfies the best -response condition – is 

determined by the Kantian su bjective vision. Different visions lead to different actions, which 19 

determine outcomes, and the choice of subjective vision should lead to the best feasible outcome. 

We have proposed an extension of the definition of MKE that allows for strategies of diffe rent 

signs. Then, we show ed that Kantians are able to achieve a Pareto -efficient outcome and fully counter 

free -riding by Nashers, assuming they choose the same strategy rescaling of a specific form. 

Finally, we ha ve provided two applications based on our technical findings. We constructed a 

two -stage dynamic model where players choose their types at the first stage; then, observing the types 

of others, they play a social dilemma game in pairs at the second stage. W e assumed that Kantians 

use the efficient strategy rescaling and that this is common knowledge. We showed that with 

endogenous choice of player types, all players choose to be Kantians. The resulting equilibrium is 

Pareto efficient and coalition -proof. Thi s establishes the superiority of Kantian optimization over 

Nash optimization under the efficient strategy rescaling. 

In our second application, we considered Kantian -Nasher interaction in the framework of 

evolutionary games. We showed that under the effici ent strategy rescaling, the choice of Kantian 

optimization is an evolutionarily stable strategy, while the choice of Nash optimization is not. 

Our results show that strategic non -equivalence and the choice of vision are key aspects of the 

analysis of strat egic interaction and enable stable cooperation. By choosing to measure strategies 

relative to the Nash equilibrium, Kantian s endogenously construct a game where their behavioral 

norm becomes self -reinforcing and evolutionarily stable. 

We believe that further research into the implications of the strategic non -equivalence of 

Kantian optimization and the stability of MKE will yie ld many interesting results. We hope our article 

serves as a step in this line of inquiry.
