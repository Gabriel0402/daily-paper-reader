# Meritocratic Fairness in Budgeted Combinatorial Multi-armed Bandits via Shapley Values 

## Shradha Sharma 1 , Swapnil Dhamal 1 and Shweta Jain 11Indian Institute of Technology Ropar 

## Abstract 

We propose a new framework for meritocratic fair-ness in budgeted combinatorial multi-armed bandits with full-bandit feedback (BCMAB-FBF). Unlike semi-bandit feedback, the contribution of individual arms is not received in full-bandit feedback, mak-ing the setting significantly more challenging. To compute arm contributions in BCMAB-FBF, we first extend the Shapley value, a classical solution concept from cooperative game theory, to the K-Shapley value, which captures the marginal con-tribution of an agent restricted to a set of size at most K. We show that K-Shapley value is a unique solution concept that satisfies Symmetry, Linear-ity, Null player, and efficiency properties. We next propose K-SVFair-FBF, a fairness-aware bandit al-gorithm that adaptively estimates K-Shapley value with unknown valuation function. Unlike standard bandit literature on full bandit feedback, K-SVFair-FBF not only learns the valuation function under full feedback setting but also mitigates the noise arising from Monte Carlo approximations. The-oretically, we prove that K-SVFair-FBF achieves 

O(T 3/4) regret bound on fairness regret. Through experiments on federated learning and social influ-ence maximization datasets, we demonstrate that our approach achieves fairness and performs more effectively than existing baselines. 

## 1 Introduction 

The stochastic multi-armed bandit (MAB) [Robbins, 1952 ]problem is a framework for sequential decision-making, where at each time step, the learner selects one arm from a set of N

arms and then receives a reward, which is drawn from a fixed but unknown probability distribution associated with that arm. The objective in the MAB setting is to maximize the expected cumulative reward over time. The combinatorial multi-armed bandit (CMAB) problem extends this framework by allowing the learner to select a subset of arms, called a super arm , at each time step. The expected reward from a subset of arms is determined by a deterministic mapping from a set of selected arms to a real-valued reward as a function of the expected rewards from the selected arms [Chen et al. , 2013; Audibert et al. , 2014 ] . Since selecting a large set of arms is often costly in many applications, this paper considers the budgeted variant of CMAB called budgeted CMAB (BCMAB), where the size of a super arm is bounded by K.In BCMAB, the learner receives feedback in one of two forms: semi-bandit feedback (SBF) , where the individual re-ward of each selected arm is observed, or full-bandit feedback (FBF) , where the cumulative reward of the super arm is ob-served. The rewards obtained in both settings are stochastic, coming from a fixed but unknown distribution. Full-bandit feedback reflects more realistic scenarios where detailed in-formation is unavailable. For example, in federated learning (FL), a server may only observe the overall performance of a selected subset of clients without having access to each client’s individual contribution. Similarly, in social influence maxi-mization, one can measure only the total spread of influence from a chosen set of seed users, while the contribution of each individual user remains hidden. A bandit algorithm that focuses solely on reward maximiza-tion often ends up repeatedly selecting the best-performing arms. While this strategy maximizes immediate gain, it can overlook arms that perform nearly as well, leading to winner-takes-all effect. This can reduce participation and engagement from under-selected arms, which may still be valuable, and lead to biased outcomes. This shows the importance of incor-porating fairness into bandit algorithms to ensure that all arms receive a reasonable chance of selection. For instance, in FL, it allows all clients to contribute to the global model, reducing bias and improving representation. In social networks, it en-sures that influence is spread more broadly rather than concen-trated on a few users. In other combinatorial decision-making tasks, it helps distribute resources more evenly, preventing over-reliance on a small subset of options. The notion of fairness in CMAB or BCMAB has been studied extensively in the semi-bandit feedback setting [ Xu et al. , 2020; Liu et al. , 2022a; Huang et al. , 2020; Li et al. , 2019; Chen et al. , 2024 ]. However, under full-bandit feedback, only one work [ Pokhriyal et al. , 2025 ] has addresses fairness by imposing constraints such as a minimum guaranteed number of pulls for each individual arm with fixed quotas. This approach has two limitations; first, the fixed quotas are often unknown and second, this approach does not consider the number of pulls in accordance with the merit of an arm. This limitation highlights the need of extending the notion of meritocratic

fairness [Wang et al. , 2021 ] , which ensures that each arm is selected in proportion to its merit, to BCMAB setting. In the context of FL, for example, clients can be viewed as arms, and their merit can be measured by how much their local updates improve the global model. Extending meritocratic fairness to BCMAB under full-bandit feedback is challenging due to two reasons. First, it is not clear how to define merit of an arm and second, in learning the merit when only the combined reward of a super arm is observed instead of individual rewards. To address the first challenge, we use Shapley value , a concept from coopera-tive game theory, to define the merit of an arm. It has been known to distribute the reward obtained by a subset of agents fairly among the individual agents [Shapley and others, 1953 ].Shapley value, however, assumes access to the marginal con-tribution of an agent to a coalition of arbitrary size. However, in BCMAB, since the subset size is restricted to K, computing marginal contributions in a coalition beyond size K is not possible. Therefore, we consider a restricted version of this concept, which we call K-Shapley value, that considers the marginal contribution of an agent by considering coalitions of size at most K. We also show that K-Shapley value naturally extends all the key properties of Shapley value in this modified setting. To address the second challenge, we first note that comput-ing the K-Shapley value requires evaluating marginal contri-butions across all coalitions of the arms restricted to size K,which grow exponentially with the number of arms. There-fore, in order to estimate K-Shapley value, one has to rely on sampling-based approximations such as Monte Carlo meth-ods [Ghorbani and Zou, 2019; Nagalapatti and Narayanam, 2021; Liu et al. , 2022b ] , even under the setting of known valuation function. When valuation function is not known, one has to learn it through bandit algorithms while integrat-ing Monte Carlo approximations within it. Therefore, unlike standard bandit algorithms, there are two sources of noise in BCMAB-FBF, one arising from learning valuation function and the other arising due to Monte Carlo approximation. This paper proposes K-SVFair-FBF algorithm, which lever-ages K-Shapley value estimations to capture each arm’s contri-bution while incorporating mechanisms to mitigate the effect of noisy approximations. In doing so, it balances equitable participation with reliable learning, even when individual re-wards are not directly observed. Even without the fairness constraint, the best achievable regret of any bandit algorithm under full bandit feedback is O(T 2/3) [Nie et al. , 2022 ] which only learns the reward function. We show that K-SVFair-FBF achieves a regret of O(T 3/4), which is slightly higher to account for the additional noise arising from Monte Carlo approximations. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work that introduces meritocratic fairness in BCMAB-FBF with noisy reward estimations. In summary, our contributions include: • Introducing a novel framework for BCMAB-FBF that en-sures fair selection of arms without relying on structural assumptions on the reward function. • Introducing K-Shapley value for restricted cooperative games where the coalitions are restricted to size at most K

and proving the axiomatic properties of K-Shapley value. • Introducing K-SVFair-FBF that adaptively learns K-Shapley value while converging to meritocratic fair solution. • Establishing theoretical guarantees for our algorithm, prov-ing that it achieves sublinear bounds on fairness regret. • Validating our framework in key real-world applications, including FL and social influence maximization. 

## 2 Related Work 

2.1 CMAB-FBF 

The CMAB-FBF has been widely studied in the context of reward maximization. For example, linear reward functions have been explored in [Rejwan and Mansour, 2020 ] , where the authors propose the combinatorial successive accepts and rejects algorithm. A large body of work has then focused on submodular rewards [ Nie et al. , 2022; Niazadeh et al. , 2021; Fourati et al. , 2023 ], where the objective function is monotone and exhibits diminishing returns. However, all of the above works focus exclusively on reward maximization, without considering fairness constraints. Further, lower bound on regret under the full bandit feedback setting is O(T 2/3) [ Nie 

et al. , 2022 ] for the reward maximization goal. Most of the bandit feedback literature considers structural properties like submodularity or linearity of rewards, which K-SVFair-FBF does not assume, making analysis much more challenging. 

2.2 Fairness in CMAB-SBF 

CMAB with concave rewards and knapsack constraints [Xu et al. , 2020 ] , ensures that each arm receives a minimum level of exposure. Similarly, [Liu et al. , 2022a ] introduces the UCB-LP algorithm to handle long-term linear constraints such as budget and fairness, guaranteeing logarithmic regret with no constraint violations. Other works include [ Li et al. , 2019 ]that address fairness in sleeping bandit setting. Under delayed feedback setting, [ Chen et al. , 2024 ] introduces merit-based fairness and designs algorithms for both reward-independent and reward-dependent delays, providing asymptotic fairness guarantees and proving sublinear regret. These studies pro-vide a strong foundation for fairness in semi-bandit feedback settings and offer a regret of order O(ln T ) which is not achiev-able under full-bandit feedback setup. 

2.3 Shapley Value 

The Shapley value [ Shapley, 1971 ] fairly quantifies individual contributions in a coalition and is widely used in domains such as machine learning for feature attribution [Sundararajan and Najmi, 2020; Frye et al. , 2020 ], crowdsourcing for re-ward distribution and resource allocation [ Nguyen et al. , 2022; Wang et al. , 2020; Iturralde et al. , 2011; An et al. , 2019; Reinhardt and Dada, 2005 ], social networks for identifying influential nodes and optimizing connections [Narayanam and Narahari, 2010; Militano et al. , 2016 ], and FL for client contribution assessment, selection, and reward distribu-tion [Wang et al. , 2019; Nagalapatti and Narayanam, 2021; Yang et al. , 2022 ]. However, computing the exact Shap-ley value is often infeasible due to its exponential cost. As a result, many works propose approximation methods tai-lored to their application [ Nagalapatti and Narayanam, 2021; Sun et al. , 2023; Liu et al. , 2022b ] . Since we tackle a budgeted CMAB setting, Shapley value cannot be computed directly as the worth of all the coalitions (of size > K ) is not known. Therefore, we should consider the solution concepts available for restricted cooperative games (R-games). When not all coalition values are known, [Willson, 1993 ] allocates payoffs using only feasible coalitions and assigns value zero to infea-sible coalitions. Consequently, this would misrepresent cases when the value of the grand coalition is infeasible (e.g., in FL). To address this, [ Albizuri et al. , 2022 ] defines R-games, where even if only a subset of coalitions is feasible, the grand coalition is always assumed to be feasible, and dividends from feasible coalitions determine each player’s R-value. In the BCMAB setting, only coalitions of size at most K are feasible, making larger ones, including the grand coalition, in-feasible and undefined. In [ Derks and Peters, 1993 ], coalition values depend on an underlying communication graph, so a coalition’s worth reflects its connected subgroups rather than assuming full communication. In BCMAB, however, coalition feasibility depends only on the subset size, not on the topolog-ical relations among arms. Therefore, existing graph-based Shapley formulations cannot model our setting. Building on the advances in Shapley value approximations, a few works have attempted to integrate Shapley value into bandit algorithms [ Gray et al. , 2023; Kolpaczki et al. , 2021 ].The focus of their work is on ranking contributors rather than ensuring fairness in online decision-making. Also, these works assume that the exact valuation for a given set is observable as opposed to having noisy observations as in our case. 

## 3 Preliminaries and Problem Statement 

In a Budgeted Combinatorial Multi-armed Bandit with full feedback (BCMAB-FBF) setting, at each time step t, the learner selects a subset of arms St with |St| ≤ K from a finite set of arms [M ] = {1, 2, . . . , M } and receives a stochas-tic reward Vt(St). Each individual arm i ∈ [M ] is referred to as a base arm, while any subset S ⊆ [M ] is called a super-arm. The set of all feasible super-arms is denoted by 

A = {S ⊆ [M ] : |S| ≤ K}. We further denote πt(i) as the probability of picking an arm i at time t by a policy π that is output by the algorithm. The expected reward of each S ∈ A 

is given by the set function V : A → R. For each St ∈ A ,the reward is independently drawn from a fixed but unknown distribution DSt with mean We consider contribution-based fairness, where each arm must be selected in proportion to the value it contributes to the total reward. Since only Vt(St) is observed, we must first de-fine and estimate each arm’s contribution to the overall reward. For this, we turn to the Shapley value, a solution concept from cooperative game theory, and propose an adaptation suitable for the BCMAB-FBF setting, which we describe next. 

3.1 K-Shapley Value 

Cooperative game theory studies a setting where a group of players collaborate to achieve a collective benefit. Such in-teractions are modeled through a coalitional game [ Narahari, 2014 ]. Formally, let [M ] denote the set of all players, and let V : 2 [M ] → R be a valuation function that assigns a real-valued payoff to every possible coalition S ⊆ [M ], with the convention that V (∅) = 0 . The pair ([ M ], V ) defines a coalitional game, where V (S) represents the total reward achievable by coalition S when its members act together, inde-pendent of those outside the coalition. Within this coalitional framework, the Shapley value provides a fair and principled method to distribute the total payoff of the game among par-ticipating agents. ## 4 Proposed Approach 

One possibility for achieving meritocratic fairness is to first estimate the arms’ Shapley values and then select the arms in proportion to these values. We call this approach, Meritocratic Uniform Random Sampling (MURaS), which is presented in Appendix B.1. 

Theorem 2 (MURaS Fairness Regret) . With Shapley values being bounded in [0 , 1] , the cumulative fairness regret of the MURaS algorithm satisfies F R T = ˜O T 4/5KM  .

When valuation function is known, K-Shapley value can be approximated using Monte-Carlo approximation. However, in our setting, only noisy observations of valuation functions are obtained via bandit feedback for a selected set. Therefore, to bound the regret, we first show that the difference between K-Shapley values and estimated K-Shapley values are bounded by the sum of two sampling errors, one arising due to Monte-Carlo approximation and the other arising due to sampling the valuation function repeatedly for a given set. Then, we get the regret of O(T 4/5) by appropriately setting the sampling parameters. The detailed proof is provided in Appendix B.2. The resulting regret is higher than that of exploration-separated MAB algorithms in the literature [Chen et al. , 2024 ],as our approach requires handling two noises. We next present K-SVFair-FBF which combines exploration with exploita-tion and achieves the fairness regret of O(T 3/4), which is closer to the best achievable regret of O(T 2/3) in full bandit feedback[Nie et al. , 2022].

## 5 Experimental Analysis 

To evaluate the effectiveness of K-SVFair-FBF, we conduct experiments on two representative real-world applications: 

Federated Learning (FL) and Social Influence Maximization 

(SIM). In FL, a central server collaboratively trains a global model across distributed clients, each holding private data. Selecting all clients at every round is impractical due to com-munication costs and non-IID data distributions, leading to biased participation when only accuracy-driven criteria are used. We model each client as a base arm and the selected subset as a super-arm, with the global model accuracy after aggregation serving as the observed full-bandit reward. Our fairness-aware selection adjusts client participation probabil-ities based on client’s merit, promoting balanced participa-tion while preserving model accuracy. In the SIM setting, each node in a social network acts as a potential influencer, and the goal is to select a subset of nodes (super-arm) that maximizes expected diffusion under the Independent Cascade model. Conventional influence maximization strategies tend to repeatedly select high-centrality nodes, marginalizing low-centrality ones. However, K-SVFair-FBF assigns each client a Shapley-based merit capturing its marginal contribution, which leads to more diverse and fair seed selection. These two applications demonstrate that our fairness framework adapts well to different settings while preserving overall performance.

## 6 Conclusion 

In this work, we introduced a novel framework for ensuring meritocratic fairness in CMAB under full-bandit feedback. Unlike existing fairness models that rely on pre-defined quotas or exposure-based measures, our approach uses the Shapley value to estimate each arm’s contribution and selects arms in proportion to their merit. We proposed the K-SVFair-FBF algorithm, which adaptively estimates Shapley values while mitigating the effect of noise from stochastic feedback. Our theoretical analysis showed that K-SVFair-FBF achieves sub-linear fairness regret, ensuring that fairness improves consis-tently with time. The strength of our framework lies in its ability to balance fairness and efficiency even when individ-ual rewards are not directly observable. In future work, a promising theoretical direction is to establish lower bounds on fairness regret that jointly captures the stochastic and ap-proximation noises. We also plan to extend this framework to dynamic environments where arms may arrive or leave over time. Exploring fairness trade-offs in non-stationary settings is another promising direction. ## B MURas Algorithm and Regret Proof 

B.1 MURaS: Meritocratic Uniform Random Sampling 

We start with a naive approach, Uniform Random Sampling (URS), which approximates the Shapley values by sampling techniques. Once it has learnt the Shapley values accurately enough, it employs a meritocratic fairness policy on the learnt Shapley values. The details are described in Algorithm 3. MURaS essentially learns the Shapley value estimates of each agent via a R randomly generated permutation. Since rewards are stochastic, relying on a single evaluation for a permutation can lead to unstable estimates. To mitigate this, the estimated Shapley value for each permutation is calculated via sampling the valuations 

L times. Once the Shapley values are estimated, the arms are selected as per the policy given in the definition of Meritocratic fairness, on the basis of estimated Shapley values. Algorithm 3 ensures fairness in the following way. First, for the initial rounds, every arm has an equal chance of being selected due to uniform sampling, and once the Shapley values are closely approximated, the algorithm returns a policy that is meritocratic fair. URS requires O(L · R · M ) to find the approximated Shapley values. This scales polynomially in K, making it tractable compared to the exponential cost of the exact Shapley value computation. ## C Experimental 

C.1 Baseline Algorithms for Federated Learning 

• CS-UCB [Xia et al. , 2020 ] : The CS-UCB algorithm models client selection in FL as a MAB problem, where an upper confidence bound (UCB) strategy with virtual queues is used to enforce fairness across clients. In CS-UCB, fairness is treated as a constraint, ensuring each client is selected at least in proportion to its dataset size, while prioritizing clients with faster responses or larger datasets. However, defining fairness based on dataset size is not good idea. A client may possess a large amount of data that is not relevant to the global learning objective. In such cases, prioritizing clients purely by dataset size can bias the global model, slow convergence, or even degrade overall performance. In contrast, our algorithm integrates fairness by using Shapley values to estimate the contribution of each client and then sampling proportionally to these contributions. • S-FedAvg [ Nagalapatti and Narayanam, 2021 ]: The work by [Nagalapatti and Narayanam, 2021 ] takes a different approach by identifying relevant and irrelevant clients based on gradient similarity and excluding those clients whose updates are harmful to the global model. Shapley-inspired metrics are used here primarily as a filtering mechanism, with client selection becoming deterministic and biased toward a subset of relevant clients. While effective in removing noisy or adversarial clients, this approach may completely eliminate certain clients from participation, potentially leading to unfairness. Specifically, S-FedAvg models federated learning as a cooperative game where each client’s gradient update is treated as a player, and computes its Shapley value to quantify contribution toward global performance. These values are then used by the central server to update client relevance scores and probabilistically select the most relevant clients in subsequent rounds. Our method differs in that it employs Shapley values as a dynamic allocation tool—rather than excluding clients, we give each one a probabilistic selection chance proportional to its merit. Moreover, their framework does not use bandits and therefore does not explore client stochasticity, while ours balances exploration and exploitation under uncertainty, ensuring both fairness and performance guarantees. • ShapFed [Tastan et al. , 2024 ]: The work [Tastan et al. , 2024 ] introduces a Shapley value based framework for evaluating client contributions in FL. Specifically, it defines class-specific Shapley values (CSSVs) by comparing gradient alignments on the last-layer parameters, which allows measuring how much each client contributes to different classes. Based on these values, they propose ShapFed-WA for weighted aggregation, ensuring that clients with higher contributions have greater influence in model updates. However, this approach assumes that all clients participate in every round. It does not address the practical constraint of subset client selection, which is critical in large scale federated systems with communication limits. Moreover, their method does not use a MAB framework. In contrast, our SVFair-CMAB algorithm incorporates both meritocratic fairness and bandit-based exploration, selecting only a subset of clients each round in proportion to their estimated Shapley-based merit. • ShapleyFL [Sun et al. , 2023 ] : The ShapleyFL framework computes surrogate federated Shapley values to weight client updates during aggregation. Clients that historically contribute more receive higher weights, and importance sampling is introduced to reduce communication costs. While this improves fairness in update weighting, ShapleyFL does not fundamentally change client selection, the weighting only applies once clients are chosen, and selection itself is not stochastic or fairness-aware. Our method differs by embedding Shapley value estimation directly into a bandit-based selection policy, ensuring that clients are sampled proportionally to their merit. • BSFL [Ami et al. , 2025 ] : The work by [ Ami et al. , 2025 ] proposes a MAB framework for client selection, focusing on minimizing training latency while maintaining model generalization. Their algorithm BSFL, uses a time-varying reward function that balances the trade-off between selecting faster clients to reduce iteration time and ensuring diversity of clients to improve generalization. They design a UCB-based method along with a simulated annealing approach to reduce the complexity of combinatorial client selection. Importantly, BSFL emphasizes system efficiency (latency and generalization) but does not explicitly address fairness across clients.In contrast, SVFair-CMAB algorithm models client selection as a fair combinatorial bandit problem. While BSFL optimizes for generalization and latency, our approach integrates Shapley value-based fairness into the selection process itself. This ensures that clients are chosen not just for speed or generalization, but in proportion to their true contributions. 

C.2 Dataset 

Synthetic Dataset: 

We begin with experiments with a synthetic dataset designed to evaluate the performance of our framework. We considered 20 arms in total, with a selection size of 5 arms per round. The experiments were run for 10,000 rounds, and the Shapley values were approximated using 500 Monte Carlo samples. The mean rewards of the arms were predefined to introduce diversity in expected contributions. These values ranged from 0.2 to 0.95, covering both low and high reward arms. To model uncertainty, each arm was associated with Gaussian noise, with standard deviations ranging from 0.1 to 0.4. Thus, the instantaneous reward of each arm at every round was drawn from a normal distribution with the specified mean and variance. This setup ensures variation in both expected rewards and noise levels, creating a challenging environment to test the fairness and efficiency of our algorithm. Furthermore, the synthetic experiments are conducted using a submodular reward function. Specifically, we model the reward as a monotone submodular function of the selected arms to align with prior CMAB literature [Nie et al. , 2022]. 

Real-World Dataset for FL: 

We used the CIFAR-10 dataset in a FL setup. The dataset contains 50,000 training images and 10,000 test images, distributed across 10 classes. To simulate a federated environment, we divided the dataset among 100 clients. The partitioning was done in a non-IID manner. To achieve this, we used the Dirichlet distribution with parameter α = 0 .2. This choice introduces a high level of heterogeneity, where many clients primarily receive data from only a few classes. For the learning model, we used VGG-16. The training setup used SGD with momentum of 0.9, a learning rate of 0.01 (with decay), categorical cross-entropy as the loss function and batch size of 64. We conduct experiments over a range of T = 1000 federated rounds with learning rate η = 0 .01 

and local epochs E = 10 .Real-World Dataset for Social Influence Maximization: 

For the influence maximization experiments, we used a subset of the Facebook network introduced by [ Leskovec and Mcauley, 2012 ]. The same dataset has also been used by [ Nie et al. , 2022; Fourati et al. , 2024 ] in their work, which further demonstrates its suitability for studying influence spread in social networks. Since the original network is vast and computationally expensive to handle, we applied the community detection algorithm proposed by [ Guillaume, 2008 ], which identified a community consisting of 534 nodes and 8158 edges. In order to study how influence spreads, a diffusion process is required. We use the independent cascade diffusion process, which works as follows: when a node becomes active at a given step, it independently attempts to activate each of its currently inactive neighbours in the following step, with a fixed activation probability of 0.1 on all edges.

C.4 Ablation Study on Parameters L and R

To analyze the sensitivity of our approach to the Monte Carlo and smoothing parameters, we conduct an ablation study by varying the number of Monte Carlo permutations ( R) and the number of repeated evaluations per permutation ( L). Specifically, we consider four settings: (i) R = 1000 , L = 400 ; (ii) R = 500 , L = 100 ; (iii) R = 500 , L = 200 ; and (iv) R = 250 , L = 50 ,see Figures 3, 4, and 5. The results in Figures 3, 4, and 5 show that increasing R and L improves the stability of the estimated 

K-Shapley values, and hence meritocratic fairness, but at the cost of higher computational overhead. The configuration R = 500 ,

L = 200 provides the best trade-off between performance and efficiency, yielding stable convergence and fairness consistency across all datasets. Lower settings (e.g., R = 250 , L = 50 ) introduce more stochastic noise in the estimation, while excessively high values (e.g., R = 1000 , L = 400 ) offer only marginal performance gains relative to the additional computation time. Hence, we adopt R = 500 and L = 200 in all experiments, as it achieves robust performance with reasonable computational cost. 

C.5 Global Model Accuracy 

Accuracy in FL: This experiment measures the accuracy performance of the global model in the FL system. We restrict accuracy evaluation to the FL setup because, unlike the synthetic and social influence experiments that works on abstract reward functions to study fairness behavior, FL includes a predictive model where global accuracy reflects learning performance. FigureFigures 6 shows that K-SVFair-FBF achieves accuracy comparable to, or better than, existing baselines. This shows that promoting fairness among clients does not harm the overall learning performance. The improvement occurs because the algorithm ensures that even clients with diverse or smaller data contributions are included during training, leading to a more generalized global model. The baselines that over-select a few high-performing clients achieve higher early accuracy but fail to maintain fairness, resulting in bias and reduced overall model robustness. We report results up to 1,000 rounds, as the global model converges within this horizon and additional rounds do not yield further accuracy improvements.
