Abstract 

Ensuring that AI agents behave safely and ben-eficially when interacting with other parties has emerged as one of the central challenges of modern AI safety. While mechanism design, as the theory of designing rules to align individ-ual and collective objectives, can incentivize cooperative behavior, it is still an open question whether it alone is sufficient to maximize LLM agents’ social welfare. This work proves that the answer is negative: drawing from incom-plete contract theory, we formally show that when contracts cannot distinguish all relevant future contingencies, there is a strictly positive welfare loss that no realistic mechanism can eliminate. We show that prosocial agents, who weigh others’ welfare alongside their own, can close this gap and achieve outcomes that are socially superior and individually beneficial. Experimentally, we show that in multi-agent resource-allocation environments and canoni-cal social dilemmas where agents are powered by large language models, prosociality is ben-eficial. The implication for AI safety is clear: to enable cooperative interactions at scale, de-signing adequate mechanisms is not sufficient; agents must be built to be intrinsically proso-cial. 1

1 Introduction 

As AI agents become more autonomous, they will increasingly act as participants in shared environ-ments. They may negotiate service agreements, act on behalf of organizations and users, allocate shared resources, maintain common codebases, trade information, or execute collaborative projects with minimal human oversight (Amayuelas et al., 2025; PricewaterhouseCoopers, 2025; Roy and Ku-mar Singh, 2026). In these settings, the central

question is not simply whether each individual agent is aligned with its user. The harder ques-tion is whether many agents, each pursuing its own goals, can reliably cooperate in a shared world. However, the trustworthiness of such systems is not guaranteed because ensuring safe and benefi-cial outcomes across networks of interacting AI agents remains poorly understood (Afroogh et al., 2024). Human societies have long faced versions of this problem. We use all kinds of strategies (e.g., con-tracts, rules, incentives, audits, penalties, and in-stitutions) to bridge common ground among peo-ple and organizations. In the early 1960s, well before the rise of AI agents, mechanism design theory (Hurwicz, 1973, 1977; Maskin, 2008; My-erson, 1981) emerged from economics and game theory to address how rules and incentives can be structured such that self-interested agents produce socially desirable outcomes. A growing body of work applies mechanism de-sign principles to multi-agent AI systems, with the aim of aligning individual incentives with col-lective welfare (Dafoe et al., 2021; Dütting et al., 2024b; Guresti et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2022). We agree that well-designed mechanisms are nec-essary. However, rules possess fundamental limits, especially in complex open-ended environments. Classical mechanism design assumes that all rele-vant variables can be specified and enforced so that every action can be observed, outcomes verified, and contingencies expressed within the mechanism itself. But it is impossible to account for every rele-vant future state in the face of uncertainty (Macneil, 1974). In this paper, we argue that mechanism design alone is insufficient to recover the most so-cially optimal outcome. We formalize this limitation using the theory of incomplete contracts (Anderlini and Felli, 1994;

Hart and Moore, 1999; Hart, 1988), developed by Grossman and Hart, which introduces the concept of incompleteness. We define incontractible cells 

as sets of future states that a mechanism cannot distinguish efficiently, even though the welfare-maximising action differs across them. When such cells arise in social dilemma settings, we show that there is a strictly positive cooperation gap with an expected welfare loss that no realistic mechanism operating within the given contractual language can eliminate (Theorem 6, Corollary 7). This gap persists even when optimal, welfare-maximising mechanisms are implemented in the specification language. Consider the example of two firms deploying AI agents to jointly execute a software development project. The agents can invest high ( ) or low ( )effort in the project. Agents can also defect ( )by not contributing while reaping the benefits of the development. When agents’ efforts cannot be observed or enforced, the resulting payoff structure resembles the top panel of Figure 1. A contract can rule out outright defection, making basic co-operation enforceable. However, agents may still converge on a suboptimal equilibrium akin to a Prisoner’s Dilemma, shown in red in the bottom panel of Figure 1. The first-best outcome, shown in green, becomes achievable only when contracts are paired with prosocial agents. This work addresses the intrinsic limitations of con-tracts by modeling prosociality through numeri-cal preferences λ, where each agent places a pos-itive weight on others’ welfare alongside its own. Under prosocial preferences, agents cooperate be-cause each agent internalizes the cost that defection would impose on others, not because cooperation serves their self-interest. We show that prosociality improves social and in-dividual welfare. In strict social dilemma games, prosociality is individually beneficial to every agent when each agent’s payoff is strictly higher than the best self-interested equilibrium (Theorem 9). Intro-ducing cooperative preferences also cannot desta-bilize existing efficient equilibria (Theorem A.3), providing a safety guarantee for system designers considering the adoption of prosocial agents. 

Main Result 

There are contexts where prosocial agents always outperform self-interested agents, re-gardless of the quality of the mechanisms implemented. We empirically validate these theoretical results on GovSim (Piatti et al., 2024), an established bench-mark for multi-agent LLM commons-governance simulation, and GT-HarmBench, a benchmark of game-theoretic AI safety scenarios (Cobben et al., 2026). In commons-governance simulations, proso-cial agents achieve higher collective gains, a greater survival rate, and lower resource over-exploitation than their selfish counterparts across all contract-ing regimes and models tested. In safety games, prosocial agents strictly outperform selfish agents in incontractible regimes, even with optimal mecha-nism design. By varying the prosociality of agents, the type of contract, and the incontractibility of the game environment across multiple frontier LLMs, we find that while contracts incentivize cooperation, prosociality delivers additional gains that cannot be reached with contracts alone, consistent with our theoretical results. Our results have three practical implications for the design and deployment of multi-agent AI sys-tems. First, our impossibility result implies that external incentive structures alone have a provable ceiling on the welfare they can guarantee. When environments are complex enough that contracts are inevitably incomplete, agents’ intrinsic values become a requirement for cooperative behavior and not an optional add-on. Second, our findings pro-vide formal motivation for building intrinsically prosocial AI agents and for developing verifica-tion methods that certify an agent’s cooperative-ness to counterparties. Without such certification, prosocial agents are exploitable by selfish ones, and welfare gains cannot be realized. Lastly, each agent’s prosociality value is a measurable design parameter: system designers can compute the min-imum prosociality required for a given interaction structure, rather than treating cooperativeness as a binary or philosophical choice. 

Contributions. We make four contributions: (1) 

Incomplete contracts in mechanism design : we offer a formalization of contractual incompleteness as a partition over future states and highlight its rel-evance to the algorithmic mechanism design com-munity, where complete contractibility is typically assumed. (2) The cooperation gap : we prove that under incontractible cells with social dilemma structure, no realistic mechanism can eliminate the welfare loss from self-interested play (Theorem 6). (3) Empirical validation: We demonstrate these effects in multi-agent LLM simulations across fron-tier models, providing evidence that the theoretical cooperation gap manifests in practice. (4) Con-tracting ability: We test the ability of LLMs to coordinate on contracts and show that while they improve over the baseline, current LLMs still have big gaps over the theoretically optimal possible contract.

Automated mechanism design. Recent work studies network learning mechanism designs au-tomatically (Liu et al., 2025), compared to pre-vious non-interpretable methods (Dütting et al., 2024a; Wang et al., 2024) and LLMs’ ability to write code, seen as a way to implement such mech-anisms (Jiang et al., 2026) or even as code simula-tors (Malfa et al., 2025) acting as the mechanism itself. Similar works study the effectiveness of mechanisms on coordinating LLM agents (Cobben et al., 2026; Tewolde et al., 2026). However, agents within these settings are assumed to be classically 

homo economicus actors, falling into the theoretical inefficiency described in this work. Prosocial behaviour. Assumptions on prosocial behaviours have been extensively studied in Game Theory (Andreoni, 1990; Apt and Schaefer, 2014; Fehr and Fischbacher, 2003; Fehr and Schmidt, 1999). Peysakhovich (Peysakhovich and Lerer, 2018) shows the benefit of prosociality over self-ishness in stag hunts using RL. Another line of research studies this phenomenon in multi-agent reinforcement learning settings, exploring how al-truistic properties can be learned when solving so-cial dilemmas (Haeri, 2021; Tennant et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2019). Classical routing games have analyzed an analogous tragedy of the commons setting (Chen and Kempe, 2008). Interestingly, Caragiannis (Caragiannis et al., 2010) finds simi-larly defined altruism in atomic congestion games to be provably harmful. Yet, atomic congestion games are not social dilemmas since there is no fixed notion of a cooperative or defective action, as the best action depends on what others are doing. 

Research gap. To the best of our knowledge, no prior work has jointly investigated prosociality with the incompleteness of contracts in multi-agent sys-tems. The closest application of the incompleteness principle is to AI Alignment (Hadfield-Menell and Hadfield, 2019), where they draw parallels from law and economics to understand the current prob-lem of AI Alignment.

4 Experimental Setup 

This section introduces our evaluation framework. In Section 4.1 we describe incontractible implemen-tations of our studied games, while in Sections 4.2 and 4.3 we detail our implementation of contract-ing mechanisms and prosociality preferences. We defer explanations of metrics to Appendix D and further details to Appendices E-I. 

4.1 Game Benchmarks TableGames. We extend the GT-HarmBench framework, a corpus of social-dilemma scenar-ios (Cobben et al., 2026), and run experiments on its only classical normal-form social dilem-mas, Prisoner’s Dilemma and Stag Hunt, which we collectively call TableGames. We test across two game variants: (i) 2 ×2 games in their original form, and (ii) 4 ×4 moral hazard extensions (Holm-strÃ¶m, 1979), in which each base action a ∈{C, D } is paired with an unobservable effort level e ∈ { H, L }, giving each player the action set 

Ai = {CH , C L, D H , D L}. This setup imposes incontractibility by introducing an unobservable effort dimension: the specification language P par-titions the state space Ω only by the realised base action profile, pooling both action and effort into a single contractible cell as in Figure 1. Appendix E fully characterises our payoff structures. 

GovSimContract. We implement GovSimCon-tract, an expansion of AgentElect (Faulkner et al., 2026), in which n agents repeatedly fish for up to 

T months from a shared pond that regenerates at rate ρ per month, with initial stock and maximum capacity h(0) . We test across two regimes: (i) de-terministic regeneration, where ρ is fixed–since ρ

is known and deterministic, a perfectly sustainable extraction rule is, in principle, writable; and (ii) stochastic regeneration, where ρ is uniformly se-lected from a fixed interval, adding randomness that renders ρ incontractible. Parameter values are left to Appendix G. 

4.2 Contract Setup 

For each experiment, we examine three settings that vary the nature of how contracts are expressed. (i) No Contract refers to the same experi-mental setting of the original benchmark. For TableGames, we implement a no-communication one-shot evaluation, while for GovSimContract, we use the original "cheap talk" setting from AgentElect (Faulkner et al., 2026). (ii) In the Natural language (NL) Contract mod-els discuss and ratify, by majority vote, an informal, unenforced contract. We inform the models that contracts are not enforced and further guide the negotiation phase with exam-ples of classical mechanisms. In both groups, we prompt models to consider fines, reward transfers (Willis et al., 2024), and trusted-third party enforcement (Mohlin et al., 2023), while, for GovSimContract, we also allow temporary ostracization (Cinyabuguma et al., 2005; Maier-Rigaud et al., 2010) and classi-cal caps on the harvest (Ostrom, 1990) (more details in Appendix F). (iii) Code Contract shares the negotiation pipeline with NL Contract , but, this time, we inform the models that contracts will be enforced. For a ratified contract, an external "coding agent" implements the natural language specification as Python code, which executes in a sand-boxed environment. We provide examples of generated Python contracts in Appendix J. 

4.3 Implementation of λ-cooperativeness 

We implement two agent types under the λ-cooperativeness model: at every step of negotiation and at decision time (i) selfish agents ( λ = 0 ) are instructed via system prompt to maximise their own payoff, while (ii) prosocial (λ → ∞ ) agents are instructed to maximise the group’s total catch. These types correspond to the cooperative and individualistic tendencies in social value orienta-tion (Van Lange, 1999). Full prompt text is in Appendix G.4. 

5 Results and Discussion 

Our experimental evaluation answers the following research questions: RQ1: Does the predicted cooperation gap manifest in social dilemma problems? RQ2: How do contracts and prosociality interact? RQ3: Where and why LLM-generated contracts fall short of the welfare-max benchmark? RQ4: What residual behaviours persist under con-tracts? RQ5: Can we recover an implicit λ value from ob-served play? We evaluate five models: the novel GPT-5.4 family (GPT-5.4 and GPT-5.4-mini) (OpenAI, 2026), GPT-4o (OpenAI et al., 2024), Grok-4.1-Fast (xAI, 2025), and Gemma-4-31B (Farabet and Lacombe, 2026). See Appendix G.5 for infer-ence parameters. We use the same metrics as GT-HarmBench (Cobben et al., 2026) and GovSim (Pi-atti et al., 2024); for completeness, we report them in Appendix D. For TableGames, we test thirty scenarios per model, with five repetitions per sce-nario across two prosociality settings, two game variants (2 ×2 and 4 ×4), and three contract condi-tions, for both Prisoner’s Dilemma (PD) and Stag Hunt (SH); in total, we run 27 , 000 one-shot ex-periments. For GovSimContract, we conduct five seeded experiments per model across six prosocial-ity settings, two regeneration regimes (determinis-tic and stochastic), and three contract conditions; in total, we run 900 multi-round experiments. We report all of our results with 95% CI half-width bands and permutation test p-scores. Main results are reported in Table 1 and Figure 2; additional granular results are in Appendix K.

RQ3: Diverse range of contract failures. In Gov-SimContract’s deterministic case, the specification language is rich enough to express the welfare-maximizing contract, yet models systematically fail to draft it in two characteristic ways. First, over-prudency : agents write caps well below the sus-tainable harvesting rate, prioritising preservation of the lake over their own welfare. This is rational only in the infinite-horizon limit; within any finite-horizon setting, it is strictly dominated by greedy maximizing strategies. Empirically, over-prudency results in depressed efficiency under Code Contract (u = 0 .65 ± 0.13 versus u = 0 .84 ± 0.12 under deterministic No Contract at p0), accompanied by only a modest gain in survival ( m = 8 .33 ± 1.79 

versus m = 6 .19 ± 3.03 ); consequently, Code Con-tract does not improve R over the No Contract baseline in either regime ( −27 .7 ± 21 .5 determin-istic, p = 0 .019 ; −4.6 ± 24 .8 stochastic), unlike NL Contract. Second, failure to renegotiate : emer-gency contracts enforcing bare survival are not up-dated even when a more efficient contract would be sustainable given the current state. Among runs in which an active code contract is provably in-efficient, agents renegotiate only 12 .2% of the in-efficient rounds, curiously similar to studies on cultural transmission and social conditioning (Ja-cobs and Campbell, 1961), where behaviours are kept even when the danger has passed. Examples in Appendix K.4. By contrast, in TableGames models draft compe-tently, but are constrained by the specification lan-guage. Under 4 ×4 Code Contract, cooperation reaches 82 pp (PD) and 98 pp (SH), yet utilitarian accuracy stays at 40 pp and 32 pp ; hidden effort is incontractible, and welfare loss follows directly. 

RQ4: Residual behaviours take the form of effort-shirking and threshold violations. 6 Limitations and Future Work 

Mutual trust. One drawback of the proposed sim-ple prosociality check is that it is vulnerable to the presence of selfish agents: even a single defect-ing agent can cause collapse, and when it faces no competition from other exploiting agents, its gains relatively outpace those of other players. In human systems, Ostrom (Ostrom, 1990) shows that communities often provide local-level checks to make sure agents are compliant and punish any de-fectors. In contrast, for computer systems, LLM agents could, in principle, check the source code of other agents and verify if the prosociality factor is present before accepting a model within a commu-nity or starting collaboration. This idea is similar to the open-source game theory agenda (Critch et al., 2022; Kovaˇ rík et al., 2023) where computer systems have access to each other’s code and can verify properties; prosociality could be one of the properties that can be verified. 

Broader set of values. In our analysis, we fo-cused on a specific formulation of prosociality as moral value. Under the classical social welfare utility function, this is known to produce unfair out-comes (Rawls, 1971; Sen, 2017). Our experimental settings exclude such issues by design, since the best utilitarian outcome is also the fair and best individual outcome, but other scenarios might not satisfy this property. Future work could expand on definitions of social welfare and values other than prosociality. 

Implementation of prosociality. We imple-mented the prosociality of the agent simply using a prompt addition. While this could still be an easy and verifiable presence in a system’s prompt, proso-ciality could be embedded more deeply through weight updates within the model (Piche et al., 2025; Tennant et al., 2025). Another idea is to use mech-anistic interpretability methods to enhance a fea-ture vector that represents prosociality (Bayat et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2026). Future work could ex-plore both directions. 

Code fidelity. The compiled law may fail to fully realize the prose agreement; this is a known prob-lem since code needs precisely unambiguous and known states to act upon. One approach from which we drew inspiration for our experimental setting is the Ricardian Contract (Grigg, 2004): write a binding law in a human language (e.g., En-glish) and implement the provisions to the best of its effort. Appeals are still resolved at the court level based on the human text. We don’t have a measure of fidelity (i.e., does the code implement the human contract?) in our work, and have not found any in the literature; it is a known problem that current measures for similar fidelity tasks are “flawed" (Räisä et al., 2025). We leave this problem to future work, as it is not within the scope of the paper to analyze this aspect. Anecdotically, models seem good at translating such clauses. 

7 Broader Impact and Conclusions 

This work introduces and operationalizes a theo-retical notion of contract incompleteness between agents, and shows how intrinsic values can yield broad benefits in agent societies. In computer sci-ence, it offers directions for designing collaboration methods between agents. More broadly, it provides theoretical and empirical evidence that societies with certain values can outperform the classical 

homo economicus model under specific assump-tions. We do not foresee negative societal impacts, as this work contributes to AI safety. We characterize contract incompleteness, introduce the cooperation gap , an efficiency gap no standard mechanism with selfish agents can close, and val-idate the framework empirically in two settings. We observe strong gains from simple prosociality that contracts fail to achieve, identify limitations in models’ ability to write such contracts, and propose formal methods to reason about incontractibility. Together, these results highlight prosociality, and more broadly morality, as a key design lever for multi-agent systems, motivating further research in this direction. Acknowledgment

A.2 Relation to PoA and PoS 

The equilibrium component of the cooperation gap is closely related to two classical measures of equilib-rium inefficiency in algorithmic game theory, the price of anarchy and the price of stability (Anshelevich et al., 2008; Koutsoupias and Papadimitriou, 1999; Papadimitriou, 2001; Schulz and Moses, 2003), but it is not identical. PoA and PoS hold a single game fixed and compare the first-best welfare with the worst or best Nash equilibrium in that game; the gap there is read off the payoff matrix. However, in this work, the designer first optimizes within an admissible mechanism class M(P), each member of which yields a different induced game with its own equilibria, and the limitation comes from what that class can observe and condition on. Roughly: PoA and PoS answer "how bad is this game?", whereas the cooperation gap answers "what is the best game the designer can write inside the language P?". 

A.3 A Safety Guarantee for λ-Cooperation 

A natural concern when deploying λ-cooperative agents is whether the modified objective could destabilise efficient equilibria already supported under self-interested play. We rule this out for every λ ≥ 0.

Theorem 8 (Preservation of Efficient Equilibria) If a∗(ω) is socially efficient and is a Nash equilib-rium of G0(ω), then a∗(ω) remains a Nash equilibrium of Gλ(ω) for all λ ≥ 0.

Discussion. Because V λi = ui + λW keeps the agent’s own utility on the table, any unilateral deviation that is unprofitable under ui remains unprofitable once a non-negative welfare term is added at the efficient profile, where W is maximised. λ-cooperation can therefore only add new equilibria or remove 

inefficient ones; it never eliminates an efficient one. This complements Theorem 6: the impossibility result motivates the behavioural intervention, and Theorem 8 certifies that the intervention has no downside on the equilibria one most cares about. The guarantee presupposes comparable cooperativeness across agents; mixed-λ populations are studied empirically in Section 4.

C Sources of Incontractibility and Property Instantiations 

This appendix supports Property 5. Section C.1 catalogues the practical sources of incontractibility that justify the partition formalism. Section C.2 then exhibits a family of instantiations of the property, that is, concrete restrictions on the admissible mechanism class M(P) under which the property provably holds. 

C.1 Sources of Incontractibility 

The partition P in Definition 4 is agnostic about why a cell collapses several states. Here, we link classical reasons to our framework. The economic literature identifies several distinct sources of incontractibility: (i) non-verifiability , a variable may be observable by both parties yet unprovable to a court, leaving residual control rights unspecified (Hart and Moore, 1990); (ii) bounded rationality and writing costs , cognitive limits and the marginal cost of drafting clauses make it rational to leave low-probability events uncontracted (Anderlini and Felli, 2000; Williamson, 1975); (iii) computational limits , some states of nature are too complex to be described in any finite lan-guage (Anderlini and Felli, 1994); (iv) strategic incompleteness , parties may deliberately leave verifiable terms vague to preserve discre-tionary flexibility (Bernheim and Whinston, 1998); and (v) cost of enforcement , applying relative clauses is infeasible (Tirole, 1999). The impossibility result of Theorem 6 applies whenever any of these sources collapses an incontractible cell that carries a social dilemma. Maskin and Tirole (Maskin and Tirole, 1999) challenged the relevance of contractual incompleteness, arguing that if (i) parties can commit not to renegotiate, (ii) agents can probabilistically forecast future payoffs, and (iii) the optimal describable contract is welfare-neutral, then the indescribability of physical states does not constrain contracting: the optimal contract can be replicated by a mechanism defined in payoff space alone. However, the foundations of the non-renegotiation assumption, and of the auxiliary conditions sustaining the renegotiation-robust variant, have been contested on grounds of bounded rationality and complexity (Hart and Moore, 1999; Segal, 1999). To probe how these assumptions fare in a multi-agent LLM setting, we test our GovSimContract scenario under monthly recontracting. 

C.2 Instantiations of the Property 

We give several mild and widely studied conditions that each force Property 5 to hold in strict social dilemmas. Each is a concrete instantiation of the property: a restriction on the admissible class M(P)

under which the property is satisfied for every social dilemma in a positive-measure region. In principle, many other reasons lead to the same incontractibility property, such as bounded description complexity, robustness to prior ambiguity, anonymity, etc. We leave to future work the problem of taxonomising the mathematical structures of incontractibility. E Table Game Structure 

We generate 4 ×4 matrices for our TableGames experiments by sampling 2 ×2 base games (R, S, T, P ),lifting each one, and discarding any whose lift violates the Generalized Social Dilemma (Definition 3) or does not have mutual high-effort cooperation as its unique social-welfare optimum. In this Ap-pendix we derive the construction of the lifted 4 ×4 payoff matrix (Section E.1) and characterise the inputs for which the lift succeeds in the Prisoner’s Dilemma (Section E.2) and Stag Hunt (Section E.3) regimes. We open-source the generation script used to sample from the resulting parametric fami-lies at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/case-for-moral-agents/external/gt-harmbench/ scripts/analysis/estimate_lambda_pooled.py 

E.1 The lifting construction 

We pair each base action a ∈ { C, D } with an unobservable effort level e ∈ { H, L }, giving the 4-action set {CH , C L, D H , D L}. The base action specifies what an agent declares; the effort level specifies what they actually invest. Effort is incontractible: a mechanism observing only the joint outcome cannot tell 

(CH , C H ) from (CL, C L), even though the two profiles differ in welfare. 

Design goals. The lifted game must satisfy four properties: 1. Faithful reduction. If both players hold effort fixed at the same level, the resulting 2 ×2 sub-game inherits the strategic structure of the base game. 2. Unique welfare optimum. (CH , C H ) is the strict utilitarian and Rawlsian welfare maximiser. 3. Productivity collapse. (CL, C L) produces an outcome strictly worse than (CH , C H ) but no worse than mutual defection. Effort represents real investment, so this cell must reflect the welfare loss from no-one contributing. 4. Faithful equilibrium structure. The pure Nash equilibrium structure of the base game lifts in a way that preserves the qualitative strategic character of each game class. We are not aware of a standard parametric construction for this lifting problem. The closest relatives are additive cost-of-effort models in the principal-agent tradition and the selfishness-level construction of Apt (Apt and Schaefer, 2014), neither of which yields a 4 ×4 normal-form game directly comparable to the 2 ×2 base. We therefore design a parametric family from first principles, fixing the construction up to two scalar parameters, ε and η, derived below. 

Effort cost ε. Our construction requires a single parameter that simultaneously controls (i) how costly high effort is, (ii) how strongly the new effort dimension perturbs the base equilibrium structure, and (iii) how the qualitatively distinct effort interactions are penalised relative to one another. Thus every cell of the lifted matrix takes the form 

X − kε (minus an additional η at two specific cells, see below ),

where X ∈ { R, S, T, P } and k is a small non-negative integer determined by the cell’s strategic role. 

Scaling. We require ε = ( R − P )/d for some scalar d > 0. The dependence on a payoff difference is forced by translation invariance: shifting all base payoffs by a constant must shift the lift by the same constant. We use R − P specifically because it is the welfare gain of mutual cooperation over mutual defection, hence ε scales with the strength of the dilemma and vanishes when the base game degenerates (R = P ). 

Choice of denominator. The denominator d is fixed by the productivity-collapse goal (3). H Recovering λ values 

This appendix describes the statistical procedure used to estimate the prosociality parameter λ and inverse temperature β from observed joint actions in the evaluation data. We first state the estimation problem and explain why standard approaches are inadequate, then present the quantal response equilibrium (QRE) model, our pooled maximum likelihood estimator, and the game-clustered bootstrap used for inference. 

H.1 Estimation Problem 

For each model m, we observe play across a collection of two-player 2 ×2 normal-form games. Game s

has a payoff matrix Πs, where Πs[i, j ] = ( ur[i, j ], u c[i, j ]) gives the row and column player payoffs for joint action (i, j ). Across repeated plays of game s, we record ns[i, j ], the number of times joint action 

(i, j ) was observed. Each game is played by identical models in both row and column positions, so we estimate a single (λ, β ) pair shared across positions and across games. Because β enters the QRE as an inverse-utility coefficient, pooling across games with a single β requires payoffs on a common scale. We normalize each game’s payoff matrix by Ms = max i,j,k |uk[i, j ]| before fitting, placing all entries in [−1, 1] with at least one entry of magnitude exactly 1. This rescaling does not affect the QRE structure of any individual game, since logit choice is invariant to additive constants and rescales monotonically with β; we report estimated β values in inverse-normalized-payoff units throughout. Note that rescaling makes it so that raw-payoff β values from prior QRE applications (McKelvey and Palfrey, 1995) are not directly comparable. Our goal is to recover λ and β from the count matrices. Since λ enters play through the equilibrium of the 

λ-cooperative game Gλ, not through a direct mapping from payoffs to actions, a player’s optimal strategy depends on what the other player does, and that other player’s strategy also depends on λ. We therefore need an equilibrium model that links λ to the observed distribution over joint actions. A naive approach, computing the λ-cooperative best response for each observation and fitting λ to maximize agreement, fails for two reasons. First, models are stochastic: the same model may play different actions across repetitions of the same game, and we need a generative model of this noise. Second, best response is discontinuous in λ, producing a nonsmooth likelihood surface that makes optimization unreliable. QRE addresses both issues.

This model plays with very high decisiveness, and once β is large enough that logit choice is numerically indistinguishable from Nash best response, the likelihood becomes essentially flat in β, leaving the upper tail unidentified. Unlike the GPT-4o case, this saturation does not affect the substantive estimand: ˆλ is tightly identified at zero, and the magnitude of β in the saturated regime does not influence whether the model is classified as prosocial. Finally, GPT-5.4-Mini has ˆλ pinned at exactly zero with a degenerate bootstrap CI [0 , 0] : every resample selects λ = 0 as its MLE. We verified that the profile log-likelihood at the unconstrained optimum has a strictly negative gradient at λ = 0 (∂L/∂λ ≈ − 10 .5 by finite difference), indicating that the data favours 

λ < 0, i.e., the model’s play is more competitive than self-interest predicts under the QRE model. The constraint λ ≥ 0 binds, producing the boundary estimate. We classify GPT-5.4-Mini as self-interested along with the other near-zero-ˆλ models, since both characterise behaviour inconsistent with prosocial play. 

H.4 Confidence Intervals via Game-Clustered Bootstrap 

We construct confidence intervals using a game-clustered bootstrap with 300 boostrap resamples per model. Each resample draws |S| games uniformly at random with replacement from the observed set (where |S| is the number of unique games in the original data), preserving all ns[i, j ] counts within each drawn game. We re-estimate (λ, β ) on each resample using the same grid search procedure, and report the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles of the bootstrap distribution as the 95% confidence interval. Clustering at the game level reflects the design of the experiment: the sampling unit is the game, and our inferential target is generalization to a new draw of games from the same population, not the within-game sampling variability of plays at a fixed payoff matrix (Cameron et al., 2008).

Figures 5 and 6 contain heatmaps depicting the action selection frequencies for players in TableGames across game, game variant, prosociality, and model. Figure 5: Joint action selection frequencies for Prisoner’s Dilemma. Figure 6: Joint action selection frequencies for Stag Hunt. K.2 The Monitoring Effect 

We report preliminary results on a monitoring effect briefly noted in the main paper: LLM agents defect less frequently when they believe the contract will be enforced. In the main experiments, enforcement co-varies with contract type, leaving the two effects confounded. To isolate monitoring awareness, we hold the contract fixed and vary only the agents’ belief about whether it will be enforced, using the following prompt modifications: • Enforced (Code Contract ): “The constitution describes specific rules for the community. It will be enforced at its best effort.” • Unenforced (NL Contract ): “The constitution describes general rules of the community. It won’t be enforced.” We replicate this experiment across TableGames and GovSimContract: for TableGames, we run thirty scenarios (fifteen from each 2 ×2 game), each repeated five times, for each monitoring regime. For GovSimContract, we run five experiments in the deterministic all-selfish setting. Both experiments use Grok-4.1-Fast exclusively. We provide immutable contracts: 1. TableGames “Both players commit to choosing the cooperative action, the first action listed in your available actions.“ 

2. GovSimContract “The current community has agreed to catch at most 3 tons of fish per month per person.”

Takeaways. These results are based on a single model (Grok), so runs for additional models are needed before drawing statistically grounded conclusions about the pure monitoring effect. The magnitude of the gap nonetheless suggests that monitoring awareness materially shapes agent behavior independently of contract content. We view a fuller characterization of behavior under monitoring awareness as a promising direction for future work, closely connected to the literature on evaluation awareness (Needham et al., 2025), where similar belief-conditional behavioral shifts have been documented.
