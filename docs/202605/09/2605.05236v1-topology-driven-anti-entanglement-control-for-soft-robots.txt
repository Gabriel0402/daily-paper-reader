# Highlights 

## Topology-Driven Anti-Entanglement Control for Soft Robots 

Haoyang Le, Shengxuan Wang, Mohan Chen, Shuo Feng 

• In the context of working with anti-entanglement with multiple soft robots manipulators, we propose a topology-aware multi-agent reinforcement framework. With its combination of topological invariants, including the winding number and the representations of the braid group in our state space and reward system, we can directly and accurately quantify and provide early warnings of entanglement risks. 

• Detailed theoretical work and mathematical calculation illustrate the practicability and excellence of the introduction of topological invariants into any open modeling in the context of the presented computational system. The improved framework has better convergence and robustness. 

• A hierarchical agent design based on hierarchical topological dynamic control framework is built on a spatiotemporal basis. Dual experience replay combines rich topological inputs into a centralized buffer reducing policy and value network synergies. There is an in-built topological safety layer that pre-checks and holds back dangerous actions. 

• This approach has been shown through lengthy simulation experience to minimize entanglement incidents (entanglement probability of just 0.7% and high task completion of 96.8% and throughput of 5.2% robot arm idle rate). It has better safety, cooperative performance, and system stability as compared to the conventional motion planning algorithm and other baseline reinforcement learning strategies (28.5% higher than the basis solutions).

A B S T R A C T 

In the field of precision manufacturing in complex constrained environments, the role of soft robots is increas-ingly prominent, and the realization of anti-winding control based on multi-intelligent body reinforcement learning has become a research hotspot. One of the core problems at present is to coordinate multiple robots to complete the unwinding operation in a highly constrained environment. The existing distributed training framework faces some observability challenges in high-density barrier and unstable environments, resulting in poor learning results. This paper proposes a topology-driven Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (TD-MARL) framework to coordinate multi-robot systems to avoid entanglement. Specifically, the critical network adopts centralized learning, so that each intelligent body can perceive the strategies of other intelligent bodies by sharing the topological state, thus alleviating the training instability caused by complex interactions; eliminating the demand for communication resources between robots through distributed execution, Upgrade system reliability; the integrated topological security layer uses topological invariants to accurately assess and mitigate the risk of entanglement to avoid the strategy from falling into local difficulties. Finally, the full simulation experiments carried out in the real simulation environment show that the method is better than the current advanced deep reinforcement learning (DRL) method in terms of convergence and anti-winding effect.

## 2. Related Work 

The robotic entanglement challenge has been conventionally studied through three closely related research routes: geomet-ric motion planning avoiding spatial entanglement, topological state analysis, and multi-agent coordination learning. While each domain has made independent progress, solutions from these routes increasingly focus on addressing the overall, cumulative entanglement threat in the long run. 

2.1. Geometric Motion Planning and Soft Robot Control 

Most early research focused on geometric path planning and collision avoidance. Foundational frameworks like Multi-Agent Path Finding Sharon, Stern, Felner et al. (2015) constructed discrete algorithms to coordinate multiple agents, while dynamic approaches Solis, Motes, Sandström et al. (2021) integrated continuous dynamic models. In the domain of soft robot con-trol, methods have evolved from physics-based models utilizing Cosserat rod theory Trivedi et al. (2008); Rucker et al. (2010) and finite element methods to reduced-order approaches such as piecewise constant curvature approximation Webster and Jones (2010). More recently, reinforcement learning techniques have been effectively employed to handle the nonlinear dynamics of soft robots Lou, Wang, Xu, Liang and Zhou (2024); Mohammad and Bezzo (2025), with extensions to multi-robot systems Adil, Sakhrieh, Mounsef and Maalouf (2025). However, these methods predominantly concentrate on local geometric coordination and Euclidean space avoidance, considering instantaneous collision states while largely ignoring the cumulative risks imposed by global topological constraints and entanglement Li, Zhang, Sun et al. (2023). 

2.2. Topological State Analysis 

To address the limitations of geometric approaches, a sec-ondary research line adopted topological tools to assess and reason about global entanglement. Braid theory emerged as one of the first techniques Ye, Han, Ding et al. (2023b) for analyzing entanglement among paths in a global state space. Topological invariants such as linking numbers and the Gauss linking integral

Ricca (1998) provided accurate mathematical measurements to evaluate entanglement degree and complexity without requiring specific geometric parameterizations. With advances in compu-tational topology, entanglement can now be estimated in real time for applications including knot detection, deformation mon-itoring Qu, Wang, Cheng et al. (2024), knot tracking Zhou et al. (2024), and sensor network analysis Onder, Koc, Gokbulut et al. (2024); Kuskonmaz et al. (2024). Nevertheless, a significant gap remains: most topological analyses are conducted offline Zhou et al. (2024) or utilize entanglement information primarily for online monitoring Onder et al. (2024); Lei and Deng (2025), with topological data often treated as external constraints rather than being actively integrated into closed-loop control policies as feedback signals. 

2.3. Multi-Agent Coordination Learning 

Advances in Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (MARL) have established robust decentralized coordination frameworks. Algorithms such as MADDPG Lowe et al. (2017) and MAPPO Wang et al. (2022) address the non-stationarity problem through centralized training with decentralized execution (CTDE). Sub-sequent improvements including R-MAPPO Sá Barreto and Ste-fanov (2024) and HAPPO Kuba et al. (2022) have further en-hanced capabilities for temporal reasoning and heterogeneous collaboration. Safety-aware MARL strategies typically impose constraints through penalty functions or barrier methods Mo-hammad and Bezzo (2025); Zheng, Li, Yu et al. (2024b,a), while methods incorporating memory modules and hierarchical structures Guo, Ma, Xia et al. (2024) support improved long-term reasoning. However, these approaches predominantly focus on immediate collision hazards and lack effective mechanisms to account for the delayed, accumulative impact of entanglement evolution triggered by historical action sequences. Theoretical analyses further indicate that value-based MARL algorithms may lack convergence guarantees in general-sum games Perolat et al. (2022), raising concerns about their stability in practical deployment scenarios. 

2.4. Integrating Topological Awareness into MARL 

Researchers have recently begun to extend modern multi-agent learning frameworks with topological analysis. Prelimi-nary approaches have incorporated topological features as ad-ditional inputs to policy networks. While these methods have achieved certain improvements, they predominantly treat topol-ogy as an external observational feature that reflects system trajectories, without fully leveraging its causal influence for proactive decision-making Du, Liu, Moens, Liu, Ren, Wang, Chen and Zhang (2021). The central challenge of utilizing topo-logical feedback to actively influence robot behaviors and antic-ipate entanglement mitigation remains largely open Halverson and Ruehle (2025). In this paper, we generalize beyond these approaches by enabling deep perception of topological states and utilizing this awareness as intrinsic feedback within the MARL control policy, thereby facilitating anticipatory entanglement prevention rather than reactive response Sun, Hu and Zhang (2023). 

## 3. Problem Formulation 

3.1. Problem Definition 

Multi-Soft-Robot Topology-Driven Coordination (MSR-TAC) addresses the challenge of coordinating a team of 𝑁 continuum robotic arms in a shared workspace 𝐖 ⊂ ℝ3 to execute mainte-nance activities 𝐓 without inducing topological entanglement in constrained environments such as aircraft engine compartments and automotive chassis. This failure mode, characterized by global constraints and historical state dependencies, can lead to system deadlocks, mission failure, or mechanical damage, representing a critical obstacle to reliable multi-soft-robot de-ployment. The theoretical foundation is based on several core assump-tions common in soft robotics and multi-robot systems Trivedi et al. (2008); Wang and Chen (2022); Lowe et al. (2017): 1. Each continuum manipulator maintains its structural in-tegrity under deformation, with material properties con-sistent with continuum mechanics principles. 2. Task parameters are predefined with specific temporal dependencies modeled as a directed acyclic graph (DAG), where each task can be performed by any available robotic arm. 3. Communication networks enable real-time coordination between high-level schedulers and individual manipulator units. 4. Robust control strategies mitigate the impact of dynamic obstacles and prevent sudden drive device failures during operation 5. Highly reliable sensors support real-time shape recon-struction and positioning, while communication infras-tructure enables real-time computation of global topolog-ical states.

3.3.2. Constraint Interpretation 

The optimization procedure is subject to eight constraints guaranteeing the geometric and topological feasibility of the manipulation process: C1 ensure a minimum safety distance between the robotic arm and obstacles, with all nodes confined within the workspace; C2 limit the velocity of each node to guarantee dynamic stability of the system; C3-C5 restrict the bending curvature, torsional curvature, and total arc length to prevent structural failure and maintain geometric integrity; C6 

ensures tasks are executed in the order specified by the directed acyclic graph (DAG); C7-C8 guarantee that each task is assigned to only one robotic arm and that each robotic arm executes only one task at a time, avoiding resource conflicts. This integrated formulation enables real-time assessment of winding risk while providing accurate posture perception for collaborative control of multi-soft-robot systems in constrained operational environments, effectively addressing the limitations of traditional motion planning algorithms in handling topological entanglement phenomena. 

## 4. Methodology 

4.1. Algorithm Overview 

Our proposed Topology-Driven Multi-Agent MARL (TD-MARL) framework transitions from reactive collision avoid-ance to proactive entanglement management via hierarchical coordination and topological sensing. As illustrated in Fig. 2, the TD-MARL framework is an interconnected system of four interconnected layers that compose an end-to-end safety control loop: (1) a topological sensing layer that computes entanglement risks from evolving robot trajectories, (2) a dual-experience replay mechanism that facilitates safety-critical policy training, (3) a heterogeneous system of specialized agents, and (4) a safety intervention layer that prevents hazards before they occur. The execution workflow starts with the ongoing computation of topological invariants from evolving robot trajectories. The computed invariants are used for risk assessments that guide experience storage policies and modulate the behaviors of agents via safety interventions. By coordinating diverse agents, the heterogeneous system achieves dual objectives of task efficiency and topological safety in practice, forming a robust system that can run in complicated multi-robot manipulation scenarios. The key innovation of this framework lies in the dynamic concurrency control mechanism: based on real-time topological hazard assessment results, the system intelligently adjusts the excitation level of operating arms, enabling full-load operation in safe environments while reducing deployment scale when entanglement risks are detected. 5.2. Performance Analysis 

5.2.1. Entanglement Prevention Capability 

As shown in Table 4, TD-MARL can bring a big improve-ment in preventing entanglement. TD-MAPPO can reduce entan-glement times by 86.3% compared with MAPPO (0.7% vs 5.1%) and 93.3% compared with geometric methods (0.7% vs 10.4%). In addition, the safety intervention rate also can reflect the mech-anism of TD-MARL. TD-MAPPO only needs to be intervened at 7.5% times compared with 19.8-37.6% for baselines. It shows that TD-MARL is not a reactive collision avoidance method, but an anticipatory entanglement prevention method. When the complexity of the environment is increased from a simple configuration (4 operating arms and 4 targets) to a complex configuration (10 operating arms and 8 targets), the proposed method still maintains stable performance. In low-density restricted scenarios, the task success rate of TD-MAPPO reaches 99.95%, and the probability of winding is only 0.1%; even under the most severe conditions, the task completion rate of 96.8% is maintained, and the performance decline is significantly lower than that of the benchmark method. Traditional algorithms such as RRT* have a winding rate of more than 30% and a task success rate of less than 70% in medium and high complexity environments, and the proposed methods show better security and efficiency; even with advanced multi-intelligent algorithms such as standard MAPPO and HAPPO, the winding rate is usually between 5% and 15%, which is difficult to meet security deployment requirements in practical applications. This shows that in a multi-arm dense system, relying only on geometric information or the lack of topological perception in collabora-tive learning cannot effectively alleviate the risk of systematic entanglement. Importantly, the framework has excellent scalability: when the number of operating arms increases from 4 to 10, the per-formance decreases by only 3%, far lower than the 15%-25% decline of the benchmark method. This scalability is mainly due to the fact that the topological perception mechanism does not require precise topological calculation - by simplifying the braid representation and adopting real-time approximate calculation, the state update can be completed within a simulation step length of 10 milliseconds, ensuring that the method is suitable for real-time multi-intelligent systems.
