## Abstract 

Vision-based Reinforcement Learning (VRL) attempts to es-tablish associations between visual inputs and optimal ac-tions through interactions with the environment. Given the high-dimensional and complex nature of visual data, it be-comes essential to learn a policy based on high-quality state representation. To this end, existing VRL methods primar-ily rely on interaction-collected data, combined with self-supervised auxiliary tasks. However, two key challenges remain: limited data samples and a lack of task-relevant semantic constraints. To tackle these challenges, we pro-pose DGC, a method that Distills Guidance from Visual Language Models (VLMs) alongside self-supervised learn-ing into a Compact VRL agent. Notably, we leverage the state representation capabilities of VLMs, rather than their decision-making abilities. Within DGC, a novel prompting-reasoning pipeline is designed to convert historical obser-vations and actions into usable supervision signals, en-abling semantic understanding within the compact visual encoder. By leveraging these distilled semantic represen-tations, the VRL agent achieves significant improvements in sample efficiency. Extensive experiments on the Carla benchmark demonstrate our state-of-the-art performance.

However, SSL methods cannot provide additional se-mantic information, which limits their ability to capture meaningful relationships between objects in a scene and to generate representations directly relevant to decision-making. This challenge is mainly due to two factors: (1) the data used for learning is derived solely from the agent’s self-exploration within the environment, restricting it to specific scenes and resulting in limited data samples; and (2) there is a lack of task-specific semantic constraints, which are dif-ficult to incorporate into the VRL’s online learning process and would be labor-intensive to collect. Recently, pre-trained large Vision Language Models (VLMs) [1, 13, 16, 21, 28, 38] have achieved excellent per-formance in visual understanding and are highly effective in generating rich, task-specific representations that integrate common-sense knowledge [36, 48, 58]. These models, trained on vast amounts of visual and linguistic data, can capture deep semantic relationships among objects, scenes, and actions, as their large capacities broaden the range of potential solutions [5, 40]. However, when agents directly use these models’ outputs for policy fine-tuning (PFT) in VRL (as shown in Fig. 1b) or intermediate features for rep-resentation fine-tuning (RFT) (as shown in Fig. 1c), limi-tations arise. While these approaches work well in general scenarios, they can sometimes result in infeasible or irra-tional decisions and representations, especially in rare or specific contexts. Furthermore, RFT lacks prompts to gen-erate reasoning-based representations. As a result, the two approaches above are typically effective for simpler tasks. For example, as illustrated in Fig. 2a, these widely-used tasks often involve straightforward control signals and clear visual inputs. However, for more complex tasks, such as Carla driving control in Fig. 2b, which requires handling high-dimensional visual inputs and continuous control con-strained by traffic rules and ethics, these approaches exhibit suboptimal performance, as shown in Fig. 2c. Given the above analyses, our intuition is to distill the knowledge of large VLMs into the compact visual encoder of the VRL agent, rather than into its policy decoder (as shown in Fig. 1d). This strategy enables reasoning-based policies to build upon robust visual representations. To achieve this, we incorporate the common-sense reasoning and language-to-image referring capabilities of VLMs dur-ing the representation distillation process, which are then refined by SSL auxiliary tasks. The distilled representa-tion is finally mapped onto the policy through VRL. Specifi-cally, we take the following steps: (1) We introduce a novel prompting-reasoning pipeline that instructs VLMs to con-vert historical observations and actions into usable super-vision signals, i.e. , convolutional kernels and feature maps. These signals guide the learning of the compact visual en-coder. (2) To minimize the impact of noisy distillation, we incorporate two widely used, task-relevant auxiliary tasks in SSL [15] that refine the distillation process, enabling better adaptation to specific scenarios. (3) Finally, by leveraging these distilled semantic representations, the compact VRL agent can effectively learn policies using the TD loss. Note that DGC aims to employ VLMs to enhance state representation, while policy optimization is fully delegated to VRL. This offers two key advantages: (1) Large VLMs are inherently trained with a focus on visual perception, en-abling us to fully leverage their representational strengths to improve state information; (2) The decoupling of pol-icy learning from representation learning allows the policy to be optimized by VRL, enabling it to autonomously and continuously adapt to varying scenarios. In summary, our contributions are mainly three-fold: • We devise a novel approach to distilling knowledge from large VLMs into a compact RL agent, enhancing its se-mantic understanding. • We develop a new prompting-reasoning pipeline that in-tegrates VLMs to provide usable supervision signals for the compact visual encoder, coupled with task-relevant SSL tasks for refinement, thus facilitating learning effi-cient decision-making policy through TD loss. • We conduct extensive experiments on the Carla bench-mark to back up our superior policy performance and sample efficiency. 

## 2. Related Work 

Representation learning in VRL. Learning decision-making from complex, high-dimensional observations de-29535 mands a high representation capacity from visual encoders. To address this, many studies have proposed joint learn-ing approaches that integrate self-supervised learning with VRL [22, 23, 51, 57]. Early methods [46, 53, 55] mainly fo-cused on reconstruction tasks to ensure that the compressed representations of visual input in latent space were com-plete. However, such completeness does not always provide the relevant information necessary for the task and may lack distinctiveness. To overcome this, some studies [15, 23, 51] preserve Markov Decision Process (MDP) properties within the latent space to filter out task-irrelevant information and only retain relevant parts. Other works [20, 47, 57] learn in-variant representations by contrasting the bisimulation dis-tances between samples. However, the above existing meth-ods cannot provide additional semantic constraints, as it mainly relies on the distance measures between samples. 

Large Models (LMs) for VRL decision-making. To clar-ify our contributions, we categorized the existing research on how LMs support VRL in decision-making into four classes: (1) LMs as Executors or Teachers use embed-ded common-sense knowledge to guide policy generation. Within this approach, some methods [31, 41, 60] directly apply the output of LMs as executable actions, while oth-ers [29, 49, 50, 61] treat it as supervision for PFT. (2) LMs as Representation Extractor [6] either directly use the pre-trained VLM for feature extraction [34, 35], or integrate with VRL for RFT to learn how to adapt to environmental changes [9, 37]. (3) LMs as Planners [4, 43, 60, 61] utilize LMs for high-level task breakdown and planning, leaving low-level actions to subsequent policy optimization algo-rithms. (4) LMs as Generalizable Policy [3, 41] take advan-tage of LMs’ generalization abilities by training on datasets containing large-scale observation-action pairs. However, this approach requires extensive labeled data, which is of-ten impractical. The above approaches are not mutually ex-clusive and may, in fact, intersect or overlap in their appli-cation. In this paper, we mainly focus on the second cate-gory and present a novel approach for generating reasoning-based representations 

Integration of VLMs and VRL. VLMs exhibit common-sense reasoning in general scenarios, but they may produce errors or hallucinations in specific contexts [17]. On the other hand, VRL suffers from low sample efficiency, re-quiring large amounts of data for effective policy learn-ing; however, it can autonomously optimize through ex-ploration. Therefore, the combination of VLM and VRL is typically complementary, with studies come in two ar-eas: (1) VRL-enhanced VLM , known as Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback [33, 44], enables VLMs to better align with specific objectives or human prefer-ences. (2) VLM-enhanced VRL [6] utilizes the common-sense knowledge priors embedded in VLMs to augment VRL. VLM-enhanced VRL can serve multiple roles:a pol-icy teacher [10, 27, 39, 42, 42], a representation extrac-tor [9, 34, 35, 37], a reward designer [30], and an assistor in exploration [12]. In our approach, we primarily lever-age the reasoning and representation capabilities of VLMs, rather than their decision-making abilities, to distill them into a VRL agent. 

## 3. Preliminaries 

In the VRL framework, the problem is typically modelled as a Partially Observable Markov Decision Process (POMDP), represented by the tuple M =< O, S, A, P, R, γ > , where 

O defines the space of visual observations, S is the set of states, and A is the action space available to the agent. Fol-lowing the standard POMDP model [15, 23, 32, 51], the interaction process can be summarized as follows: (i) the agent receives the environment state st by stacking recent visual observations, e.g., {ot, o t−1, o t−2}; (ii) the agent samples an action at ∈ A from a stochastic policy π(at|st);and (iii) the agent transitions to the next state st+1 ∼P(st+1 |st, a t) and receives a reward rt+1 ∼ R (st, a t). The goal of VRL is to learn an optimal policy π∗ that maximizes the expected cumulative reward over time. The SAC algorithm [18, 19] is a popular RL baseline that aims to maximize a reward signal while promoting policy entropy to better balance the trade-off between exploitation and exploration. ## 4. Methodology 

4.1. Overview 

In our DGC workflow, shown in Fig. 3, the training phase utilizes large VLMs as priors to provide supervision sig-nals for distilling knowledge into a compact RL agent. VLMs in DGC include reasoning models, such as QWen-VL-Chat [2] and ChatGPT-4o [1], as well as linguistic-image referring models, like the CLIP-series [21, 48]. The reasoning model initiates a prompting-reasoning process by taking as input the latest three consecutive image observa-tions, st := {ot−2, o t−1, o t}, along with their associated historical actions, {at−2, a t−1}. This model infers a se-mantic category from the observations, which directly in-fluences the subsequent decision, and passes this category to the image-language referring model. The pairing model then generates a convolution kernel based on the seman-tic text, producing a pixel-level saliency feature map that highlights relevant regions within the image observations. Together, these two outputs provide the supervision signals required for the distillation process in the VRL agent. The VRL agent, using only the three historical image ob-servations st as input, encodes these into visual latent fea-tures via a compact visual encoder ΦRL enc , aligning them with the supervisory signals from the upper level. Task-relevant refinement heads are incorporated to minimize the noise introduced during distillation. The resulting distilled representation zt is then used to train the policy decoder 

ΦRL dec , enabling it to learn low-level actions at through TD loss. During testing, the upper-level large VLMs can be discarded, ensuring efficient operational performance. 

4.2. Prompting-reasoning pipeline 

Fine-tuning a VLM without labelled data in an online RL setting can be challenging, particularly for complex plan-ning tasks where reasoning accuracy is limited. Conse-quently, using prompts for in-context learning becomes an essential strategy [5, 60]. These prompts should offer task definitions, common sense rules, and chain-of-thought guidance to enable the model to tackle complex tasks effec-tively without the need for additional training. The predefined prompts, illustrated in Fig. 4a, consist of three main components: (1) a task definition prompt ,which specifies the VLM’s role during inference and stan-dardizes the input and output formats, including image in-puts, action data, and chain-of-thought reasoning outputs; (2) a common sense prompt , which incorporates general safety rules for driving; and (3) a chain-of-thought prompt 

that guides the VLM through step-by-step scene under-standing, future scenario prediction, prioritization of atten-tion, and the generation of semantic categories for an area of interest. Once the VLM is provided with these predefined prompts, denoted as P, it receives three consecutive obser-vation – ot, ot−1, and ot−2 – along with the textual input “actions: at−1 and at−2.” Based on this input, the VLM outputs the corresponding semantic category, denoted as

## 5. Experiments 

5.1. Experimental setup 

We utilize the widely-used Carla benchmark [22, 23, 51, 57], which is developed in 3D physical environments and provide rich, complex visual observations. These obser-vations require a powerful visual encoder to filter and integrate task-relevant information for effective decision-making. The Carla benchmark consists of three scenarios: Jaywalk (#JW), Highbeam (#HB), and Highway (#HW), as shown in Fig. 5. Specifically, in the Jaywalk scenario, the ego-vehicle encounters pedestrians randomly crossing the road; in the Highbeam scenario, the ego-vehicle encoun-ters cyclists while dealing with varying lighting conditions caused by high beams from vehicles in the opposite lane; and in the Highway scenario, the ego-vehicle is surrounded by 20 vehicles traveling at different speeds. The goal is to train the VRL agent to drive the ego-vehicle as far as pos-sible within 500 steps while avoiding collisions. We report the mean and variance of episode return (ER) and driving distance (D) in meters across 20 episodes with three differ-ent seeds.

In our method, we mainly selected two VLMs: QWen-VL-Chat [2] for common-sense reasoning and CLIP se-ries [48] for linguistic-image referring. The visual encoder of the VRL agent comprises four layers of CNNs with ReLU non-linearity. The output feature map ft and fea-ture vector zt have dimensions of 6 × 6 × 256 and 512 ,respectively. More implementation details can be found in the supplementary material. 

5.2. Comparison with state-of-the-art methods 

We compared our DGC with extensive state-of-the-art (SOTA) methods, categorized into four classes: (i) a VRL baseline (SAC [18, 19]), (ii) a data augmentation-based VRL method (DrQ [52]), (iii) SSL for VRL methods, including DeepMDP [15], CURL [26], DBC [57], and MLR [55], and (iv) VLM-based methods, including Ex-ecutor, PFT (policy fine-tuning), and RFT (representation fine-tuning). In the VLM-based Executor, we modify the fourth item in the Chain-of-Thought Prompt (Fig. 4) for in-put to QWen-VL-Chat to generate control signals. ## 6. Conclusion 

In this paper, we explore the common-sense reasoning and language-to-referring abilities of VLMs to support repre-sentation distillation into a compact VRL agent’s visual encoder. This distilled representation is further refined through SSL auxiliary tasks and leveraged to learn the pol-icy using the TD loss inherent in VRL. Extensive exper-iments back up our superiority in both sample efficiency and policy performance.
