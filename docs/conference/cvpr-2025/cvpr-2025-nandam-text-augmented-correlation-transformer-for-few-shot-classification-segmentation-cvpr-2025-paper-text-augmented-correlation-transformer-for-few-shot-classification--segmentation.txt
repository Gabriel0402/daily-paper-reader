# Abstract 

Foundation models like CLIP and ALIGN have transformed few-shot and zero-shot vision applications by fusing visual and textual data, yet the integrative few-shot classifica-tion and segmentation (FS-CS) task primarily leverages vi-sual cues, overlooking the potential of textual support. In FS-CS scenarios, ambiguous object boundaries and over-lapping classes often hinder model performance, as lim-ited visual data struggles to fully capture high-level seman-tics. To bridge this gap, we present a novel multi-modal FS-CS framework that integrates textual cues into support data, facilitating enhanced semantic disambiguation and fine-grained segmentation. Our approach first investigates the unique contributions of exclusive text-based support, us-ing only class labels to achieve FS-CS. This strategy alone achieves performance competitive with vision-only methods on FS-CS tasks, underscoring the power of textual cues in few-shot learning. Building on this, we introduce a dual-modal prediction mechanism that synthesizes insights from both textual and visual support sets, yielding robust multi-modal predictions. This integration significantly elevates FS-CS performance, with classification and segmentation improvements of +3.7/6.6% (1-way 1-shot) and +8.0/6.5% (2-way 1-shot) on COCO-20 i, and +2.2/3.8% (1-way 1-shot) and +4.3/4.0% (2-way 1-shot) on Pascal-5i. Addi-tionally, in weakly supervised FS-CS settings, our method surpasses visual-only benchmarks using textual support ex-clusively, further enhanced by our dual-modal predictions. By rethinking the role of text in FS-CS, our work estab-lishes new benchmarks for multi-modal few-shot learning and demonstrates the efficacy of textual cues for improving model generalization and segmentation accuracy.

This flexibility allows FS-CS to handle diverse sce-narios, even though it may slightly underperform compared to traditional FS-C and FS-S on synthetic benchmarks [11]. While previous FS-CS methods [11, 12] have made sig-nificant progress, they predominantly rely on visual data. Recent advances in few-shot and zero-shot vision appli-cations, such as few-shot classification [32, 41, 42], few-shot segmentation (FSS) [24, 29], and zero-shot segmen-tation [4, 14, 43], have shown the benefits of incorporat-ing textual information from models like CLIP [20]. Prior works in FSS have used textual cues from CLIP [29, 30] and Word2Vec embeddings [24], yet these studies did not explore the integration of rich text specifically for FS-CS or the potential of using an exclusive text support set. While zero-shot segmentation methods can generate segmentation directly from text, there are key distinctions: our method is trained in a few-shot episodic manner, whereas zero-shot methods train across the full dataset on all seen classes. Additionally, zero-shot segmentation methods are not in-herently designed to produce classification predictions, and extending them for classification is non-trivial. Finally, our approach supports flexible configurations such as N-way 1-shot, 1-way K-shot, and N-way K-shot settings, which are not feasible with zero-shot segmentation methods. FS-CS predictions based solely on visual support sets often suffer from semantic ambiguity, leading to issues such as under-segmentation and misclassification in cases of unclear background/foreground boundaries or overlap-ping classes. Conversely, predictions based solely on text may lack fine-grained detail, resulting in over-segmentation (Figure 1). These challenges highlight the need for a multi-modal FS-CS approach that can effectively combine visual and textual cues. In this work, we introduce a novel FS-CS approach that uniquely supports both text-only and multi-modal (text and image) support sets. Our method leverages a correlation-based approach, a technique shown to be effective in FS-CS tasks [11, 12], where foreground maps for classification and segmentation are generated by calculating correlations be-tween query and support features. To accomplish this, we utilize the frozen CLIP [20] model, a multi-modal architec-ture that processes both image and text modalities through dedicated encoders, producing aligned embeddings for cor-relation. By using frozen CLIP instead of vision-only mod-els like DINO [2], we maintain a comparable level of model complexity without adding learnable parameters, resulting in only marginal increases in memory consumption and run-time (Table 4). To fully exploit these aligned vision and text em-beddings, we propose the Text-Augmented Classification-Segmentation Transformer (TA-CST). TA-CST incorpo-rates two transformer blocks: one for vision-only correla-tions (between query and support images) and another for vision-text correlations (between query images and support text). However, the feature maps generated by a corre-lation transformer block [12] alone have limited capacity for capturing both multi-scale and global semantics. To address this, we introduce a Feature-Enrichment Module that captures both granular and global semantics, enabling finer segmentation and more accurate classification. Un-like traditional FSS methods, which achieve multi-scale fea-tures through complex architectures [28] or multiple cross-attention layers [19, 30] and lack global semantic capture, our approach uses a single-layer attention block with gran-ular Key and Value (KV) representations via atrous convo-lutions at varying scales [3]. This module captures multi-scale information and global semantics, enhancing the FS-CS task by integrating both fine-grained details and broader context (Figure 6). In cases where the support set includes only text, our classification performance can be limited, particularly with smaller datasets like Pascal-5i. To address this, we pro-pose a zero-shot knowledge distillation strategy that aligns our model’s classification predictions with zero-shot pre-dictions generated by CLIP. Specifically, we generate zero-shot predictions from CLIP using class-specific positive and negative prompts, such as “This is a photo of {class-label }”and “This is not a photo of {class-label }.” This approach enhances performance across datasets of varying sizes, pre-serving CLIP’s generalization capabilities in text-only set-tings. Fig 2 shows our FS-CS pipeline, which can handle gen-erate predictions with exclusive text (text-only) or multi-modal (text-augmented) support sets. Our experiments demonstrate that text-only support is a viable solution for FS-CS, achieving competitive performance with leading methods on the Pascal-5i and COCO-20 i datasets. We achieve even higher performance with multi-modal sup-port, demonstrating the effectiveness of our novel text-augmented FS-CS approach. For a fair comparison, we also replicate CST with CLIP-B/16 as our baseline. 25358 We provide a brief summary of our novel contributions below: • We first demonstrate that textual data alone can solve the FS-CS task, achieving competitive results, and show that combining text and image-based predictions further im-proves FS-CS performance. • We introduce a novel Feature Enrichment Module, which enhances feature maps with multi-scale and global se-mantics, resulting in better segmentation and classifi-cation predictions through a single attention layer with multi-scale Key and Value representations. • In the text-only setting, we introduce a novel distillation strategy to maintain CLIP’s classification generalization, aligning our model’s performance with zero-shot predic-tions from CLIP. • We extend the correlation transformer used in prior meth-ods to support multi-modal support sets, enabling fusion of classification and segmentation predictions from both visual and textual cues. 

# 2. Related Work Few-shot classification (FS-C) & Segmentation (FS-S) 

Existing few-shot classification methods include metric learning [25, 26, 37, 40], meta-learning [8, 13, 39], and finetuning [15, 16, 27]. Metric learning constructs an em-bedding space that clusters similar samples while distanc-ing dissimilar ones. Meta-learning emphasizes adapting to new tasks from limited samples, and fine-tuning enhances a well-initialized feature space. Few-shot segmentation methods adopt various strategies: some determine classi-fier weights per class [21, 22], others leverage prototype learning [5, 23], and some incorporate a decoder to produce segmentation outputs [18, 36, 38]. ASNet [11] introduced the combined task of few-shot classification and segmenta-tion, using a ResNet backbone [10] for feature extraction. CST [12] builds on this in weakly supervised settings with a self-supervised DINO [2] backbone for correlation map-ping. 

Classification & Segmentation with Text Recent works [9, 32, 41, 42] explore prompt tuning for CLIP [20] in few-shot multi-label classification. Although CLIP has been used for text-based supervised [34, 35], weakly supervised [14], zero-shot segmentation [1, 4, 43], as well as FSS [24, 29, 30], it has not been explored for FS-CS. Text-based FSS methods [24, 29, 30] rely on complex architectures with multiple components and cross-attention blocks, constrained by at least one target class in the query. These methods also fail to generate class predictions, unlike our simple FS-CS approach. While [30] uses positive/negative prompts with Grad-CAM priors for FS-S, we apply them for zero-shot distillation, preserving CLIP’s generalizability. GPT4-Vision [17] tackles weakly supervised FS-CS with an LLM and lacks exclusive text support, unlike our compact CLIP-B/16, which enables text-based prediction generation and augmentation while addressing unique FS-CS challenges. 

# 3. Methodology 

This section presents our novel framework for few-shot classification and segmentation (FS-CS) that supports pre-dictions from a multi-modal support set, consisting of both textual labels and support images. Using a meta-learning approach inspired by correlation-based methods [11, 12, 18], our model is trained on episodic tasks, each compris-ing a query image with a support set. We follow an N -way 

K-shot setup, where there are K examples of N classes in the support set. The model’s objective is to generate a fore-ground mask for all target classes along with classification predictions for each, as specified in the support set. Our Text-Augmented Classification-Segmentation Transformer (TA-CST) advances beyond traditional FS-CS methods [11, 12], which rely solely on visual data, thus limiting support set options to vision only. By incorporat-ing textual cues from class labels, our approach enhances prediction quality by using the textual predictions in tandem with visual predictions. This is achieved through a dual correlation transformer, designed specifically to handle both visual and textual support sets effectively, but limited in its ability to capture both global semantics and multi-scale features crucial for FS-CS tasks. To address these limitations, we introduce a feature en-richment module that augments the transformer’s capacity to process varying spatial scales critical for segmentation tasks, including both few-shot and traditional segmenta-tion [3, 5], while maintaining the global context essential for classification. The feature enrichment module achieves this balance through a single attention layer with a gran-ular Key-Value (KV) structure, which embeds multi-scale inductive bias without losing the global semantics of the transformer. During experiments, we observed a drop in classifica-tion accuracy when using exclusive text-based support, par-ticularly with smaller datasets (e.g., Pascal-5i). To address this and preserve the generalization capability of CLIP in classification, we propose a zero-shot knowledge distilla-tion strategy. Zero Shot Knowledge Distillation: Classification perfor-mance in our method suffers when support samples are lim-ited to text, especially on datasets like Pascal-5i, where the training set is smaller, amplifying the accuracy drop. To counter this, we utilize CLIP’s zero-shot capability [20]. Unlike standard zero-shot classification, where text pro-jections of known classes are compared to image projec-tions, our task involves determining the presence of a spe-cific support class. In zero-shot prediction, we use the pos-itive prompt, ’This is a photo of {class-label },’ followed by the negative prompt, ’This is not a photo of {class-label }.’ Both prompts are processed through the CLIP text encoder and projection layer, producing the output embed-dings T+,p -cls s and T−,p -cls s for the [EOS] token. The sim-ilarities, P os and N eg , are then defined between the query image projection Vp-cls q and the positive and negative pro-jections, T+,p -cls s and T−,p -cls s , respectively. The zero-shot prediction is then derived by concatenating these similari-ties: Zcls = Concat (N eg, P os ). # 4. Experiments 

We conduct our experiments using CLIP ViT-B/16 [20] as the feature extractor in our model, while previous models’ results were obtained with ResNet-50 [10]. The feature en-richment module uses dilation values of 3, 6, 10, and all experiments are optimized with the Adam optimizer at a learning rate of 1e−3. To ensure an unbiased comparison, we replicate CST using CLIP-B/16 (Baseline) as the feature extractor, paired with their standard DINO (ViT-S/8) back-bone. Evaluation adheres to established protocols [11, 12] and is performed across four folds on Pascal-5i and COCO-

20 i. Evaluation is conducted with N-way K-shot episodes, while training uses 1-way-1-shot episodes. Further details on experiments and ablations are provided in the supple-mentary material.

5.5. Effect of the zero shot knowledge distillation on FS-CS 

Table 8 presents the averaged results of the ablation study on zero-shot distillation components aimed at enhancing classification performance. The results underscore the im-portance of zero-shot augmented classification predictions and cross-entropy loss in improving accuracy. In the ab-sence of cross-entropy loss, classification loss dominates, which undermines the goal of preserving CLIP’s gener-alizability. Augmented predictions align effectively with CLIP’s zero-shot classification, helping to minimize cross-entropy loss. As a result, combining both components is more effective than using either one individually. 

# 6. Conclusion 

This paper demonstrates that text-only prompts can achieve high performance in Few-shot Classification and Segmen-tation (FS-CS), often outperforming prior methods. We further improved results by combining text with support image predictions (Text-Augmented), leveraging a multi-modal approach. To address CLIP’s generalization limits, we introduced zero-shot knowledge distillation and a fea-ture enrichment module for capturing fine details and global semantics.
