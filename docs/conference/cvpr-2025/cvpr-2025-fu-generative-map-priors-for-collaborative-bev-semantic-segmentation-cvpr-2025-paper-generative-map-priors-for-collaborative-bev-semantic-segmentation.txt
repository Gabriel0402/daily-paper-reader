## Abstract 

Collaborative perception aims to address the constraint of single-agent perception by exchanging information among multiple agents. Previous works primarily focus on col-laborative object detection, exploring compressed transmis-sion and fusion prediction tailored to sparse object features. However, these strategies are not well-suited for dense fea-tures in collaborative BEV semantic segmentation. There-fore, we propose CoGMP, a novel Collaborative framework that leverages Generative Map Priors to achieve efficient compression and robust fusion. CoGMP introduces two key innovations: Element Format Feature Compression (EFFC) and Structure Guided Feature Fusion (SGFF). Specifically, EFFC leverages map element priors from codebook to en-code BEV features as discrete element indices for trans-mitted information compression. Meanwhile, SGFF utilizes a diffusion model with structural priors to coherently inte-grate multi-agent features, thereby achieving consistent fu-sion predictions. Evaluations on the OPV2V dataset show that CoGMP achieves a 6.89 /7.64 Road/Lane IoU improve-ment and a 32 -fold reduction in communication volume. 

## 1. Introduction 

Collaborative perception aims to achieve global optimal perception with minimal message exchange between multi-agents. It has notable applications in fields such as vehicle-to-everything [54, 63] and autonomous drone swarms [45, 53]. In scenarios with multi-agents, they exchange com-plementary information to overcome individual constraints like restricted viewpoints [7] and occlusions [10], thereby enhancing perceptual ability. Specifically, senders need to adopt compression strategies to retain local information as much as possible under limited transmission bandwidth. Meanwhile, receivers are committed to aggregating infor-mation from senders to achieve global perception despite conflicts caused by pose errors and prediction discrepan-cies. To this end, collaborative perception methods focus on achieving efficient information compression on sender side and robust information fusion on receiver side.

Existing methods [6, 17, 26, 32, 43, 49–52] primar-ily address the 3D object detection task in Bird’s Eye View (BEV). However, accurate environment understand-ing requires perceiving not only surrounding objects but also scene layouts [4, 19, 23]. Therefore, we explore the compression and fusion strategies for collaborative BEV se-mantic segmentation, which has not been explored. De-signing appropriate strategies for dense features presents significant challenges as shown in Fig. 1 (a). During the sender compresses BEV features with spatial mask and di-mensionality reduction, information degradation inevitably occurs, resulting in inferior perception quality. On the re-ceiver side, when features from multi-agents are fused, con-flicting semantic estimations in overlapping regions present a substantial obstacle and lead to erroneous fusion predic-tions. Existing strategies tailored to collaborative detection fail to address the above challenges due to their dependency on object-level sparsity, which is absent in the dense BEV segmentation task because of non-negligible feature repre-sentation differences. Therefore, we propose CoGMP , a novel Co llaborative framework with Generative Map Priors for BEV semantic This CVPR paper is the Open Access version, provided by the Computer Vision Foundation. Our method leverages the in-herent Map Priors (e.g ., layout structure, road shapes, and lane continuity) from generative models to help collabora-tive perception. As illustrated in Fig. 1 (b), on the sender side, we introduce an innovative Element Format Feature 

Compression ( EFFC ) method, which utilizes element pri-ors to represent continuous BEV features as discrete map element indices. Specifically, we first construct map ele-ment priors contained in the codebook by training the VQ-VAE [41] on BEV maps. Then, we determine the indices in the codebook by predicting the element type based on BEV features. During message transmission, replacing continu-ous BEV features with discrete element indices efficiently reduces communication volumes. Furthermore, senders se-lect the critical element indices to be transmitted, which can be used by receivers to reconstruct the dropped elements. Compared with reconstructing complex high-dimensional BEV features, element priors enable easier information re-covery by estimating element types. By abstracting BEV features based on element priors, EFFC significantly re-duces communication volume. On the receiver side, we propose an innovative Structure 

Guided Feature Fusion ( SGFF ) method, which robustly fuses features from senders under the guidance of global structural priors. In particular, SGFF progressively injects conditions from multi-agents into diffusion process [16], with the ego’s preliminary perception as the initial state. Benefiting from the map structure prior learned through dif-fusion training, SGFF can adaptively select complementary information and resolve conflicts among agents based on implicit map structure priors. Moreover, considering the efficiency limitations of diffusion-based models when ap-plied to perception tasks, we design an uncertainty adaptive time scheduler to enable high-quality fusion prediction with fewer iterations. By reformulating the multi-agent feature fusion as a conditional diffusion process, SGFF achieves global consistent fusion predictions. In summary, our approach efficiently compresses in-formation by sharing codebook element priors and lever-ages structure priors in the diffusion model to ag-gregate elements from multi-agents in appropriate re-gions. We validate the effectiveness of our method on the OPV2V [47] dataset, and comprehensive experi-ments demonstrate that CoGMP achieves state-of-the-art performance-communication trade-off. Compared to pre-vious methods, CoGMP achieves 6.89 /7.64 Road/Lane IoU improvements and a 32 -fold communication volume reduc-tion simultaneously. Additionally, improvements reach up to 10 .59 /11 .17 Road/Lane IoU under the similar transmis-sion bandwidth, and communication volume is further re-duced by 512 factor while still maintaining superior perfor-mances. Overall, our main contributions are as follows: • We propose a novel collaborative BEV segmentation method CoGMP that leverages generative map priors for efficient compression and robust fusion. • We introduce EFFC for compression in the form of dis-crete map element indices and an SGFF process to fuse elements using a conditional diffusion model. • We conduct extensive experiments on the OPV2V dataset, demonstrating that our method achieves a state-of-the-art performance-communication trade-off. 

## 2. Related Works 

Collaborative perception. Existing researches primarily focus on efficient information compression and robust in-formation fusion within the collaboration process. Re-garding information compression, previous methods re-duce communication volume by limiting transmission tar-gets [11, 29, 30, 57], reducing feature channels [20, 27], and using spatial filtering maps based on objectness scores [17, 18] or mutual information [50]. For robust information fusion, previous approaches employ various techniques, such as attention mechanism [26, 49, 52], graph optimiza-tion [32, 44], neural reasoning [40], and multi-scale interac-tions [43, 51] for multi-agent features integration. However, most methods are specifically designed for object detection task, which can not apply to BEV segmentation task. Al-though some studies [28, 42, 48] have developed collabo-rative BEV segmentation frameworks, their works typically employ task-driven compression while overlooking the uti-lization of structural prior information specific to maps. To fill this gap, our work introduces rich map priors from gen-erative models, to achieve efficient information compres-sion via element priors and robust information fusion with structure priors. 

Diffusion models for perception. The recent achieve-ments of diffusion models [16, 35] in generative tasks have sparked a growing interest in adapting for discriminative tasks. Recent works apply diffusion models to various perception tasks, including image segmentation [1, 5, 9, 21, 60], depth estimation [13, 14, 24], and object detec-tion [8, 15, 34, 46, 56]. These studies demonstrate the ef-fectiveness of diffusion models in addressing classical per-ception tasks, inspiring us to apply them to collaborative perception. 

BEV segmentation. The BEV segmentation task utilizes sensor data as inputs and outputs a BEV segmentation map containing road structures such as drivable areas and lane markings. Previous approaches have mostly employed dis-criminative models based on CNNs [31, 37, 58] or trans-formers [2, 33, 61], performing per-pixel classification on BEV maps. Framework. As shown in Fig. 2, our method utilizes a BEV encoder to generate BEV features for each agent. Then, for other agents acting as senders, we apply Element Format Feature Compression to use discrete key element indices as transmission units, thereby reducing communi-cation volume. Specifically, we utilize the Element Type Inference module to compress the original continuous BEV features into discrete type indices and Element Code Map-ping module to convert the indices to element features. Sub-sequently, the Critical Element Selection module is used to select the key elements, which enables Masked Element Re-covery module to maximize the restoration of map informa-tion after the transmission. Leveraging the prior of diverse map elements, we can represent the entire map’s informa-tion using a limited set of element indices. Further details can be found in Sec. 3.2. For the ego agent acting as the receiver, we integrate the elements from other agents into the ego prediction through 

Structure Guided Feature Fusion . In particular, the receiver obtains element features from its own BEV features as men-tioned above. Moreover, to aggregate element features from senders, we employ a conditional latent diffusion model which incorporates global structure priors of BEV maps. Through the diffusion reverse process, we use the features from senders as a condition to guide the progressive en-hancement of the ego features. Finally, a VAE decoder is utilized to predict the segmentation result based on the fused features. Refer to Sec. 3.3 for more details.

3.3. Structure Guided Feature Fusion 

With messages derived from multi-agents, previous meth-ods achieve feature fusion through various techniques ( e.g ., GNN [44], CNN [17], transformer [48]). However, these approaches struggle to handle cross-agent conflicts arising from the limitations of individual perception. To address the above issues, we propose a novel Structure Guided Feature 

Fusion (SGFF) method. Our approach utilizes a diffusion model to capture the global map structure prior for gener-ating BEV segmentation results. By progressively injecting the perceived information of multi-agents as conditions for various regions, our method produces more consistent and realistic predictions under the guidance of structural pri-ors. ## 4. Experiments 

We conduct experiments on the camera-track BEV segmen-tation task of the OPV2V collaborative perception dataset. We demonstrate the effectiveness and robustness of our method through quantitative evaluations in Sec. 4.2. We conduct ablation experiments to validate the impact of our proposed modules in Sec. 4.3 and performed experimental result visualization in Sec. 4.4. 

4.1. Experimental Setup 

Datasets. OPV2V [47] is a widely used, large-scale open simulated collaborative perception dataset. Data for OPV2V is collected from 8 towns within CARLA [12]. It comprises 73 scenes with a total of 11464 frames, with each scene lasting average 25 seconds. Each scene features 2-7

autonomous vehicles, each equipped with four directional cameras that together provide a 360 -degree field of view. During testing, one autonomous vehicle is designated as the ego vehicle, with evaluations focused on a 100 m × 100 m

area centered around it and a map resolution of 39 cm .

Evaluation metric. We use Intersection-over-Union (IoU) of Road and Lane categories as our evaluation metrics, which measure segmentation accuracy by quantifying the overlap between predicted and ground-truth areas. Follow-ing previous works [18, 20, 47], communication volume is measured in terms of the number of bytes transmitted per message as log 2(b(M)/8) .

Implementation details. Following [44], we assume that all agents have a communication range of 70 meters, and agents beyond the ego’s communication range do not partic-ipate in collaboration. Our training process can be divided into three stages. Firstly, we perform map-based VQ-VAE training, using latent features with a resolution of 32 × 32 ,and a codebook containing 128 element types, each with a 

64 -dimensional feature vector. Then, referring to the com-mon training procedure [48], we secondly conduct single-agent training with additional Lpri and Lmask to optimize EFFC. Based on the frozen single-agent model, we thirdly train U-Net of SGFF by Lldm to achieve multi-agent fusion. Unless otherwise specified, we use 10 iterations in SGFF. 

Baseline methods. We compare with the previous state-of-the-art methods: F-Cooper [6], AttFuse [47], V2VNet [44], DiscoNet [27], and CoBEVT [48]. In order to make a fair comparison under the same communication volume cost, we adopted a BEV feature-oriented compression transmis-sion strategy [17] for the baselines.

4.3. Ablation Studies 

Effectiveness of SGFF. The SGFF module enhances the element feature of the ego agent by integrating elements from other agents. ## 5. Conclusion 

This paper introduces CoGMP, a novel collaborative per-ception framework for BEV semantic segmentation. The core idea of the method is to leverage generative models to learn the element and structure priors of BEV maps. Ben-efiting from informative map priors, our method signifi-cantly reduces the communication volume during the multi-agent collaboration via efficient feature compression and achieves consistent and realistic predictions by employing a robust fusion process based on diffusion models.
