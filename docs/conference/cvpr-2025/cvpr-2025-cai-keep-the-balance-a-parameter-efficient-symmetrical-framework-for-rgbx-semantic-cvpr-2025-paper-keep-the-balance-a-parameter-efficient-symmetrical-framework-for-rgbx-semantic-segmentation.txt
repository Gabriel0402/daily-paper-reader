# Abstract 

Multimodal semantic segmentation is a critical challenge in computer vision, with early methods suffering from high computational costs and limited transferability due to full fine-tuning of RGB-based pre-trained parameters. Re-cent studies, while leveraging additional modalities as sup-plementary prompts to RGB, still predominantly rely on RGB, which restricts the full potential of other modali-ties. To address these issues, we propose a novel symmetric parameter-efficient fine-tuning framework for multimodal segmentation, featuring with a modality-aware prompting and adaptation scheme, to simultaneously adapt the ca-pabilities of a powerful pre-trained model to both RGB and X modalities. Furthermore, prevalent approaches use the global cross-modality correlations of attention mech-anism for modality fusion, which inadvertently introduces noise across modalities. To mitigate this noise, we propose a dynamic sparse cross-modality fusion module to facili-tate effective and efficient cross-modality fusion. To further strengthen the above two modules, we propose a training strategy that leverages accurately predicted dual-modality results to self-teach the single-modality outcomes. In com-prehensive experiments, we demonstrate that our method outperforms previous state-of-the-art approaches across six multimodal segmentation scenarios with minimal computa-tion cost.

Firstly, to more thoroughly leverage the potential of the X modality, we propose a novel approach to strengthen its features through powerful pre-trained models. Although these models are initially pre-trained on RGB images, prior research has shown that their capabilities can be effec-tively transferred to other modalities [21, 77]. Building on these findings, we introduce a model based on parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) with a symmetrical architecture that leverages the powerful feature representation capabili-ties of pre-trained models to simultaneously strengthen both RGB and X modalities. Thus, this framework treats the X modality as an equal contributor alongside RGB, rather than a supplementary role. Secondly, to mitigate the noise associated with global cross-modality correlations, we propose a modality-specific de-formable attention mechanism, denoted as Dynamic Sparse Cross-modality Fusion (DSCF) module. It enables our model to sparsely and dynamically identify the dominant re-gions within the features of each modality, facilitating effec-tive cross-modality fusion. To further boost the above mod-ules, i.e., modality feature enhancement and cross-modality fusion, we introduce an innovative training strategy, called the Masked Modality Self-Teaching Strategy. This strat-egy involves randomly masking one of the modality inputs, thereby discouraging over-reliance on any single modality during training. For evaluation, we conduct comparative experiments across six datasets with various modalities. The results demonstrate that our model significantly outperforms the state-of-the-art PEFT methods; for example, it achieves a 7.2% mIoU advantage over SOTA on PST-900 [44]. No-tably, with only 4% of the training parameters of full fine-tuning methods [25], our model shows comparable or even better performance. Moreover, our proposed model strikes an excellent balance between model effectiveness and effi-ciency, with only an additional 5.5M parameters compared to the baseline, leading to a significant absolute gain of +10.7% mIoU on the NYUD-v2 dataset [45]. In overall, our contributions are summarized as follows: • To fully harness the utility of the X modality in RGB-X semantic segmentation, we introduce a parameter-efficient fine-tuning framework featuring a symmetrical architecture. This framework treats the X modality as an equal contributor alongside RGB, rather than a supple-mentary role. • We present a novel Modality-Aware Prompting and Adaptation module, designed to simultaneously adapt the capabilities of a powerful pre-trained model to both RGB and X modalities. • To mitigate the noise associated with global cross-modality correlations, we propose a Dynamic Sparse Cross-modality Fusion module to facilitate effective and efficient cross-modality fusion. • To improve modality enhancement and fusion, we pro-pose a training strategy that leverages accurately pre-dicted dual-modality results to self-teach the single-modality outcomes. • As demonstrated, our model achieves state-of-the-art per-formance against PEFT-based methods, and is even com-parable to FFT-based methods on six downstream mul-timodal semantic segmentation datasets, adding only a minimal computational burden. 

# 2. Related Work 

Multimodal Semantic Segmentation. Multimodal seman-tic segmentation has been extensively studied, with mul-timodal segmenters proposed to improve performance by leveraging complementary information from supplemen-tary modalities such as depth [5, 7, 9, 24, 40, 43, 50, 61, 70, 73], thermal [13, 17, 33, 44, 49, 55, 68, 74], polarization [27, 38, 58], and events [1, 64, 65]. However, most of these methods rely on a full fine-tuning strategy, which is time-consuming and inefficient, and the large parameter storage burden makes them unsuitable for many applications and difficult to deploy. Moreover, these methods primarily fo-cus on single-task settings, which may not generalize well across different sensing data [4, 17, 36, 45, 66]. In this work, taking a different path, we explore a novel parameter-efficient paradigm that is applicable to various multimodal semantic segmentation tasks with promising performance. 

Parameter-Efficient Fine-tuning for Vision. With the de-velopment of large models [18, 41, 62], parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) has gained increasing importance. Ini-tially applied in NLP [22, 23, 29], PEFT has recently been widely explored in computer vision [28, 53, 54, 77]. Its core concept is to maintain the efficiency and performance of pre-trained models by freezing their weights and train-ing only a minimal number of additional parameters. Popu-lar PEFT methods include adapter-based tuning [8, 20, 22] and prompt tuning [29, 32, 56]. Recently, DPLNet [12] and GoPT [19] have attempted to integrate PEFT into the multimodal dense prediction task. These methods primarily rely on the RGB modality while treating other modalities as prompts. In this way, auxiliary modality and inter-modality 10588 Figure 2. # 3. Method 

## 3.1. Overview 

In this work, we propose a novel RGB-X multimodal seg-mentation model with symmetric architecture. We em-ploy the pre-trained Transformer as backbone (i.e., Swin-Transformer [37] pre-trained on ImageNet [10]) with a lightweight linear segmentation head. Specifically, in con-trast to previous PEFT methods that regard X modalities as less significant or supplementary prompts, our approach treats RGB and X as equal contributors. The overall ar-chitecture is illustrated in Fig. 2. Specifically, our model comprises of four stages. Each stage primarily consists of three key steps: 1) modality-aware prompting and adap-tation (Sec. 3.2), 2) dynamic sparse cross-modality fusion (Sec. 3.3), and 3) the production of segmentation results. Firstly, our proposed model processes the inputs of two different modalities {Irgb , I x} through independent patch embedding layers to obtain the corresponding tokens, i.e., 

Xrgb and Xx. Subsequently, in the MAPA module, these tokens are combined and projected onto the distribution of each modality to generate the corresponding prompts 

{Prgb , P x}. Consequently, the multimodal tokens, aug-mented with specific prompts, are subsequently passed through L frozen pre-trained Transformer layers equipped with modality-specific adapters to produce the correspond-ing modality tokens, denoted as { ˆXrgb , ˆXx}. Secondly, 

{ ˆXrgb , ˆXx} are aggregated via the DSCF module, result-ing in fused tokens ˆX. Thirdly, the fused token is fed into a lightweight segmentation head to compute the segmentation map. For training this model, we apply the MMST strategy (Sec. 3.4). In the subsequent sections, we will concentrate on one single stage of our model to elucidate the technical details. 

## 3.2. Modality-Aware Prompting and Adaptation 

MAPA can be further divided into three steps: First, it amalgamates the dual-modality features into a unified rep-resentation, facilitating information sharing across modali-ties. Subsequently, this unified representation is projected onto the distribution of each modality to generate heteroge-10589 neous prompts for the following steps. Finally, the gener-ated prompts are integrated with modality-specific adapters to produce modality-specific features.

## 3.3. Dynamic Sparse Cross-modality Fusion 

Previous PEFT methods [19, 20] often employ tailored modules to establish global associations for different modalities. However, introducing these modules may result in unnecessary computational overhead, since not all im-age regions are necessary for the task. To capture dominant regions from different modalities and establish long-range contextual modality relationships, inspired by deformable attention [57], we proceed as follows. We first identify key deformable points from various modalities within a low-rank space, creating a multimodal point set. Subsequently, we sample and weight these salient points, and establish their long-range contextual relationships. As shown in Fig. 2(b), to reduce the computational bur-den, we use linear projection functions to map the RGB and X modality tokens { ˆXrgb , ˆXx} into a low-rank space, re-sulting in { ˜Xrgb , ˜Xx}. Then, two uniform grid maps p ∈

R hr × wr ×2 are generated as the reference points for the low-rank features, where r denotes downsampling rate. For-mally, the value of the reference points are linearly spaced at 2D lattice coordinates {(px, p y )|(0 , 0) , ..., ( hr − 1, wr − 1) }.Subsequently, these locations are scaled into [−1, +1] .On the other hand, a lightweight offset prediction net-work is used to predict the corresponding offsets for the reference points, which is expressed as below: 

∆prgb = φoffset ( ˜Xrgb ), ∆px = φoffset ( ˜Xx),prgb = ∆ prgb + p, p x = ∆ px + p,  (4) where φoffset (·) represents offset prediction network, com-prising a depth-wise convolution with stride r, followed by layer normalization, GELU activation, and 1 × 1 convolu-tion. prgb and px denote the deformed points within the 10590 RGB and X modalities, respectively. As shown in Fig. 3, they focus on the interest regions within each of their re-spective modalities. We utilize the union of the deformed point sets from both modalities {prgb ∪px} to sparsely sam-ple multimodal features and extract key multimodal repre-sentations { ˙Xrgb , ˙Xx} ∈ Rn×˜c, where n is the number of sampled points in both prgb and px.Next, we design a learnable modality dominance eval-uation function W(·), which consists of two linear opera-tions with a RELU activation function, to measure the im-portance of different modalities for each deformed point. Specifically, we first fuse multimodal features ˜Xrgb and ˜Xx

using a fusion network F(·) consisting of Conv-BN-ReLU, to obtain coarse fused features Q. We then utilize the same deformed point sets to sparsely sample Q , deriving evalua-tion features ˙XQ ( ˙XQ ∈ Rn×˜c). By feeding ˙XQ into W(·),the weights wrgb and wx (wrgb , w x ∈ Rn×1) for key multi-modal representations ˙Xrgb and ˙Xx can be computed. We weigh them to obtain the essential deformed fused features: 

˙X = wrgb ˙Xrgb + wx ˙Xx. (5) The deformed keys K and values V are obtained by the projections Wk ˙X and Wv ˙X. Last, Q, K, and V are deliv-ered into the multi-head attention module to construct inter-modal long-range contextual relationships. The output re-fined fused features are processed through an up-projection, resulting in fused tokens ˆX, before feeding into the segmen-tation head. 

## 3.4. Masked Modality Self-Teaching Strategy 

Previous PEFT methods have demonstrated poor robust-ness and subpar performance under complex real-world scenarios, attributed to their heavy reliance on the domi-nant modality and insufficient exploitation of inter-modality complementary information. To overcome this limitation, we propose the MMST training strategy. Specifically, during the training phase, we randomly mask the entire input image of one of the two modalities to simulate extreme situations of modality im-balance, thereby reducing the model’s over-reliance on a particular modality. In these situations, supervising only the segmentation outcomes of the fused features ˆX† tends to encourage the model to rely on the feature fusion capabil-ities of DSCF, potentially neglecting the sufficient exploita-tion of inter-modality interaction and feature adaptation at the early stage, i.e., MAPA. Thus, we introduce additional supervision on ˆXrgb and ˆXx before their fusion in DSCF. To achieve this, we utilize the correctly predicted regions

## 4.3. Model Efficiency 

We compare our approach with other methods in terms of model parameters and inference speed. As shown in Ta-ble 6, our focus on a lightweight and efficient design, with all proposed modules implemented in low-rank spaces, al-lows our model to maintain a low parameter count and achieve rapid inference speeds, even with a dual-stream ar-chitecture. Consequently, our model offers significant ad-vantages for practical applications. 

# 5. Conclusion 

In this paper, we propose a novel symmetric framework for unifying multimodal semantic segmentation in a parameter-efficient manner. This framework treats the X modality as an equal contributor alongside RGB, rather than a supple-mentary prompt. Specifically, we present MAPA, designed to simultaneously adapt the capabilities of a powerful pre-trained model to both RGB and X modalities. We propose DSCF to facilitate effective and efficient cross-modality fu-sion. To enhance modality enhancement and fusion, we propose a new training strategy, MMST. Superior perfor-mance on public benchmarks demonstrates the effective-ness and efficiency of our model. 

Limitations. Although our model performs well on multi-ple downstream multimodal semantic segmentation tasks, it has not been explored for visual-language tasks. In future work, we will consider incorporating language modalities to support a broader range of visual-language tasks.
