## Abstract 

We present EvOcc, a novel framework for evidential seman-tic occupancy mapping. It consists of two parts: (1) an evidential approach for calculating the ground-truth 3D se-mantic occupancy maps from noisy  LiDAR  measurements, and (2) a method for training image-based occupancy esti-mation models through a new loss formulation. In contrast to state-of-the-art semantic occupancy maps, our approach explicitly models the uncertainty introduced by unobserved spaces or contradicting measurements, and we show that using it results in significantly stronger models. Evaluated as ray-based  mIoU , our evidential semantic occupancy map-ping approach improves over the baselines by at least  15 .8% 

for the ground truth and  5.5%  for the trained model. Overall, we make a significant contribution towards more detailed and uncertainty-aware 3D environment understanding and safe operation in autonomous driving. 

## 1. Introduction 

A critical component for safe automated driving is accu-rate and reliable environment perception, which includes understanding the scene geometry as well as interpreting its semantic content. While various 3D representations have been explored in the past, semantic voxel grids have be-come a recent de facto standard for  ML -based environment perception [15, 31, 32, 34, 35]. For a long time,  automated driving  relied on  LiDAR  sen-sors due to their low and distance-independent measure-ment errors. However, recent methods attempt to recon-struct the 3D environment from camera images alone [ 10 –12 , 15 , 17 , 19 – 21 , 24 ], since  LiDAR  is sparse, expensive, and redundant. This makes predicting accurate environment maps from images an important task, and further brings up the problem of obtaining ground-truth training data. The lat-ter is usually produced by recording  LiDAR  measurements, and employing heuristics to convert them into semantic occu-pancy maps. However, such reconstructed occupancy maps

In summary, we make the following contributions: • We present a complete 3D evidential mapping framework for semantic occupancy consisting of two parts: occupancy GT generation and image-based occupancy estimation. • We apply a novel  BBA  formulation based on noisy, par-tially annotated  LiDAR  measurements, and employ a novel loss that directly uses the obtained evidential semantic oc-cupancy maps as supervision. • We demonstrate both qualitatively and quantitatively, that the semantic occupancy maps produced by our trained model and mapping approach are significantly more accu-rate than those from  state-of-the-art  methods in terms of agreement with  LiDAR  measurements (see Sec. 5.3). ## 2. Related Work 

3D Occupancy Datasets. Training perception models for automated driving requires large-scale multimodal datasets, such as KITTI [ 8], nuScenes [ 2], Waymo [ 30 ] and Agro-verse2 [ 36 ]. These datasets record the surrounding environ-ment of the vehicle with multiple onboard sensors, such as  LiDAR  and cameras. Additionally, they provide an-notations for the raw sensor data for tasks like 3D object detection [ 17 , 19 , 23 , 24 ] and semantic  LiDAR  segmenta-tion [ 12 , 37 ]. The task of camera-only semantic occupancy prediction [ 18 , 31 , 32 , 34 , 35 ] is to predict the occupancy state and class of each voxel in the environment of the vehi-

> 1https://github.com/boschresearch/evocc

cle, given the onboard camera images. A common process for obtaining  ground-truth  (GT ) 3D occupancy grids is aggregating  LiDAR  measurements over multiple time steps under the consideration of moving ob-jects [ 31 , 32 , 34 , 35 ]. The resulting aggregated point clouds are then partitioned into dense voxel grids, where each voxel containing a point is labeled according to the point’s annota-tion. Finally, ray casting is used to determine the free space labels between the  LiDAR  origin and the first occupied voxel. The quality of the occupancy grids can be improved through additional processing steps. For instance, Occ3D [ 31 ] and SurroundOcc [ 35 ] propose to make the point cloud more dense using mesh and surface reconstruction [ 13 , 33 ]. Fur-thermore, Occ3D enforces consistency between the camera images and the occupancy grid through image-guided voxel refinement. OpenOccupancy [ 34 ] improves the label quality with additional human supervision and their Augment and Purifying process. Kälble et al. [15] leverage  evidence the-ory  (ET ) and achieve the current state of the art by proposing to model the occupancy state of each voxel based on the number of transmissions and reflections through it. Our work builds up on [ 15 ] and proposes a framework using  ET 

that can model beliefs for semantic classes. 

3D Semantic Occupancy Prediction. The task of 3D se-mantic occupancy prediction is to estimate the state of each voxel in the environment of the vehicle using only cam-era images. A voxel can either be free or occupied by a specific class. The CVPR 2023 Occupancy Prediction Challenge [ 31 , 32 ] is a popular benchmark for this task. The winner of this challenge, FB-OCC [ 20 , 21 ], extends BEVDet [ 11 ] by a forward-backward view transformation module. TPVFormer [ 12 ] addresses the high memory and compute requirements for a dense 3D grid and projects the 3D space onto 3D planes. Additional depth supervision for the process of splatting 2D image features into the 3D space has been introduced by BEVDepth [ 17 ]. BEVDet4D [ 10 ] ag-gregates additional information over time for the task of 3D object detection. In this work, we use BEVDet4D-Occ-R50 as a baseline model, which is an adaptation of BEVDet4D specifically tailored for the task of semantic occupancy pre-diction and commonly used as reference model. We show that training this model on the new ground truth obtained with our approach gives superior results. 

Occupancy Grid Mapping. Static occupancy grid map-ping using a Bayesian interpretation of probabilities is a well-explored area in robotics [ 7, 25 ]. The state of a voxel cell is modeled as a Bernoulli-distributed random variable and can either be occupied or free. ## 4. Method 

Here we present our EvOcc framework split into two stages. The first stage (Sec. 4.1) reconstructs accurate semantic oc-cupancy maps from sparse  LiDAR  scans with partially anno-tated semantic labels. The second stage (Sec. 4.2) provides models trained to predict semantic occupancy with signifi-cantly improved accuracy compared to the state of the art.

5.2. Model Training 

To demonstrate the impact of the more accurate  GT  data provided by our method, we compare BEVDet4D-Occ [ 10 ,11 ] trained on our semantic occupancy data and our proposed supervision to the same model trained on the commonly used Occ3D [ 31 ] dataset. We use ResNet-50 [ 9] as the backbone for BEVDet4D-Occ [ 10 , 11 ]. The model predicts semantic occupancy grid maps, using the surround-view camera images as model inputs. Importantly, we do not 

change the model architecture, but only the loss and the number of outputs in the last layer to match the number of hypotheses. Additionally, we use our generated semantic occupancy data instead of Occ3D’s data for supervision. BEVDet4D-Occ uses additional depth supervision dur-ing training, which is not required for our occupancy 

GT . We start training from a model checkpoint trained on Occ3D [ 31 ]. We continue training for 24 epochs using the same hyperparameters, but only changing the model super-vision to our evidential formulation. Finally, we reduce the learning rate by a factor of 10 and continue training for 24 more epochs. The baseline model follows the same training schedule, but is supervised with Occ3D’s data.

## 6. Conclusion 

In this work, we presented a novel approach to obtain high-quality 3D semantic occupancy maps using  ET . Our ap-proach introduces a new  BBA  formulation for modeling 

LiDAR  measurements with partial semantic annotations. We compared our generated ground truth to that of other  state-of-the-art  semantic occupancy datasets and showed that our data matches the  LiDAR  measurements better by a large margin in terms of geometry and semantics. We also demon-strated that using our ground truth for training the semantic occupancy prediction model BEVDet4D-Occ significantly improves the model’s performance. In conclusion, our work sets a new state of the art for semantic occupancy ground truth generation and model training, and therewith makes an important contribution towards autonomous driving. 27474 References
