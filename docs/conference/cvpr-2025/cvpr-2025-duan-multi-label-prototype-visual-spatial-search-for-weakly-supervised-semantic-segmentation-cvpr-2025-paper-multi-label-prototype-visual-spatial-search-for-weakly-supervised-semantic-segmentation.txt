# Abstract 

Existing Weakly Supervised Semantic Segmentation (WSSS) relies on the CNN-based Class Activation Map (CAM) and Transformer-based self-attention map to generate class-specific masks for semantic segmentation. However, CAM and self-attention maps usually cause incomplete segmen-tation due to classification bias issue. To address this issue, we propose a Multi-Label Prototype Visual Spatial Search (MuP-VSS) method with a spatial query mechanism. Specif-ically, MuP-VSS consists of two key components: multi-label prototype representation and multi-label prototype optimization . The former designs a global embedding to learn the global tokens from the images, and then pro-poses a Prototype Embedding Module (PEM) to interact with patch tokens to understand the local semantic informa-tion. The latter utilizes the exclusivity and consistency prin-ciples of the multi-label prototypes to design three prototype losses to optimize them, which contain cross-class proto-type (CCP) contrastive loss, cross-image prototype (CIP) contrastive loss, and patch-to-prototype (P2P) consistency loss. CCP loss models exclusivity of multi-label prototypes learned from a single image to enhance the discriminative properties of each class better. CCP loss learns the consis-tency of the same class-specific prototypes extracted from multiple images to enhance the semantic consistency. P2P loss is proposed to control the semantic response of the pro-totype to the image patches. Experimental results on Pascal VOC 2012 and MS COCO show that MuP-VSS significantly outperforms recent methods and achieves state-of-the-art performance.

For the WSSS task, a vital step is to generate high-quality class-specific pseudo labels by leveraging weak la-bels. Image-level labels are the simplest form of weak su-pervision, providing the categories that an image contains without any localization label, like points or boxes. Existing WSSS methods rely on CNN-based Class Activation Map (CAM) [49] or Transformer-based self-attention maps [16] to extract object localization maps as pseudo pixel-level an-notations. However, CAM and self-attention maps cannot completely locate objects. As shown in Fig. 1, CNN-based methods only focus on the salient object regions via adap-tively aggregating channel features, e.g. , the head of cats of Fig. 1 (a). Although the Transformer-based methods can compensate for the shortcomings of CNNs, they still can-not segment complete regions, e.g. , the red area of Fig. 1 (b). We think that the incomplete segmentation is caused by the classification bias , where the multi-label classification loss prompts the model to focus on discriminative regions that contribute more to the classification task, resulting in incomplete segmentation. 

To address classification bias, we propose a novel Multi-Label Prototype Visual Spatial Search (MuP-VSS) method to generate the pseudo pixel-level labels by spatial query mechanism. As shown in Fig. 1 (c), we define multi-label prototypes as class-specific query vectors to search the vi-sual space of the image by calculating the similarity scores, which are acted as a class-specific pseudo label. A cru-cial design is that multi-label classification loss is used in patch tokens of images instead of the multi-label prototypes, thereby eliminating the impact of classification bias. Specifically, Multi-label prototype transforms WSSS tasks into embedding representation tasks. Hence, the key idea lies in how to learn multi-label prototype represen-tations. To this end, we propose two key components to build our MuP-VSS: multi-label prototype representation and multi-label prototype optimization. For multi-label pro-totype representation, we extract multi-label global tokens from the whole image via convolution-style global embed-ding, then joint patch tokens to learn multi-label prototypes. Further, a Prototype Embedding Module (PEM) is devel-oped to interact information and enhance class-specific re-sponses between patch tokens and multi-label prototypes via their similarity corrections. For multi-label prototype optimization, we utilize the exclusivity and consistency of multi-label prototypes as implicit constraint conditions to optimize their representation. As shown in Fig. 2, exclusiv-ity is reflected in the fact that multi-label prototypes from a single image should be different and push apart from each other, e.g. , the mutual separation between symbols with dif-ferent colors. Consistency implies that prototypes of the same category across multiple images are consistent and close to each other, e.g. , the mutual attraction among sym-bols with the same color. Based on these properties, we propose cross-class prototype (CCP) contrastive loss and cross-image prototype (CIP) contrastive loss to control the representation of multi-label prototypes. CCP loss increases the discrimination between multi-label prototypes learned from a single image and CIP loss narrows the similarity distance between prototypes of the same category learned from a batch of images. Furthermore, we propose a patch-to-prototype (P2P) consistency loss to enhance consistency between patch tokens and multi-label prototypes. Extensive experiments on Pascal VOC 2012 and MS COCO show that our MuP-VSS achieves state-of-the-art performance when only image-level labels are available. Our main contributions are summarized as: 

• We propose a novel Multi-label Prototype Visual Spatial Search (MuP-VSS) framework to address the classifica-tion bias, where transforms a WSSS task into presentation task of multi-label prototypes. 

• We propose a global embedding approach to model multi-label prototypes via projecting global image representa-tion and a Prototype Embedding Module (PEM) to en-hance the semantic response. 

• We design two self-supervised losses, cross-class proto-type (CCP) and cross-image prototype (CIP) contrastive loss, to optimize multi-label prototypes and a patch-to-prototype (P2P) consistency loss to learn the response of prototypes to patch tokens. 

# 2. Related Work 

2.1. Weakly Supervised Semantic Segmentation 

CNN-based CAM. Weakly supervised semantic segmen-tation utilizes weak supervisory signals, like image-level labels [20, 46], box annotations, and scribbles [4], to cal-culate loss and optimize the model. The image-level label is the simplest weak supervisory signal and has been ex-tensively studied in the field of weakly supervised seman-tic segmentation [8, 11, 23, 40]. CNN-based CAM [49] is groundbreaking and pioneering work for WSSS, which only trained a simple classification network to activate the most 30242 discriminative object regions, resulting in rough semantic masks. Tao et al. [8] designed a knowledge transfer with a simulated inter-image erasing approach to weaken the orig-inal activation. Jo et al. [21] proposed hierarchical cluster-ing of class-specific features to mine the fine-grained class information with adjacent multiple classes. Jang et al. [20] employed texts as flexible signals to capture the global con-text. 

Transformer-based Self-Attention Map. With the success of vision transformer, the migration of transform-ers [23, 37, 45] is actively conducted in WSSS. The Transformer-based self-attention maps are utilized to re-fine the class-specific features and generate semantic maps. Yoon et al. [45] proposed a class token infusion strategy from multiple images to enhance global-local consistency. Different from the consistency between class tokens, Wu et al. [37] leveraged data augmentation to develop a dual stu-dent framework to progressively introduce more trustwor-thy pseudo-labels. Recently, the advances in large models and vision-language models accelerated the development of WSSS. Zhang et al. [23] introduced the Segment Anything Model (SAM) [22] into the WSSS. Since SAM lacks the ability of semantic recognition, WeCLIP [46] leveraged the CLIP [31] to extract image and text features and designed a simple decoder to interpret extracted semantic features for final prediction. 

2.2. Prototype Learning with WSSS 

Class prototype learning has been well studied in few-shot [17, 28, 29] and domain adaptation/generalization [12, 19] tasks. Recently, the generalization of class prototypes has attracted the attention of the segmentation community [4, 34, 48, 50], which reveals the excellent potential of application in image segmentation. Du et al. [14] in-troduced pixel-level prototype mining strategy to develop dual-view contrastive learning. However, pixel-level proto-type lacks the global information of images, resulting in the sub-optimal performances. To address this issue, Chen et al. [7] designed a self-supervised image-specific prototype exploration to capture global semantic cues. In addition to prototype exploration, Chan et al. [4] proposed a prototype-based feature augmentation method to enhance scribble su-pervision. Besides, Zhao et al. [48] built a plug-and-play prototype-based secondary discriminative feature enhance-ment to address incomplete CAM. Further, Tang et al. [34] found that knowledge bias between instances and contexts affects the generalization of prototypes to comprehensively understand global semantics. Prototype learning becomes the new driving force for the WSSS community, which mines core pixels [4] or attention mechanism [7] with class-specific semantics. However, ex-isting prototype-based WSSS methods usually utilize CAM as the central pixel to generate prototypes, which still re-tain classification bias. Different from the existing proto-type learning, we joined multi-label prototypes with patch tokens to learn global class-specific correlation via CCP and CIP losses rather than classification loss, thus avoiding the effects of classification bias.

3.3. Multi-label Prototype Contrastive Learning 

How to optimize the proposed multi-label prototypes is cru-cial for MuP-VSS. A straightforward approach is to adopt multi-label classification loss. However, multi-label clas-sification loss brings two challenges: category unbalance and discriminability bias. Category unbalance means that a single image contains only a small number of categories, resulting in other class-specific prototypes not being op-timized effectively. Discriminability bias is the same as CAM, which only recognizes partially the most discrimi-native object regions. Therefore, directly using multi-label classification is ill-suited. To overcome the defect of multi-label classification loss, we utilize contrastive learning to construct multi-label prototype loss from cross-image and cross-class views. 

Cross-Class Prototype Contrastive Loss . Given an im-age, its different class-specific prototypes contain different semantic information, especially foreground class-specific prototypes. Therefore, these prototypes should have a large distance in feature space. Based on this observation, we use a class-specific prototype to optimize the representation of another class-specific prototype by pushing the distance between them:

3.4. Multi-label Prototype Inference 

As shown in Fig. 4, we employ the multi-label prototypes as class-specific query tokens to match patch tokens and then generate class-specific semantic maps Mp ∈ RC×h×w,which are the query results of prototype-to-patch at the inference stage. Each class-specific prototype is used to match all patch tokens via computing the similarity score, where a high similarity score means that the patch be-longs to the category, and a low similarity score means that the patch is abandoned by the category. By this way, C

class-specific prototypes can obtain C corresponding class-specific semantic maps: 

Mp = P ⊙ F T , (8) where FT is the transposed F.

Mp is a semantic response of prototype to patch, which ignores the spatial distribution of objects. To refine the spatial coherence, we utilize the affinity maps Ap between patch-to-patch to learn spatial dependencies. Further, we use the spatial dependencies learned from patch tokens to enhance the class-specific semantic maps Mp and obtain refined semantic maps Mref :

# 5. Conclusion 

In this paper, we introduce a query-based VSS strategy as an alternative solution to address the classification bias. To this end, we propose a Multi-label Prototype Visual Spatial Search (MuP-VSS) model to learn multi-label prototypes as query tokens, where a global embedding approach is em-ployed to extract multi-label prototypes from scaled-down input images. Besides, Prototype Embedding Modules (PEM) are developed to implant blocks of ViT to interact in-formation between multi-label prototypes and patch tokens. To optimize MuP-VSS, we customize three loss functions to learn the representation of multi-label prototypes: cross-class prototype (CCP) contrastive loss, cross-image proto-type (CIP) contrastive loss, and patch-to-prototype (P2P) consistency loss.
