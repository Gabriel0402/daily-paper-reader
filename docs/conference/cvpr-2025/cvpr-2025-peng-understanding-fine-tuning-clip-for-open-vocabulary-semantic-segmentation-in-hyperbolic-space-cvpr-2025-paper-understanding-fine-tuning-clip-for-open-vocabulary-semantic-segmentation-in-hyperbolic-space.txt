# Abstract 

CLIP, a foundational vision-language model, has emerged as a powerful tool for open-vocabulary semantic segmentation. While freezing the text encoder preserves its powerful embed-dings, recent studies show that fine-tuning both the text and image encoders jointly significantly enhances segmentation performance, especially for classes from open sets. In this work, we explain this phenomenon from the perspective of hierarchical alignment, since during fine-tuning, the hier-archy level of image embeddings shifts from image-level to pixel-level. We achieve this by leveraging hyperbolic space, which naturally encoders hierarchical structures. Our key observation is that, during fine-tuning, the hyperbolic ra-dius of CLIP’s text embeddings decreases, facilitating better alignment with the pixel-level hierarchical structure of vi-sual data. Building on this insight, we propose HyperCLIP, a novel fine-tuning strategy that adjusts the hyperbolic radius of the text embeddings through scaling transformations. By doing so, HyperCLIP equips CLIP with segmentation capa-bility while introducing only a small number of learnable parameters. Our experiments demonstrate that HyperCLIP achieves state-of-the-art performance on open-vocabulary semantic segmentation tasks across three benchmarks, while fine-tuning only approximately 4% of the total parameters of CLIP. More importantly, we observe that after adjustment, CLIP’s text embeddings exhibit a relatively fixed hyperbolic radius across datasets, suggesting that the granularity re-quired for this segmentation task might be quantified using the hyperbolic radius.

# 2. Related Work 

## 2.1. Open-vocabulary Semantic Segmentation 

Prior works on open-vocabulary semantic segmentation typi-cally tackle this task by leveraging CLIP [ 46 ]. Early efforts, 4563 e.g., [ 68 ], directly fine-tune CLIP on mainstream segmen-tation datasets, like COCO [ 4]. However, these approaches argue that fully fine-tuning CLIP’s encoder significantly diminishes its ability to generalize to unseen classes. To mitigate this issue, some methods [ 11 , 17 , 60 , 61 ] take the opposite approach, fine-tuning an additional mask genera-tor [ 8 ] for segmentation while keeping CLIP frozen to pre-serve its generalization capabilities. However, this approach, with its frozen parameter space, lacks segmentation aware-ness, leading to a misalignment between regions and text descriptions [ 37 ]. Other studies [ 9 , 62 , 65 ] propose more sophisticated solutions that fine-tune only specific parame-ters, e.g., certain layers of CLIP, enabling pixel-level pre-dictions while keeping the majority of CLIP’s parameters fixed to minimize the loss of generalization. While these approaches show significant advantages, they often rely on very small learning rates, implicitly promoting minimal devi-ations from the pre-trained CLIP, which limits segmentation performance. In summary, there is still limited understand-ing of how fine-tuning influences CLIP’s representations and why fine-tuning CLIP’s text encoder appears to enhance segmentation performance. In this paper, we aim to pro-vide several new insights that reveal the underlying logic by incorporating a novel perspective: hyperbolic space. 

## 2.2. Learning in Hyperbolic Spaces 

Unlike Euclidean geometry, hyperbolic space can be viewed as the continuous analog of a tree [ 2 ], enabling a hierarchical understanding of knowledge structures. Previous research has demonstrated the advantages of representing such hierar-chical relationships in various domains, including molecular structures [ 66 ], 3D data [ 6, 56 ], text data [ 16 , 70 ], and im-ages [ 18 , 27 ]. Furthermore, several studies have investigated hyperbolic learning in vision tasks to model hierarchical rela-tionships across different modalities [ 10 , 30 , 47 ], showcasing the benefits of capturing the semantic hierarchy among var-ious data types. Nevertheless, while most studies typically leverage the intrinsic hierarchical properties of hyperbolic space, this paper seeks to capture the detailed changes in semantics reflected by the hyperbolic radius. This approach potentially complements current fine-tuning efforts in open-vocabulary tasks by offering a novel hyperbolic perspective. 

# 3. Preliminary Background 

## 3.1. Hyperbolic geometry 

Hyperbolic geometry often has constant negative curvature and is fundamentally different to the Euclidean or spheri-cal geometry which has zero or constant positive curvature, respectively. This enables unique properties such as the di-vergence of parallel lines and the exponential volume growth towards the boundary. Hyperbolic geometry has five differ-ent analytic models to build the hyperbolic space [ 5]. In this work, we utilize the classical Poincar ´e ball model as its effec-tiveness in capturing hierarchical structures [ 16 , 20 , 41 , 47 ].

# 4. Methodology 

In this section, we introduce HyperCLIP for open-vocabulary semantic segmentation. HyperCLIP is based on the Poincar ´eball model, which aims to adjust the hyperbolic radius of CLIP’s embeddings, thereby equipping CLIP with segmen-tation ability. 

## 4.1. Fine-tuning in Hyperbolic Space 

The core technique in HyperCLIP involves linear transfor-mations, i.e., scaling, in the hyperbolic space. One of the primary methods [ 64 ] to learn the weights of linear trans-formations in hyperbolic space is hyperLoRA [ 64 ]. Given a feature embedding z in Euclidean space, this method first maps z to the hyperbolic space at a local reference point, typically the center, using the exponential map (e.g., Eq. 4). Linear transformations with learnable weight matrix W is then applied to z within the hyperbolic space to obtain the hyperbolic representation. Then, a logarithmic map (e.g., Eq. 5) is applied to map the hyperbolic representation back to the Euclidean space. Formally, in the Poincar ´e ball model, this process is realized as:

## 5.3. Ablation Study 

Ablation of fine-tuning CLIP. We assess the efficacy of var-ious fine-tuning design choices for HyperCLIP. As shown in Table 6, we freeze one of CLIP’s encoders and compare the performance with several commonly-used PEFT methods, e.g., LoRA [ 21 ]. Compared to fully freezing CLIP’s en-coders (row 1), fine-tuning either encoder independently al-lows for minimizing its hyperbolic radius, thereby equipping the corresponding encoder with segmentation ability. How-ever, one modality’s hierarchical level remains at the image level, which hinders CLIP from fully acquiring segmenta-tion ability. Consequently, jointly fine-tuning both encoders aligns the hierarchical levels at the pixel level, resulting in op-timal performance. We also compare our method with exist-ing PEFT strategies. Most of these methods, which operate in Euclidean space, struggle to capture the transition between different hierarchical levels. Notably, HyperLoRA [ 64 ] also updates parameters in hyperbolic space. However, because HyperLoRA does not specifically adjust the hyperbolic ra-dius, it fails to fully endow CLIP with segmentation ability, resulting in inferior performance compared to our method. 

Ablation of the size of diagonal blocks. The size of the diagonal block in our scaling transformations determines the number of parameters required to equip CLIP with segmenta-tion ability. To investigate its impact, we conduct an ablation study on the scaling transformations by systematically vary-ing the size of the diagonal block. As shown in Table 4, accu-racy improves incrementally on datasets with fewer classes. However, on datasets with more classes (e.g., A-847 ), the accuracy performance reaches a saturation point around a block size of 128. This suggests that increasing the dimen-sionality of the diagonal block introduces more trainable parameters, which may lead to over-fitting on a closed set. 

Effect of the hyperbolic radius of text embeddings. In or-der to closely examine the effect of the hyperbolic radius on equipping CLIP with segmentation ability, we apply a mean squared error (MSE) loss during fine-tuning. This MSE loss encourages the average hyperbolic radius of the text embed-dings to converge towards different target values. As shown in Fig. 5, the initial value of the average hyperbolic radius is approximately 7.5, which is subsequently minimized to around 6.0 to achieve the optimal value for segmentation. Deviating from this value, either by increasing or decreasing the expected radius, results in diminished performance. This observation offers a new insight: the hyperbolic radius re-quired for a vision task might be relatively fixed, suggesting that it could serve as a metric to quantify the granularity of different vision tasks.

# 6. Conclusion 

In this paper, we propose HyperCLIP, a novel fine-tuning strategy for CLIP tailored to open-vocabulary semantic seg-mentation. Recognizing the hierarchical structure of images, we hypothesize that aligning the hierarchical level of CLIP’s text encoder with pixel-level representations is crucial for accurate pixel-level predictions. HyperCLIP achieves this by adjusting the hierarchical level in hyperbolic space through scaling transformations applied to CLIP’s embeddings, en-abling precise cross-modal alignment. Extensive experi-ments demonstrate that HyperCLIP sets new state-of-the-art results in open-vocabulary semantic segmentation. More importantly, we observe that after adjustment, CLIP’s text embeddings maintain a relatively fixed hyperbolic radius across datasets. This suggests that the segmentation task’s granularity may be quantified using the hyperbolic radius, offering a promising direction for future research. 4569 Acknowledgments
