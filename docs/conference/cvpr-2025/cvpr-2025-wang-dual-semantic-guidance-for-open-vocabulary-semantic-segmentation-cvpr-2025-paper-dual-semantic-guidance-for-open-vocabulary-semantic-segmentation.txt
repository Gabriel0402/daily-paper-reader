# Abstract 

Open-vocabulary semantic segmentation aims to en-able models to segment arbitrary categories. Currently, though pre-trained Vision-Language Models (VLMs) like CLIP have established a robust foundation for this task by learning to match text and image representations from large-scale data, their lack of pixel-level recognition neces-sitates further fine-tuning. Most existing methods leverage text as a guide to achieve pixel-level recognition. How-ever, the inherent biases in text semantic descriptions and the lack of pixel-level supervisory information make it chal-lenging to fine-tune CLIP-based models effectively. This pa-per considers leveraging image-text data to simultaneously capture the semantic information contained in both image and text, thereby constructing Dual Semantic Guidance and corresponding pixel-level pseudo annotations. Particularly, the visual semantic guidance is enhanced via explicitly ex-ploring foreground regions and minimizing the influence of background. The dual semantic guidance is then jointly uti-lized to fine-tune CLIP-based segmentation models, achiev-ing decent fine-grained recognition capabilities. As the comprehensive evaluation shows, our method outperforms state-of-art results with large margins, on eight commonly used datasets with/without background. 

# 1. Introduction 

Open-Vocabulary Semantic Segmentation (OVSS) aims to segment arbitrary categories in open environments, distin-guishing itself from traditional semantic segmentation [1вҖ“ 3], which operates within a predefined set of categories. Re-cently, Visual-Language Models (VLMs) such as CLIP [4] and Align [5] demonstrate exceptional capabilities in align-ing textual descriptions with corresponding images, mak-ing them highly suitable for open-vocabulary tasks. How-ever, these VLMs with image-level visual-text alignment lack fine-grained expressive power, resulting in poor seg-mentation precision when directly applied to the OVSS * Liang Wan is the corresponding author of this paper. task. Currently, several approaches [6вҖ“10] train segmen-tation models based on VLMs using image-text data which is rich in semantic information. This enables the models to acquire fine-grained segmentation capabilities while re-taining their generalization ability across open-vocabulary categories, which provides a promising strategy for tackling semantic segmentation tasks in open-vocabulary scenarios. LetвҖҷs look deeper in open-vocabulary semantic segmen-tation tasks utilizing VLMs: the core principle involves aligning pixel-level features with text embeddings, and as-signing each pixel a category based on these alignments. Currently, some methods [6, 8] trained on image-text data rely on the VLM modelвҖҷs ability to align part of images with the complete texts, establishing weak constraints for segmentation training. In contrast to employing complete texts, some methods [7, 9] attempt to extract nouns from texts to obtain category semantic information that matches local regions in images. However, nouns lacking detailed descriptions can suffer from вҖңInherent biasesвҖқ, which arise from the semantic disparity between coarse-grained text and corresponding images. In image-text data, the nouns in text often offers coarse-grained information, where вҖқcatвҖқ and вҖқdogвҖқ are both labeled as вҖқanimalвҖқ (Fig 1.a), yet there are substantial differences in image level. Moreover, some ex-isting methods [6, 9] lack the pixel-level semantic super-visory information to fine-tune VLMs. As a result, poorly segmented images can still align with the corresponding text well, as shown in Fig 1.b. In this paper, we denote the semantic information used for matching images pixels as semantic guidance . We identify that the crucial factor in improving the modelвҖҷs fine-grained segmentation performance is the establishment of granular semantic guidance (at both text and image level) and accurate segmentation annotations. To acquire more precise semantic guidance and segmentation labels, we ex-plore the feasibility of extracting semantic guidance from images which are abundant in semantic content. The most direct form is the various instances within an image, as these instances provide an unbiased semantic representation of the corresponding regions. To ensure inter-modal consistency within the VLM model, we also ex-tract nouns from text and match them with the instances to form textual semantic guidance. The dual-modality seman-tic guidance mechanisms harness the comprehensive infor-mation within image-text data, synergistically improving fine-grained recognition capability of the VLM model. Specifically, we propose Dual Semantic Guidance to ex-tract semantic information from both visual and textual per-spectives within image-text data. The pipeline consists of 

Dual Semantic Guidance Generation (DSGG) and Dual Se-mantic Guidance Segmentation (DSGS). In DSGG, we uti-lize SAM to extract instances from images, and enhance vi-sual semantics to focus more on instance through a training-free Region Awareness Attention module. In DSGS, we design a training strategy for dual semantic guidance to collaboratively en-hance the modelвҖҷs fine-grained representation capabilities. Our method surpasses state-of-the-art results on various test datasets, including coco-stuff [12], ADE [13], etc., achiev-ing an average mIoU of 48.3 on three datasets with back-ground, and 42.8 on five datasets without background. 

# 2. Related Work 2.1. Vision Foundation Models 

The latest advancements in foundational vision models demonstrate that large-scale pre-training techniques have facilitated the development of potent and versatile mod-els [5, 11]. These models exhibit remarkable adaptability across various tasks [14вҖ“19]. The emergence of highly ex-pressive vision-language models, such as Align and CLIP, has been witnessed in the realm of large-scale contrastive pre-training of text-image pairs. Furthermore, SAM [11] has undergone iterative training on billions of weakly an-notated images, achieving remarkable segmentation per-formance, but it lacks semantic discriminative capabilities. Researchers have proposed various integration strategies: SAM-CLIP [20] constructs a unified model that skillfully integrates the functionalities of SAM and CLIP into a single framework. Open-Vocabulary SAM [21] integrates a CLIP encoder with a SAM decoder, bridging these two distinct components through knowledge distillation. Additionally, some methods [22вҖ“24] integrate multiple models [25вҖ“27] to collaboratively form a powerful open-vocabulary seg-menter. However, such methods often demand substantial computational resources. Compared to these workflow-based integrated models, our approach relies solely on CLIPвҖҷs image encoder as the seg-menter during the testing phase, achieving open-vocabulary segmentation without adding any additional modules. 

## 2.2. Open-Vocabulary Semantic Segmentation

However, these VLM models prioritize the alignment between the holis-tic image and its textual description, overlooking the fine-grained discriminative capabilities of individual pixels. Some works [28вҖ“30] employ supplementary modules and mechanisms to enhance the VLMвҖҷs segmentation capabil-ities, which entail substantial annotation costs. Our approach focuses on constructing dual seman-tic guidance from both image and text, generating pixel-level pseudo-labels to jointly facilitate the adaptation of the CLIP model to dense visual prediction tasks.

## 4.2. Implementation Detail 

We generate a vast number of masks via SAM ViT-B, and utilize the part-of-speech tagging algorithm from NLTK [33] for noun selection. For K-Means [47] cluster-ing, we set k = 5. For the segmentation model, we employ MaskCLIP [48] with ViT-B/16. For Region Awareness At-tention, we utilize CLIP with ViT-B/16. During the exper-iments, we set total batchsize = 128 with a initial learning rate = 6.4e-4, and 30,000 iterations are conducted (we open the last 6 layers of image encoder for training). Further-more, we ultimately train the model for additional 1,000 iterations on textual semantic guidance alone to adapt it to the textual modality received during inference. The cosine schedule [49] is adopted to adjust the learning rate, and the weight decay of the AdamW optimizer [50] is set to 0.05. The model is trained on two RTX 3090 GPUs for five hours. In addition, we solely use image encoder for the datasets without background (w/o bg); whereas for datasets with background (w/ bg), we additionally activate the Back-ground Screening for background prediction.

## 4.5. Discussion Alternative Ways for Region Awareness Attention In Table 5, we evaluated five substitution methods to validate the efficacy of Region Awareness Attention. Firstly,we in-troduced Gaussian noise(see вҖңGaussianвҖқ) to the background regions, which resulted in a decline in performance, be-cause the addition of Gaussian noise does not ensure that the model focuses on the foreground regions. We also at-tempt to mask the attention of the patch tokens correspond-ing to the background regions(see вҖңmask backgroundвҖқ), and find that this strategy severely impaired the modelвҖҷs feature encoding capabilities. Concurrently, we explore learnable strategy: replacing background tokens with class-specific tokens [53] (see вҖңclass promptвҖқ). This strategy is inferior to those of our method, as the lack of training stage pre-vent these training based methods from effectively adapt-ing. Finally, we refer to the mask pooling strategy of MaskCLIP(see вҖңmask poolingвҖқ), whose experimental re-sults are lower than ours, as the low-resolution feature map of CLIP will contain background information and interfere with the instance feature expression. Limitation In the Dual Semantic Guidance Generation, we rely on SAM to generate a multitude of masks for each image, a process that is time-consuming. Furthermore, akin to existing methods in OVSS, our visualization results still suffer from rough boundaries. To boost computational effi-ciency, we opt for K=5 in K-Means due to its superior per-formance. Adaptive selection of K is intriguing, which will be considered in future work. 

# 5. Conclusion 

Pre-trained Vision-Language Models (VLMs) establish the foundation for recognizing open-vocabulary categories in semantic segmentation tasks. Nonetheless, the dearth of pixel-level annotations in image-text data hinders the provi-sion of thorough and accurate supervisory signals for VLM fine-tuning. This paper introduces Semantic Guidance Gen-eration, which leverages extensive image-text data from vi-sual and textual dimensions, offering comprehensive and precise data to enable VLMs to attain pixel-level segmen-tation capability. Furthermore, we develop the Dual Se-mantic Guidance to facilitate efficient model training.
