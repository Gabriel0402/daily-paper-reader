Abstract 

We study the complexity of computing Bayes-Nash equilibria in single-item first-price auctions. We present the first efficient algorithms for the problem, when the bidders’ val-ues for the item are independently drawn from the same continuous distribution, under both established variants of continuous and finite bidding sets. More precisely, we design polynomial-time algorithms for the white-box model, where the distribution is provided di-rectly as part of the input, and query-efficient algorithms for the black-box model, where the distribution is accessed via oracle calls. Our results settle the computational complexity of the problem for bidders with i.i.d. values.

# 1 Introduction 

First-price auctions have been used since antiquity for the sale of various commodities such as land, property, farming rights, livestock, or tax and public procurement contracts [Cassady, 1967]. Today, these auctions are widely used in markets for advertising space, in the form of “ad auctions” (see, e.g., [Balseiro et al., 2021; Despotakis et al., 2021; Paes Leme et al., 2020; Conitzer et al., 2022; Alcobendas and Zeithammer, 2022]), constituting a multi-billion-dollar industry [Bowman, 2024]. Part of the appeal of these auctions is their simplicity and intuitive nature: the winner is the bidder with the highest bid (breaking ties uniformly at random), who pays an amount equal to her bid. The development of the theory of first-price auctions dates back to the early 1960s and the pioneering work of Vickrey [1961]. Vickrey studied the equilibria of the auction—or, more precisely, of the strategic game induced by the incentives of the bidders to underbid their values for the item—when values are independently drawn from the uniform distribution. Over the next decades, several variants of the auction were studied systematically in a plethora of works in economics, e.g., see [Griesmer et al., 1967; Riley and Samuelson, 1981; Milgrom and Weber, 1982; Chwe, 1989; Plum, 1992; Marshall et al., 1994; Lebrun, 1996, 1999; Maskin and Riley, 2000; Lizzeri and Persico, 2000; Athey, 2001; Reny and Zamir, 2004]. Most of these works were mainly concerned with establishing the existence, and often the uniqueness, of equilibria of the auction. For the simpler case of symmetric first-price auctions (most notably for i.i.d. values), the literature has managed to produce elegant analytic solutions, e.g., see [Vickrey, 1961; Riley and Samuelson, 1981; Milgrom and Weber, 1982]. Symmetric auctions capture cases where the bidders’ information is generated by the same technologies or market parameters, and in fact constitute the “textbook” model studied in the auction literature [Myerson, 1981; McAfee and McMillan, 1987; Krishna, 2009]. While the economic theory established the existence of equilibria and managed, via the aforementioned analytic formulas, to describe them for symmetric variants, it did not address the question of how these equilibria can be formally computed . The computation of equilibria in games has been a focal point of research in computer science over the past two decades, e.g., see [Daskalakis et al., 2009; Chen et al., 2009; Mehta, 2018; Rubinstein, 2018]; for the first-price auction in particular, this question was notably studied in a series of recent works [Filos-Ratsikas et al., 2023; Chen and Peng, 2023; Filos-Ratsikas et al., 2024, 2025]. While these works provided computational hardness results for computing equilibria, these results only concern variants of the auction with rather general assumptions on the value distributions, namely subjectivity 

[Filos-Ratsikas et al., 2023, 2024] or arbitrary correlation [Filos-Ratsikas et al., 2025], or variants with nonstandard tie-breaking rules [Chen and Peng, 2023]. On the other hand, the positive results obtained in these works, even for simple symmetric settings like auctions with i.i.d. values, are somewhat limited: currently, the best known result for these settings is a polynomial-time approximation scheme (PTAS), i.e., an algorithm which computes an ε-approximate equilibrium in time polynomial in the input parameters, but possibly exponential in 1 /ε , see Filos-Ratsikas et al. [2023, 2025]. The inconclusive nature of these results leaves us with an “asymmetry” for symmetric auctions: mathematically, the equilibria of those auctions can be described by clean analytic formulas, but corresponding polynomial-time algorithms for computing them are unknown. Aiming to address this asymmetry, we are faced with two main challenges. First, the afore-mentioned analytic solutions are not readily appropriate for computational purposes. Hence, making use of them to obtain polynomial-time algorithms requires careful consideration of the representation of the inputs and outputs to the problem and a meticulous “translation” of the mathematical insights to computational methods. In fact, considering approximate equilibria 2might be necessary, as exact equilibria might involve irrational numbers in their description [Filos-Ratsikas et al., 2023], and hence cannot be found in standard models of computation. In those cases, a computational algorithm is one that achieves an approximation within the best possible precision, and the goal is to design such algorithms that run in polynomial time. The second challenge stems from the fact that these analytic formulas only apply when the bidding space is continuous , e.g., an interval of real numbers. For discrete bidding spaces, which are typically studied in most of the related work in computer science [Filos-Ratsikas et al., 2023; Chen and Peng, 2023; Filos-Ratsikas et al., 2024, 2025], such analytic solutions are not generally available. To the best of our knowledge, the only known result of this flavour is due to Chwe [1989], who studies the equilibria of the first-price auction under two crucial assumptions, namely that the (discrete) bids are equidistant , and that the values are uniformly distributed . Chwe’s equilibrium existence result can be interpreted as a non-computational 

method to describe an equilibrium of the auction in this special case. Thus, the first challenge highlighted above applies here as well, together with the additional challenge of extending the results beyond the case of equidistant bids, and beyond the uniform distribution. 

1.1 Our Results and Techniques 

We study first-price auctions in which a set of n bidders submit their bids for one item for sale. Each bidder knows her own value for the item, and has incomplete information about the values of her competitors, via a (common) continuous distribution, from which the values are independently drawn (i.i.d.). Our goal is to design efficient algorithms that compute equilibria, and in fact, even symmetric equilibria of the auction. These are (Bayes-Nash) equilibria in which every bidder employs the same strategy, i.e., the same mapping from values to bids. We consider two variants of the auction, depending on whether the bidding space is continuous or discrete ; we refer to the two variants as the Continuous First-price Auction with Continuous Bids (CCFPA) 

and the Continuous First-price Auction with Discrete Bids (CDFPA) , respectively. Our results can be summarized in the following informal theorem. 

Informal Theorem 1. A (symmetric) equilibrium of the First-Price Auction can be computed efficiently, for auctions with either continuous or discrete bids. 

To be more precise, when the distribution is given to the algorithm directly as part of the input, an algorithm is deemed “efficient” if it runs in polynomial time in the size of the input representation. This representation can be explicit , i.e., via some succinctly described function (e.g., piecewise polynomial), or implicit , where the input is some meaningful computational device (e.g., an appropriate arithmetic circuit) that the algorithm can use to compute the distribution’s cdf. We also (for the first time in the literature of the problem) consider the 

black-box model, where the algorithm has query or oracle access to the distribution. In this case, the efficiency of the algorithm is measured by the number of queries that it performs to the cdf oracle, and the number of arithmetic operations that it performs. Furthermore, we will consider an algorithm to be efficient if it computes either an exact 

equilibrium whenever possible (i.e., whenever the equilibrium can be described using rational numbers), or otherwise an approximate equilibrium with the best possible accuracy (ideally, exponentially small error). An algorithm that computes an ε-approximate equilibrium in time polynomial in the input size and 1 /ε will be referred to as a Fully Polynomial-Time Approxima-tion Scheme (FPTAS) , whereas a PTAS, mentioned earlier, runs in polynomial time for every 

fixed ε (but may be exponential in 1 /ε ). For a more in-depth discussion of these (standard) ap-proximation notions, the interested reader is referred to the classic textbook of Vazirani [2003]. In the black-box model, we will deem an algorithm efficient if it computes an ε-approximate 3equilibrium using the (provably, qualitatively) smallest possible number of queries as a function of ε, as well as a polynomial number of arithmetic operations. 

Algorithms for the CCFPA. For the CCFPA, leveraging the analytic formulas from the work in economics, we are able to obtain the following results: - For the black-box model, in Section 3.1 we present an efficient algorithm which computes an 

ε-approximate equilibrium with number of queries polynomial in 1 /ε , as well as a polynomial number of arithmetic operations. We complement this result with an asymptotically matching lower bound on the number of queries required to compute an ε-approximate equilibrium, establishing that our algorithm is the best possible. - Via a careful inspection of our analysis in the black-box model above, it further follows that the same algorithm can be transformed into an FPTAS, for the case when the distribution is provided in the input implicitly via an arithmetic circuit, see Section 3.1.1. - Finally, in Section 3.2, we consider the setting where the (cdf of the) distribution is explicitly provided as an input to the algorithm, via a piecewise polynomial function (similarly to, e.g., [Filos-Ratsikas et al., 2023, 2024, 2025]). In this case, we present a different algorithm, which computes an exact equilibrium in polynomial time, hence strengthening the aforementioned FPTAS above, for this very natural special case. This result further implies that in this case there is an exact equilibrium with rational bids on rational input values. 

Algorithms for the CDFPA. As we mentioned above, unlike the case for the equilibria of the CCFPA, the literature in economics has not in general managed to provide analytic solutions for the equilibria of the CDFPA, with the exception of the aforementioned work of Chwe [1989], which only applies to the special case of uniform distributions with equidistant bids. Therefore, in order to come up with a general-purpose efficient algorithm for computing equilibria of the CDFPA (besides this special case), we would first need to provide a new, constructive proof of existence , which we would then have to transform into a computational method. This is precisely what we do in Section 4. By unfolding the details of Informal Theorem 1 for this setting, we obtain an algorithm which computes an ε-approximate equilibrium, and - in the black-box model, it performs a number of queries which is polynomial in log(1 /ε ) and log(1 /L ), where L is the Lipschitz parameter of the distribution, as well as a polynomial number of arithmetic operations, and - in the model where the distribution is given in the input as an arithmetic circuit, it runs in time polynomial in log(1 /ε ) and log(1 /L ) and the other parameters of the input. For the case of the explicit representation of the distribution via a piecewise polynomial function, the dependence of the Lipschitz parameter above vanishes, as it is “absorbed” in the description of the polynomial pieces. 

1.2 Related Work and Discussion 

The systematic study of equilibrium computation in first-price auctions was initiated by Filos-Ratsikas et al. [2023], where the CDFPA was formally introduced and studied. The main result of this work is the computational hardness (specifically PPAD-hardness and FIXP-hardness, see [Etessami and Yannakakis, 2010]) of computing equilibria of the auction for distributions that are not only different for every agent, but also subjective , i.e., different from the perspective 

4of each agent. Chen and Peng [2023] subsequently showed a similar PPAD-hardness result without the subjectivity assumption, but for an involved (trilateral) tie-breaking rule, rather than the standard uniform tie-breaking that is typically used in the literature of the auction, e.g., see [Vickrey, 1961; Riley and Samuelson, 1981; Milgrom and Weber, 1982; Athey, 2001; Krishna, 2009]. Filos-Ratsikas et al. [2024, 2025] showed corresponding NP-hardness results for a variant of the auction with discrete distributions, under the same subjectivity assumption, or for distributions that are arbitrarily correlated between bidders. In terms of positive results, Filos-Ratsikas et al. [2023] provided a polynomial-time algo-rithm for the case where both the number of bidders and the number of bids is fixed, which employs off-the-shelf results of Grigor’ev and Vorobjov [1988] for approximately solving systems of polynomial inequalities. Using a “bidding shrinkage lemma” due to Chen and Peng [2023], Filos-Ratsikas et al. [2024, 2025] observed that the same algorithm can be transformed into a PTAS for a fixed number of bidders, and any bidding space. Furthermore, the dependency on the number of bidders can be removed for symmetric auctions, yielding a PTAS that holds under no further assumptions. As we explained earlier, a PTAS is only efficient when the ap-proximation parameter ε is constant; in contrast, our algorithm in Section 4 achieves the best possible (inversely exponential) approximation for the same setting. To the best of our knowledge, the complexity of equilibrium computation in the CCFPA had not been studied prior to our work. This variant of the auction was studied only as a technical tool by Filos-Ratsikas et al. [2025], as part of their “bid densification” approach: in a quest to achieve positive results for the CDFPA, the authors consider how its equilibria would be approximated by equilibria of the CCFPA, for which analytic solutions are known (by the seminal work of Milgrom and Weber [1982]). Despite this connection, the authors did not show how to compute equilibria of the CCFPA in polynomial time. Furthermore, their resulting algorithm for the CDFPA, although displaying a graceful dependency on ε, crucially also depends on other, non-controllable parameters such as explicit bounds on the pdf of the distribution and the granularity of the bidding space. For our results in Section 4 we do not impose any such assumptions (other than a mild Lipschitz-continuity assumption). In a complementary direction, S ¸eref Ahunbay and Bichler [2025] study structural properties of equilibria in standard first-price auctions, showing uniqueness of Bayesian coarse correlated equilibria (and extending the analysis to all-pay auctions). There is also a growing line of work that employs tools from online learning and optimization to characterize (and in some cases compute) stable outcome notions in first-price auctions, particularly in repeated and non-stationary environments [Feng et al., 2021; Bichler et al., 2025; Wen et al., 2025; Hu et al., 2025].

Remark 1. For positive results like the ones presented in our work, restricting attention to symmetric, non-overbidding, monotone equilibria is without loss of generality, as it only makes the results stronger. 62.2 Distribution Representation 

Since F is a continuous distribution, we need to discuss how to represent it in a way that is suitable for computational purposes. We will consider three different representations, all of which are standard in the literature of related problems with continuous functions in their inputs, e.g., see [Deligkas et al., 2021; Hollender and Rubinstein, 2025; Filos-Ratsikas et al., 2023]. 

Black-box (Oracle) Model: In this model, we assume that the algorithm has oracle or query access to the distribution, i.e., the distribution will be accessed via queries that input a value 

x ∈ V = [0 , 1] and output the cdf F (x) of the distribution. The efficiency of an algorithm will then be measured by the (worst-case) number of such queries that it performs. Furthermore, for our positive results, we will make sure that the number of any additional arithmetic operations needed are polynomial (in the relevant parameters). Such a model is of particular interest for applications where the designer cannot have perfect knowledge of the distributional value prior, but can perform some kind of “market analysis” and “estimate” the bidder population quantiles. 

White-box Implicit Model: In this model, we assume that the algorithm has access to some “meaningful” computational device, e.g., a Turing machine or an appropriate arithmetic circuit (see, e.g., [Deligkas et al., 2021, Appendix B]). The algorithm will then access the cdf of the distribution for a given value x ∈ V = [0 , 1] via “feeding” x to this device and obtaining F (x). Since the input to the algorithm includes a description of this device rather than an abstract cdf oracle, this model is typically referred to as the white-box model. Concretely, we will assume that the input to the algorithm is a well-behaved arithmetic circuit x 7 → F (x), as defined in [Fearnley et al., 2021, Section 3.1.3]. These are circuits with gates performing the arithmetic operations {+, −, ×, max , min , > } and rational constants, with the additional restriction that, for any computational path that leads to an output, there is at most a logarithmic number of multiplication gates. This restriction is required to ensure that the circuit does not “internally” generate numbers of magnitude (inversely) doubly-exponential (in the size of the input and the description of the circuit), preventing its efficient evaluation. Furthermore, we will assume that the input to the problem also includes a Lipschitz parameter 

L, such that the distribution is L-Lipschitz. 

Explicit Model: In this model, we assume that the distribution can be represented in a suc-cinct form, and thus can be given directly as input to the algorithm. More precisely, following [Filos-Ratsikas et al., 2023, Sec. 6], we will assume that the cdf of the distribution is piece-wise polynomial, and hence it will be provided in the input in terms of the coefficients of the polynomial in each piece. 2.3 Continuous vs Discrete Bids 

So far, we have not imposed any conditions on the bidding space B. We will distinguish between auctions with continuous and discrete bidding spaces. In the former case, we will set the bidding space to be the entirety of the unit interval, that is, B = [0 , 1]. For the latter case, the bidding space is a finite subset of [0 , 1], which is given explicitly as part of the input. We will refer to these variants as the Continuous First-Price Auction with Continuous Bids (CCFPA) and the 

Continuous First-Price Auction with Discrete Bids (CDFPA) , respectively. 1

Note that, in the CDFPA, every (monotone) bidding strategy β : V → B is a piecewise-constant function. Given the discrete bidding space B = {b1, b 2, . . . , b m} ⊂ [0 , 1] 2, we can represent β by specifying jump points 0 = s0 ≤ s1 ≤ . . . ≤ sm = 1 such that β(v) = bj when 

v ∈ (sj−1, s j ]. Notice that the ordering between the jump points is not strict, meaning that some of them might coincide resulting in certain bids in B not being used by β. This will be the canonical representation of strategies, and hence of equilibria, throughout the paper; see Figure 1 for a pictorial view. We also note that previous works on the problem [Filos-Ratsikas et al., 2023; Chen and Peng, 2023; Filos-Ratsikas et al., 2025] use the same representation for the output of the auction.

# 4 Discrete Bidding Space (CDFPA) 

In this section we study the CDFPA, where the bidding space B is a finite subset of [0 , 1]. Namely, the bidding space is B = {b1, . . . , b m} with 0 = b1 < b 2 < · · · < b m < 1. Our main theorem of the section is the following: 

Theorem 4.1. An ε-BNE of a CDFPA can be computed in the black box model using poly( n, m, log L, log(1 /ε ), log(1 /α )) operations and oracle calls to the L-Lipschitz cdf F of its value distribution, where α := min i∈[m](bi+1 − bi) is the minimum distance between consecutive bids. 8

Furthermore, in the white box model, the algorithm runs in polynomial time in the size of the input. 

Before we provide the proof of the theorem, we offer some useful discussion. As we mentioned in the introduction, in the context of equilibrium computation, the CDFPA is inherently more challenging than the CCFPA. This is because, unlike for the equilibria of the CCFPA, the literature in economics has not in general managed to provide analytic solutions for the equilibria of the CDFPA. An exception to this is the work of Chwe [1989], which provides a method to obtain an equilibrium under two assumptions: (i) the distribution being uniform, and (ii) the bids being equidistant, i.e., B = {0, 1

> q

, 1} for some integer q.Our algorithm solves the problem for the general setting, without any of these two assump-tions. In order to achieve this, we make the following crucial observation: if we know the utility achieved by the bidders at equilibrium at the highest point in the value space, i.e., at v = 1, then we can efficiently compute the equilibrium, i.e., the set of jump points that describe the equilibrium bidding strategy. This observation already yields the result of Chwe [1989] as a special case. Indeed, we can reinterpret Chwe’s method as follows. It turns out that for the uniform distribution, the utility at equilibrium at point v = 1 is always 1 /n , where n is the number of bidders. As a result, the crucial observation yields a method for efficiently comput-ing the equilibrium when the distribution is uniform. Note that this method works even for non-equidistant bids, so it already allows us to remove assumption (ii). In order to remove assumption (i), namely the uniformity assumption, we make the following additional observation. When we run our method for obtaining an equilibrium from a utility value U at v = 1 that does not correspond to the utility value at an equilibrium, it still outputs a set of jump points. Of course, these jump points do not correspond to an equilibrium in that case, but we can still extract useful information from them. We show that our method for computing a set of jump points s = ( s0, . . . , s m) from a utility value has the following two crucial properties: (a) on input U = 0, the method outputs s with s0 = 0, and (b) on input U = 1, it outputs s with s0 > 0. This suggests the following approach: perform binary search on the value 

U in order to find a value U ∗ where the output s is such that s0 is barely above 0. Indeed, we are able to show that if we can find such a value U ∗ then we obtain an approximate equilibrium. The most challenging part of the analysis is showing that our method is continuous, in the sense that the jump points that it outputs depend continuously on the input value U . # 5 Conclusion and Future Work 

Our work provides the first efficient algorithms for computing equilibria in symmetric first-price auctions, with efficiency measured either by the number of queries to the distribution oracle (in the black-box model) or by running time (in the white-box model). The existence of these algorithms essentially settles the computational complexity of the problem for the case of i.i.d. private values, closing an important gap in the literature. This allows future work to focus on 

asymmetric auctions, in particular the case of bidders with independent private values (IPV), for which the computational complexity of equilibrium computation is still open. Within the realm of symmetric auctions, the only (minor) gap left by our results in this paper is the case of inverse-exponential approximations in the implicit white-box model for the CCFPA, for which our algorithm is an FPTAS; in contrast, in the explicit-input model our corresponding algorithm computes an exact equilibrium in polynomial time. Additionally, our lower bound in Section 3.1 for the black-box model suggests that any white-box algorithm for ε-approximate equilibria that runs in time polynomial in log(1 /ε ) would need to “open the box” provided in the input. We suspect that the existence of such an algorithm would be effectively precluded by an associated PPAD-hardness result. Given the absence of PPAD-hardness results for the more general IPV case, however, obtaining a corresponding result for the symmetric auction setting seems quite challenging. Finally, one could aim to generalize our algorithms to symmetric settings beyond auctions with i.i.d. private values. In particular, it would be meaningful to study the case of symmetric affiliated private values of Milgrom and Weber [1982]; for this case, the best known positive result is a PTAS due to Filos-Ratsikas et al.
